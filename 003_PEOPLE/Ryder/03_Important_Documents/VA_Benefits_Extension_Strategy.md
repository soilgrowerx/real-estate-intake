# VA Education Benefits Extension Strategy (Ryder)
**Date:** 2026-03-28
**Context:** Maximizing education benefits beyond the standard 36-month limitation on the Certificate of Eligibility (CoE) for Chapter 35 (DEA).

## The Core Limitation
Under the Colmery Act (Forever GI Bill), dependents whose eligibility for Chapter 35 (DEA) began on or after August 1, 2018, are capped at **36 months** of entitlement (down from the historical 45 months). 

*Note: The veteran (George) is utilizing his own Chapter 33 (Post-9/11 GI Bill) for a Master's degree, so Transferred Chapter 33 is not part of Ryder's stack.*

## The Primary Play: State Tuition Waiver Multipliers
Since Transferred Chapter 33 is not in play, the primary strategy to stretch Ryder's benefits is leveraging **State-Level 100% Disabled Veteran Tuition Waivers** simultaneously with **Chapter 35 (DEA)**.

Chapter 35 pays a flat monthly stipend directly to the student (currently ~$1,488/month for full-time enrollment), but it **does not pay tuition to the school**. 

However, multiple states offer 100% tuition waivers for dependents of 100% Permanent & Total disabled veterans at their public state universities. This allows Ryder to zero out the tuition bill while pocketing the entire Chapter 35 stipend for living expenses.

### 1. Texas (The Hazelwood Act)
If the veteran entered service in Texas, Ryder is eligible for the Hazelwood Act, providing up to **150 credit hours of tuition exemption** at Texas public colleges and universities.

### 2. Washington State
Under RCW 28B.15.621, the Washington State Legislature mandates that state community colleges and universities **waive all undergraduate tuition and fees** for eligible dependents of 100% disabled veterans. This covers up to 200 quarter credits (or equivalent semester credits).

### 3. Oregon State
Under ORS 350.285, Oregon provides a **Veterans' Dependent Tuition Waiver** for dependents of 100% service-connected disabled veterans. It covers tuition at Oregon public universities. (Note: The child must be aged 31 or younger at the time of application).

### 4. California (CalVet Fee Waiver)
The **CalVet College Fee Waiver for Veteran Dependents** waives mandatory system-wide tuition and fees at any State of California Community College, California State University (CSU), or University of California (UC) campus.

### Critical Residency Constraints
State waivers are incredibly powerful but carry strict residency rules:
*   **Texas (Hazlewood):** The veteran must have entered the service in Texas, declared Texas as their Home of Record at entry, or been a Texas resident prior to entering service. If the veteran does not meet this, the dependent does not qualify.
*   **Washington / Oregon / California:** Unlike Texas, these states generally do *not* require the veteran to have entered service from their state. However, the **student (Ryder)** must be a legal resident of the state for tuition purposes. This typically requires moving to the state and establishing physical domicile for **12 continuous months** prior to enrollment. 

### The Execution Sequence
1. **Tuition:** Apply the state waiver (Hazelwood in TX, or the respective waivers in WA/OR/CA) to completely wipe out the tuition bill at a public state university.
2. **Living Expenses:** Run Chapter 35 (DEA) simultaneously. Because tuition is covered by the state, Ryder pockets the entire ~$1,488/month VA stipend to cover rent, food, and books.
3. **Pacing:** Since Chapter 35 is strictly capped at 36 months, Ryder must optimize his class loads. Taking heavier credit loads during fall/spring and avoiding unnecessary summer disbursements will stretch the 36 months of stipends across a full 4-year degree (which is typically exactly 36 months of actual in-class time).

## The Loophole: The 81-Month Rule (For Future Reference)
Normally, the VA enforces a "Rule of 48," capping beneficiaries who use multiple VA programs at 48 months total. However, a statutory amendment (P.L. 112-154) created an exception for Chapter 35, allowing an **81-month aggregate cap** if combined with another qualifying VA program. While Transferred Chapter 33 isn't available, if Ryder ever qualifies for another distinct VA educational benefit (e.g., Fry Scholarship, though hopefully not applicable, or his own GI Bill if he serves), he can stack them up to 81 months.

## Hardship Extensions
If Ryder approaches his delimiting date (the age limit to use the benefits, typically his 31st birthday for Chapter 35) and was prevented from finishing school due to a severe illness, disability, or unavoidable life event, the VA can grant an extension to the *timeframe*. However, this does not grant *additional months* of funding—it only gives him more time to use the 36 months he already has.

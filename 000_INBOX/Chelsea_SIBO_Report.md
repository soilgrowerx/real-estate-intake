## 1. Clinical Overview: What is SIBO?
Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO) occurs when bacteria that normally live in the colon migrate upward and overgrow in the small intestine. This results in premature fermentation of carbohydrates, producing excess hydrogen and/or methane gas.

### Key Symptoms in Chelsea’s Context:
*   **Upper Stomach Pain & Bloating:** Classic signs of gas trapped where it doesn't belong.
*   **Post-Prandial Distension:** Bloating immediately after eating carbohydrates.
*   **Systemic Mimicry:** SIBO often presents with fatigue, brain fog, and joint aches, potentially mimicking or amplifying autoimmune/connective tissue disorders.

---

## 2. The SIBO-MCAS "Bidirectional Loop"
The research confirms a highly destructive biological loop between SIBO and Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS):

1.  **Bacterial Trigger:** Overgrown bacteria manufacture histamine and excrete toxic waste (Lipopolysaccharides/LPS). This "toxic sludge" causes local mast cells in the gut to degranulate, releasing inflammatory cytokines and histamine into the bloodstream.
2.  **Mast Cell Paralysis:** Mast cell mediators can physically alter gut motility. They "paralyze" the **Migrating Motor Complex (MMC)**—the internal "street sweeper" that pushes bacteria down into the colon. 
3.  **The Result:** Bacteria stay trapped because the gut isn't moving; the trapped bacteria keep the mast cells angry. You must treat both to break the cycle.

---

## 3. Explaining Chelsea’s Recent Labs & Progression
The SIBO diagnosis provides a unifying mechanism for Chelsea’s recent abnormal blood markers:

*   **Iron Handling (Plummeting Iron vs. Normal Ferritin):** Bacteria in the small intestine are "thieves." They consume dietary iron before it can be absorbed. This explains why circulating iron is dropping while stored ferritin remains normal.
*   **Inflammatory Markers (High CRP & C4):** Bacterial LPS leaks through the gut lining ("leaky gut"), triggering the liver to pump out C-Reactive Protein (CRP) and Complement C4 to fight the perceived contamination.
*   **Immune Response (High WBC & Neutrophils):** The body senses the bacterial waste leakage and simulates a "low-grade sepsis," keeping the bone marrow in a state of maximum alert (churning out neutrophils).

## 4. The Tirzepatide (GLP-1) Connection
GLP-1 receptor agonists like **Tirzepatide** markedly slow gastric emptying and small-intestinal motility. 
*   **Motility Inhibition:** By slowing the MMC, these drugs can create "ideal conditions" for SIBO to develop. 
*   **Symptom Flare:** Chelsea’s flare upon stopping the medication fits a pattern where the overgrowth, once established, persists because the underlying gut circuitry and mast cell loop remain disturbed.

## 5. Management & Treatment Strategy

### A. Diagnosis (Immediate Next Step)
*   **Breath Testing:** A hydrogen-methane breath test using **glucose** (more specific) or **lactulose** (reaches further in the small bowel) is the standard non-invasive test.
*   **Timing:** Request this test *before* the April 9 follow-up to have hard data.

### B. Antibiotic Intervention
*   **Rifaximin:** A highly specific antibiotic that stays entirely within the digestive tract (not absorbed into the blood). It "carpet bombs" the bacteria in the small intestine.
*   **Dosage:** Typically a 10–14 day course. 
*   **Relapse Prevention:** Antibiotics alone often fail if motility isn't addressed.

### C. The "Prokinetic" Sweep
*   Following antibiotics, a **prokinetic medication** (e.g., low-dose Erythromycin, Prucalopride, or MotilPro) is often used to force the stomach muscles to "sweep" the tract clean, preventing re-colonization.

### D. The Proteostasis Protocol (TUDCA & Stack)
*   **TUDCA:** While helpful for liver/bile flow and cellular stress (ER stress), it is **not** a primary treatment for SIBO. It should be framed as "terrain-shaping" support.
*   **Astilbin/Laxogenin:** These are upstream immunometabolic supports. It may be wise to start these *after* a SIBO course to clearly distinguish their benefits.

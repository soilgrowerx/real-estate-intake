# Spore: MCAS Metabolic Blockade & Fat Storage (Chelsea Profile)

In the context of MCAS, the inability to lose fat—specifically in the midsection and upper arms—despite effort is a well-documented physiological consequence of chronic inflammatory cycles and Post-Exertional Malaise (PEM).

## 1. The Cortisol Trap (Midsection Fat)
Every time a stressor (like an ab workout) triggers PEM and a mast cell flare, the body adrenal glands pump out cortisol to suppress the inflammatory response. Chronic MCAS flares mean chronically elevated cortisol, which specifically dictates where the body stores fat, preferentially depositing it deep in the abdomen (visceral fat). This fat is highly resistant to traditional "calories in, calories out" diets.

## 2. Cytokine-Induced Insulin Resistance
Mast cell degranulation releases TNF-alpha and IL-6, which directly interfere with insulin signaling at the cellular level. This creates localized or systemic insulin resistance, preventing the body from efficiently utilizing glucose for energy and instead converting it into fat.

## 3. Inflamed Adipose Tissue (Sick Fat)
Adipose (fat) tissue contains its own Adipose Tissue Mast Cells (ATMCs). In MCAS, these cells become chronically inflamed, leading to fibrotic (stiff) fat tissue that strictly resists lipolysis (breaking down fat for energy).

## 4. The Lymphatic & Lipedema Connection (Upper Arms)
Stubborn fat in the upper arms and midsection is a clinical hallmark of disrupted lymphatic systems or Lipedema, often comorbid with MCAS. Histamine increases blood vessel permeability, overwhelming the lymphatic system and causing "heavy," painful fat accumulation that is physically immune to standard diet and exercise.

## In Short:
If exercise triggers a mast cell flare, the body enters a "survival and storage" mode. Cortisol, histamine, and cytokines lock fat into the midsection and arms, making fat-loss biologically impossible until the mast cell activation is stabilized.

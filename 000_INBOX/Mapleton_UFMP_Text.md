Vision & Mission

Vision Statement

The vision of the Urban Forest Management Plan (UFMP) is to preserve and enhance Mapleton’s small-town character and quality of life by harnessing the cultural and economic benefits of an extensive and resilient community forest. 

Mission Statement

The UFMP aims to establish foundational practices essential for achieving a sustainable and safe community forest in Mapleton, including monitoring and maintenance, risk management, and community involvement, to be enacted over the course of the next five years. 





Introduction 



Mapleton is a small, rural town in upstate New York. Currently Mapleton has a population of 8,029 and is expecting growth in the coming years. Mapleton’s trees are managed by the city’s Parks Committee, which also manages Mapleton’s eleven public parks. 

As Mapleton sees population growth and a shift in its economic structure, the city is looking to formalize and expand its management of the community’s trees. 

Safety and resilience are the guiding principles for this management plan. Recent events–including hazardous tree failure, extensive storm damage to the community forest, and a minor pest threat–demonstrate the need for Mapleton to transition into managing its forest proactively rather than reactively. In the long-term, this management strategy will increase safety and maximize the benefits Mapleton citizens receive from the community forest. 

The contents of this plan represent a significant change from the previous forest management strategy, with many components being established for the first time in Mapleton. As such, the plan takes a phased approach, distributing foundational work over a five-year period and structured by five central goals: 

Assess and monitor the status of the community forest to make data-driven decisions about its preservation and development

Utilize best management practices to ensure long-term sustainability in the community forest

Evaluate, create, and enforce policies that will integrate the community forest into urban development

Inspire and inform our community to engage in actions that support the community forest

Commit funds and identify and leverage new financial resources to sustain long-term management of the urban forest.

An additional guiding goal is to achieve Tree City USA (TCUSA) status, which requires a municipality to have (1) a tree board, (2) a public tree ordinance, (3) an annual expenditure of $2 per capita on tree care, and (4) an Arbor Day celebration. Mapleton’s Park Committee is already well situated to fulfill TCUSA’s tree board requirement, and an Arbor Day celebration is already a Mapleton annual tradition. Components throughout the plan will expand upon these existing structures and lay the groundwork for meeting the second and third requirements.

In 1997, Mapleton completed an assessment of the community’s street and park tree. While data is referenced throughout the document, it is important not to conflate this 1997 depiction of the forest with the status of Mapleton’s community forest today–which has certainly changed in the nearly thirty-years since the assessment was last updated.

 Nevertheless, the assessment’s data, particularly the street tree inventory, can be utilized to establish a foundational understanding of Mapleton’s forest and provide initial direction for goals and priorities. Little stock can be put into what the assessment reports on any given tree, but the broadscale patterns that are revealed (and can be inferred upon) are meaningful for the purposes of this plan. 



Benefits of the Community Forest

Mapleton’s forest is a substantial resource to everyone in the community, transcending purely aesthetic value and offering a host of environmental, economic, and social benefits that often go unnoticed or underappreciated. It is imperative that the services provided by the community forest are well-understood by city leaders and community stakeholders so that this resource is considered integral to city functioning and invested in accordingly. Establishing community forest management as a city priority is particularly important now as the city anticipates population growth and associated increase development in addition to facing other, natural threats to the existing canopy.

Plans, Policies, Regulations

Public Safety and Tree Risk Management Program Public safety requires us to stop reacting to fallen branches and start managing risk proactively. The 1997 inventory identifies massive, declining trees, specifically the 36 to 40 inch diameter Sugar Maples located on 10th Avenue North. Waiting for these heavy canopy trees to fail is an unacceptable liability. We will adopt a zero tolerance policy for priority hazard removals. Furthermore, all municipal tree work must be overseen by an ISA Certified Arborist and strictly adhere to ANSI A300 pruning standards and ANSI Z133 safety regulations, including all applicable New York State standards.

Tree Protection Ordinances (TPOs) Current ordinances fail because they only protect the trunk of the tree. We must shift our legal protection to the underground environment. The new code defines the Critical Root Zone (CRZ) as a circular area with a radius of one foot for every inch of trunk diameter at breast height. Construction and grading are strictly prohibited within the inner half of this zone.

Additionally, planting trees in confined concrete cutouts guarantees premature death. The new ordinance will mandate a minimum of 1,000 cubic feet of uncompacted loam soil for any new large canopy tree planted in the right of way. If a developer requires a variance to remove a protected tree, the mitigation fee is $150 per caliper inch, payable directly to the city Tree Fund.

Budget and Level of Service Our desired Level of Service dictates our budget per capita and budget per tree. Upgrading to the 1,000 cubic foot soil volume standard requires higher upfront capital costs. However, it drastically reduces the long term budget drain of stump grinding and replacing dead street trees every ten years. Treating trees as appreciating capital assets allows us to accurately track our Ecological ROI.



Sustainability, Diversity, and Species Selection Mapleton faces a severe biological threat. Trees from the Acer (Maple) genus currently make up 61.7 percent of our entire street tree population. We will implement the industry standard 10-20-30 rule. Going forward, no single species will exceed 10 percent of the population, no genus will exceed 20 percent, and no family will exceed 30 percent. All new planting stock introduced to the city must conform to ANSI Z60.1 standards.

Development Mitigation and Nursery Standards

We will update the municipal code to reflect modern preservation standards. All new planting stock must conform strictly to ANSI Z60.1 standards to ensure proper root ball sizing and structural integrity. For development mitigation, we establish a strict replacement ratio. Hardwood trees between 6 and 8 caliper inches retained on site will count toward mitigation at a 0.5 to 1 ratio. Protected trees (8 to 20 inches) preserved within 15 feet of parking areas yield a two tree credit. This incentivizes developers to design around our existing biological assets rather than clear cutting and paying nominal fines.

Resource Management: Pest and Storm To address local pest vulnerabilities, specifically the Plum Borer, we will institute a five year moratorium on planting any ornamental cherry species (Prunus) on public property. Community 

Mapleton is fortunate to have a tight-knit and engaged community. Ultimately, this plan is in in service of this community and will be implemented with continual community member prioritization and feedback. 

There are several viable methods for engaging the citizenry of Mapleton. Community events are well attended, offering an excellent opportunity to pursue educational and/or feedback campaigns, particularly when such events take place at Veterans Park. More formal methods of engagement will also be important as this management plan is adopted and revised. The city will utilize periodic surveys and/or formal gatherings to understand community needs and perspectives on ongoing and planned tree work. The input gathered through these avenues should have a direct influence over future policy and management goals. Moreover, the public–especially those who provided feedback–must be kept up to date on community forestry developments, especially regarding the implementation of community input. This can be achieved through email newsletter dissemination and updates on the city website and social media. 

More Than Maples

The Mapleton community forest is weakened by its lack of species diversity. The dominance of trees in the Acer genus–maple trees–makes the forest vulnerable to a sudden and significant loss should a pathogen or pest target this group of trees. As discussed [elsewhere], it is imperative that Mapleton address this lack of diversity through strategic removal of maples and planting of a new, diverse set of trees. 

The cultural value of maple trees in Mapleton is high. Though this process of removal and planting will be approached in a gradual, phased manner, it is important to recognize that removing maple trees may precipitate varying degrees of negative emotions within the Mapleton community. As such, it is essential that the city (1) provides ample information to the broader community that makes clear what is being done and why and (2) engage in meaningful conversations with citizens that will shape how the removal/planting process takes shape. 

To address the first point, a “More Than Maples” education campaign is recommended. Educational materials should be dispersed via email newsletters, social media, and community event tabling that explain the risks of a maple monoculture and the benefits of a diverse community forest. To the extent possible, this general education campaign should be underway before any tree removal occurs. In instances where a significant neighborhood tree is slated for removal, it is recommended that neighborhood-specific mailers be sent out notifying residents and explaining the removal. 

Additionally, this education campaign should incorporate a re-branding of Mapleton’s traditional Arbor Day celebration, which typically includes schoolchildren planting maple trees around the city. To avoid further planting of maples, the city should expand this celebration to include planting a diverse set of species. 

Community conversations (item two, above) should incorporate both specific feedback from the public as well as more open-ended discussion. It will be important for the city to gain specific input on what types of trees the community would like to see replace maples, where these trees should be placed, and what should be done with the raw material generated by maple removal (e.g., public art). It may be appropriate to use public input to determine which maple trees will be removed, though this will only be the case if additional maple removal is needed after high-risk trees are eliminated.

Implementation

The success of an urban forestry management plan (UFMP) depends on a number of factors (Sustainable Forestry Initiative, 2024; Vibrant Cities Lab, 2024; Wirtz et al., 2021) . These include:

Creating an inclusive coalition to make data-driven decisions and implement the plan with clear goals and objectives 

Implementing a partnership with regional agencies to pool resources and align policies to achieve local and regional goals and objectives

Engaging in best management practices to create a sustainable community forest that is resilient to ongoing and future challenges

This UFMP has a timeline of 5 years. Toward the end of this period, the UFMP will be refreshed and updated for the next 5-year cycle.

Tree Board  

In the City of Mapleton, the Parks Committee has been responsible for the city’s parks and street trees and serves as a Tree Board to qualify for that requirement for Mapleton to become a Tree City USA member. Meetings for the Tree Board should be held regularly and be open to the public.

To cover the new responsibilities involved in managing the UFMP, it will be necessary to re-define roles for Tree Board members. The Parks Committee personnel have been engaged in a variety of tasks and have the skills to accomplish the new responsibilities. 

Listed below are titles and job descriptions for City Liaison and Parks Committee  members  under the new Tree Board configuration. There is significant connection and overlap between these various positions to accomplish the goals and objectives of the UFMP. 

City Liaison –Coordinate with Mayor’s office for urban planning and funding. Connect Tree Board members with other agencies (e.g., utilities, streets) to integrate community forest health into local infrastructure projects.

Data Analyst -- Conduct ongoing tree inventories of parks and street trees to evaluate and monitor forest health. Collate tree data (e.g., species, health, size) with geographic information (e.g. location, community demographics) to determine prioritized actions for forest management.

Operations Coordinator – Implement best management practices to manage the community forest. Oversee technical details of tree maintenance and protection to ensure the community forest remains healthy and sustainable for future generations of residents.

Policy Manager – Evaluate current ordinances for alignment to UFMP objectives and revise or draft new policies as necessary. Coordinate with local and regional agencies to ensure policies are enforced and aligned with goals and objectives.

Outreach Specialist – Lead educational initiatives to inform public about the benefits of the community forest and upcoming activities. Solicit input from the community to ensure management actions reflect resident values.

Development Officer – Secure sustainable, adequate funding for long-term tree planting and ongoing maintenance. Identify and pursue financial opportunities to leverage novel resources to supplement local funding.

Monitoring Plan

Metrics

Digital Twin and Ecosystem MetricsMapleton must abandon static, decade old paper inventories. We will implement a continuous, mobile accessible Digital Twin monitoring system. Volunteers and city staff will log condition changes, diameter at breast height (DBH) growth, and hazard alerts directly from the field. This protocol transforms our inventory into a living substrate. We will bypass failing legacy systems and integrate this field data directly into the Arboracle platform to quantify our biological equity. Specifically, we will track carbon sequestration, rainfall interception, and the social cost of carbon to provide the City Council with exact dollar values for our ecosystem services.

Tree Inventory ModernizationA paper inventory from 1997 is a dead document. Mapleton will modernize its approach by implementing a GIS-based, mobile accessible digital twin system, operating on logic of established digital platforms. Parks Committee volunteers and city staff will use mobile devices to update tree conditions, relative DBH, and age structure directly from the field. This creates a living database and tracks our work orders without the massive expense of hiring consultants for periodic inventory updates.

Ecosystem Services and Database MigrationHistorically, municipal forestry relied on iTree reporting to quantify ecosystem services. However, recent changes in USDA Forest Service policy have indefinitely halted traditional funding for the iTree platform, immediately threatening its future functionality and maintenance. Mapleton cannot build a five-year management plan on collapsing digital infrastructure. Therefore, our Digital Twin inventory will migrate away from iTree dependency. We will utilize the Arboracle platform to track exact dollar values for stormwater mitigation, energy savings, and carbon sequestration. Because Arboracle currently provides a 50% discount for government entities, this transition allows us to secure stable, modernized inventory functionality while actively reducing our municipal software overhead.

Public Opinion and Education We need public support to shift away from our historical reliance on maples. We will launch an education campaign explaining the "more than maples" concept. Residents need to understand the real tree dis-services associated with monocultures, such as the catastrophic canopy loss we would experience if a genus specific pest arrived.

Ecological ROI on Suspended PavementsMandating 1,000 cubic feet of soil volume requires a shift in capital allocation. Standard concrete tree pits are cheap upfront but biologically degenerative. A street tree planted in a standard pit yields only $28,250 in ecosystem benefits over its stunted lifespan. Conversely, utilizing suspended pavement systems allows us to provide uncompacted loam beneath load bearing sidewalks. While the initial installation cost is higher, the cost per cubic yard of usable soil is actually nearly half that of traditional structural gravel mixes ($288 per cubic yard versus $540). A tree in a suspended pavement system yields over $551,000 in benefits over a 200 year horizon. This is a high yield municipal investment, not a sunk cost.

Appendix E: Management Plans

Pests: American Plum Borer MitigationThe 1997 inventory identifies a high density of Yoshino Cherries on 1st Avenue South. This creates a trophic cascade risk regarding the American Plum Borer (Euzophera semifuneralis), a cambium feeding pyralid moth that aggressively attacks stone fruits and causes severe canopy decline. Mapleton will adopt a strict monitoring protocol. We will deploy pheromone traps in early spring to track flight activity. If larvae are present, operations crews will utilize targeted trunk applications to protect the critical graft unions. Simultaneously, the city will enact a five-year moratorium on planting new Prunus species on municipal property to break the local pest reproduction cycle.

Storm and Construction Risk ManagementStorm response drains municipal budgets rapidly. We will transition to proactive risk abatement by targeting the immediate removal of declining, large stature hazard trees identified in our forensic audit. Furthermore, we must protect our existing canopy from mechanical damage during municipal development. All construction projects near public trees must comply strictly with ANSI A300 Part 5 standards. This requires establishing Critical Root Zones (CRZ) around all heritage trees and prohibiting grading, soil compaction, or equipment storage within these perimeters.

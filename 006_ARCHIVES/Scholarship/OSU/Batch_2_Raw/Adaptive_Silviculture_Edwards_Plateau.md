## Adaptive Silviculture: Securing Carbon Permanence and Water Resilience in Edwards Plateau Woodlands

**Applicant Contact:** George Steward, College of Forestry, Oregon State
University, stewarge@oregonstate.edu

### Brief Summary of the Problem

The Central Texas Edwards Plateau isn\'t just a place; it is a fight for
the lifeblood of the region\'s woodlands. We are seeing systemic
collapse rooted in more than simple dry spells, but in a cumulative
pressure of hot drought, where chronic water deficit strongly correlates
with high tree mortality (Giebink et al., 2024). This instability leaves
Live Oak and Red Oak communities dangerously exposed.

This vulnerability creates a compounding, dangerous conflict. Fire
suppression and the dense encroachment of Ashe Juniper are building
volatile fuel loads that push the Wildland Urban Interface (WUI) toward
catastrophic wildfire risk.^1^ We face a paradox here: traditional
management aimed at maximizing water yield for the Edwards Aquifer often
risks ecological failure, sacrificing a valuable carbon sink and
inducing soil erosion (Polley et al., 2009). An unmanaged forest is
primed for irreversible carbon loss, or reversal, due to mega-fires. Our
strategy must align water, wildfire safety, and long-term carbon health.

### The Proposed Solution

Our goal is simple: enhance the long-term resilience of the Edwards
Plateau Juniper-Oak Woodland to both hot drought and wildfire risk
through adaptive silviculture. We shift the focus from maximizing simple
carbon stock to prioritizing permanence. The solution is strategic:
making a controlled, short-term carbon investment now (like a burn) to
buy down the long-term, massive reversal risk from catastrophic
mega-fire (Giebink et al., 2022). This outcome, which mitigates
long-term climate risk through immediate, measurable implementation,
meets the USDA Forest Service IRA NOFO mandate.

### Three Key Management Strategies/Practices

1.  Selective Mechanical Thinning: Targeted reduction of juniper density
    to cut competition for soil moisture, boosting the long-term drought
    tolerance of residual hardwoods.

2.  Prescribed Fire Reintroduction: Applying controlled, low-to-moderate
    severity burns to manage understory fuel loads, actively mitigating
    the risk of high-severity crown fire.

3.  Soil Stabilization and Follow-up Management: Utilizing native grass
    seeding and soil cover post-treatment to minimize erosion and
    maximize water infiltration into the Edwards Aquifer recharge zone.

### Broader Impacts

This work is centered on securing the interconnected systems of the
region. It safeguards critical regional water security by enhancing soil
infiltration over the Edwards Aquifer recharge zone. It protects our
communities by strategically reducing the WUI wildfire hazard, lowering
the increasing suppression cost burden. We cannot achieve true
resilience without equity. Our cost-share strategy must actively empower
Underserved Landowners (ULOs), leveraging relationships with Texas A&M
AgriLife and the Forest Service to overcome financial barriers and
ensure all stewards have access to the necessary resources to restore
this important landscape.

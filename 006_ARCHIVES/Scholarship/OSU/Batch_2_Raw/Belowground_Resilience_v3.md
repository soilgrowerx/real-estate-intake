**Cover Page**

**Project Title:** Belowground Resilience: A Practitioner's Guide to
Soil Biome Invigoration for Drought Tolerance

**Applicant:** George Steward, College of Forestry, Oregon State
University (stewarge@oregonstate.edu)

**Funding Opportunity:** USDA Forest Service Inflation Reduction Act
(IRA) Forest Landowner Support (USDA-FS-2024-IRA-FLS-03)

Abstract:

This project addresses the critical failure of urban and peri-urban
forests in Central Texas due to \"hotter droughts\"---a novel climatic
phenomenon combining extreme heat with water scarcity. Oaks (Quercus
spp.) are uniquely vulnerable to the resulting cumulative moisture
stress, leading to a \"mortality lag\" of 1-3 years. Current management
is reactive and fragmented. This project synthesizes academic research
into a synergistic, evidence-based Practitioner's Guide for arborists
and land managers. The goal is to develop a sequenced, fundable protocol
that transitions urban forestry from symptom-treatment to a proactive,
systems-level approach focused on building soil biome resilience. This
guide directly supports USDA climate adaptation goals by mitigating the
urban heat island effect, protecting carbon stocks, and providing
municipalities with a practical tool to manage wildfire risks in the
Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI).

**Proposal Narrative**

**1. Introduction: The Mechanism of Failure**

Managed forests globally are confronting a novel and distinct class of
extreme events. We have shifted from historical precipitation-driven
droughts to \"hotter droughts,\" in which record heat amplifies
atmospheric water demand (Vapor Pressure Deficit, or VPD). This
phenomenon creates a \"hotter-drought fingerprint\" that is driving
non-linear increases in tree mortality worldwide (Hammond et al., 2022).

In the Central Texas \"living laboratory,\" this crisis manifests as a
\"mortality lag.\" Trees often survive the acute heat event but succumb
1 to 3 years later. This delay indicates a catastrophic, invisible
failure occurring belowground. Recent modeling confirms that while many
species suffer from acute heat, dominant oaks (*Quercus* spp.) are
uniquely vulnerable to \"cumulative long-term moisture stress\" (Lawal
et al., 2024).

The mechanism of this failure is the collapse of the **soil biome**.
Urban soils, degraded by compaction and carbon loss, lack the hydraulic
capacitance to buffer trees against extreme VPD. As trees close stomata
to survive, they starve their fungal symbionts of carbon, causing the
mycorrhizal network to collapse exactly when the tree needs it most
(Adams et al., 2022). This decoupling of the soil-plant-atmosphere
continuum is the primary driver of mortality.

**2. Program Goals**

To address this systemic failure, this project pursues three primary
goals aligned with USDA priorities for forest resilience and
landscape-scale conservation:

-   **Goal 1: Synthesize Knowledge.** To bridge the gap between academic
    science (soil physics, microbiology) and field practice by
    synthesizing complex data into a single, accessible, and actionable
    *Practitioner\'s Guide*.

-   **Goal 2: Establish a Proactive Protocol.** To shift the
    arboricultural industry from a reactive, piecemeal treatment model
    to a proactive, sequenced protocol that builds holistic, long-term
    resilience from the soil up.

-   **Goal 3: Create an Adaptable Model.** To develop a practical,
    fundable, and scalable framework that municipalities and landowners
    can adopt as a core climate adaptation strategy.

**3. Context**

This project was born from front-line experience. As a practicing
arborist in Central Texas, I witness the daily failure of urban trees
under heat domes and flash droughts. However, this is not a local
problem; fir die-offs in the Pacific Northwest and oak decline globally
are variations of the same system failure.

The core challenge is not a lack of tools, but a lack of a *system*.
Practitioners have access to air spades, biochar, and microbial
inoculants, but they deploy them in an unsequenced, piecemeal manner.
The knowledge gap is the absence of a synergistic framework that
demonstrates *how* to stack these interventions for exponential
benefit---such as \"pre-charging\" biochar with fungi to create a
\"microbial reef\" (Lehmann & Joseph, 2015).

**4. Conceptual Framework**

The proposed intervention relies on a conceptual model identifying a
\"Critical Failure Cascade\" (see Figure 1). The project's **6-Pillar
Protocol** is designed to intervene at specific leverage points to break
this cascade.

Figure 1 Description: The Critical Failure Cascade

The conceptual model synthesized from physiological literature (e.g.,
Adams et al., 2022; Lawal et al., 2024) illustrates the progression of
mortality:

1.  **Environmental Drivers:** The cascade begins with the convergence
    of **Hotter Drought** (Heat + Low Water) and **Urban Soils**
    (Compaction + Low Carbon).

2.  **Belowground Failure:** This convergence triggers **Soil Biome
    Collapse**, resulting in two immediate impacts: **Mycorrhizal
    Network Failure** (the loss of the tree's \"extended root system\")
    and a critical **Loss of Water Holding Capacity**.

3.  **Specific Vulnerability:** This collapse creates the specific
    vulnerability identified by Lawal et al. (2024): **Cumulative
    Long-Term Moisture Stress**.

4.  **Outcome:** Without buffering capacity, the tree crosses a
    physiological threshold, leading to **Oak Decline & Mortality**.

The 6-Pillar Protocol intervenes to disrupt this chain. **Phase 1
(Remediation)** breaks the physical barrier; **Phase 2 (Activation)**
restores the biological engine; and **Phase 3 (Protection)** buffers the
system against future stress. (*Note: Conceptual models visualized using
Mermaid JS; quantitative outputs derived from HYDRUS-1D and ArcGIS Pro
simulations.*)

failure cascade\" where environmental drivers lead to belowground
impacts, creating a specific vulnerability that results in oak decline.

**5. Implementation Plan**

The implementation follows a sequenced \"Data-to-Dirt\" methodology. The
protocol is not a one-time event but a phased reconstruction of the
ecosystem.

**Phase 1: Foundational Remediation (The Physics Phase)**

-   **Methodology:** The site is assessed using soil core sampling to
    determine baseline bulk density. Pneumatic excavation is then
    performed radially from the trunk using an AirSpade 2000 (at 90-100
    psi) to fracture the soil profile without damaging root architecture
    (Holding & Pepper, 2013). This targets the removal of mechanical
    impedance \>1.5 g/cm³.

-   **Measurement:** Success is defined as a reduction in bulk density
    to \<1.4 g/cm³ and a demonstrated increase in infiltration rates,
    verifying that the physical constraints on root proliferation have
    been removed.

**Phase 2: Biological Activation (The Biology Phase)**

-   **Methodology:** Immediately following decompaction, the soil is
    amended with **\"Pre-Charged\" Biochar** (Pillar 3). This biochar is
    high-temperature (\>600°C) hardwood feedstock, selected for \>50%
    mesoprosity, and incubated for 2-4 weeks with a microbial solution
    to saturate pores. This is followed by a liquid drench of a specific
    microbial consortium (Pillar 4) containing *Rhizophagus irregularis*
    (AMF), *Pisolithus tinctorius* (EMF), and *Bacillus* spp. (PGPR).

-   **Measurement:** Effectiveness is modeled via hydraulic conductivity
    improvements (see Figure 2). Biochar amendment targets a 5x to 8x
    increase in the soil's capacity to absorb and transmit water,
    creating the \"sponge\" necessary to buffer against precipitation
    variability.

**Figure 2 Caption:** Modeled soil hydraulic conductivity before and
after mechanical decompaction and biochar amendment shows a 4-5 fold
increase in water transmission capacity in the top 30 cm of soil.

**Phase 3: Long-Term Protection (The Sustainability Phase)**

-   **Methodology:** The system is sealed with a 3-4 inch layer of
    coarse woody mulch (Pillar 6) to insulate the soil and prevent
    re-compaction.

-   **Measurement:** Long-term success is monitored via canopy stomatal
    conductance models (see Figure 4). The goal is to maintain
    conductance above the \"critical stress threshold\" during simulated
    drought events, proving that the treated soil allows the tree to
    continue gas exchange and photosynthesis when control trees have
    shut down.

**Figure 4 Caption:** Modeled stomatal conductance trends in *Quercus
fusiformis* during a 14-day hotter drought simulation demonstrate
retained gas exchange and lower physiological stress in treated trees.

**6. Broader Impacts**

The benefits of the \"Belowground Resilience\" protocol extend far
beyond the single tree to address fundamental societal challenges.

Societal and Public Health Benefits

By proactively improving soil health, the project ensures the viability
of the urban tree canopy, which is the most effective natural mitigator
of the urban heat island effect. This constitutes a direct public health
intervention. Preserving mature trees reduces peak summer ambient
temperatures, providing cooling benefits documented to reduce severe
impacts on human health, particularly for vulnerable populations.

Risk Management in the WUI

In the Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI), this protocol acts as critical
risk management infrastructure. Biologically healthy, well-hydrated
trees are inherently less flammable than drought-stressed, \"zombie\"
trees. By maintaining turgor pressure and reducing deadwood production,
the protocol mitigates the risk of catastrophic wildfire that threatens
communities and infrastructure.

Engaging Underserved Landowners

To adhere to the USDA NOFO requirement for equity, the project
explicitly incorporates a strategy for Underserved Landowners (ULOs). We
will target outreach to communities in High Poverty Areas using the
Climate and Economic Justice Screening Tool (CEJST) (see Figure 3). The
guide will include a module on \"Frugal Innovation,\" outlining how to
adapt the protocol using low-cost, locally sourced materials (e.g.,
municipal wood chips, homemade compost teas) to overcome financial
barriers, ensuring that high-quality climate resilience is not a luxury
good.

**Figure 3 Caption:** Priority Intervention Zones for soil biome

Collaborations

This project leverages a network of expertise. Academic guidance is

dissemination will be conducted through the International Society of

practitioners who manage the urban forest.

7\. Capability and Capacity

As a practicing arborist with an existing client base and equipment
(AirSpade), the applicant possesses the immediate capacity to execute
the pilot treatments. Financial oversight will be managed through
established business accounting practices compliant with federal
standards. The project scales by training municipal crews; a single crew
can treat 40-60 trees annually, making landscape-scale resilience
feasible within municipal budget cycles.

Budget Narrative

Funds will be used to support a Graduate Research Assistant for
essential data synthesis and modeling of the protocol\'s efficacy using
HYDRUS-1D software. This modeling is a prerequisite for validating the
\"water savings\" claims of the protocol before broad deployment. A
modest materials budget supports physical testing of amendment synergies
(e.g., biochar pre-charging) in the Central Texas test plots. If funding
were reduced by 10%, the project would prioritize modeling over field
materials; if increased, the project would expand field validation to a
second site in the Pacific Northwest to test geographic transferability.

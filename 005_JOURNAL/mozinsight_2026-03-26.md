## Alex Hormozi's Cold Email Strategy: 7 Principles for 2026

> "It takes a very long time to lay all the bricks across the bridge. Then once the last brick is in, all of a sudden the money that was just stockpiled… it flows through." — Alex Hormozi

---

### Principle 1: Patience & The Compound Effect

Cold email is a long game. Your first 100,000 emails might not generate the results you want—that's not failure, that's data. The gap between average (3.43% reply rate) and elite (10%+) is not talent; it's iteration. Analyze, improve, repeat.

**Practical tip:** Expect your first six months to be a learning period. Track every metric. Test every variable.

---

### Principle 2: Personalization is Non-Negotiable

"It's like walking to a nightclub versus walking to a library. Even if you're the same person, you might dress differently."

The best way to get your email ignored? Make one-to-one communication look like one-to-many (the "billboard problem"). Context-driven personalization outperforms generic {{first_name}} insertion:

- Recent funding rounds
- Hiring activity
- Role changes
- Product launches
- Visible company growth

**Result:** Only 5% of senders personalize every email—but those who do see 2-3x better results.

---

### Principle 3: Lead Magnets Change Everything

"The craziest response rates… the big breakthrough was when we switched the lead magnet."

Instead of asking for calls in every email, give something away that people would actually pay for. The key phrase: "obviously costs money."

**Examples:**
- Free trials of your software
- Personalized audits
- Mini courses that solve a specific problem
- Tools or calculators with immediate utility
- Implementable templates

**The shift:** Instead of "Can we schedule a call?" → "I put together a 3-minute audit of your current setup. Found some opportunities you might want to see."

---

### Principle 4: Pick One Channel and Master It

There are four ways to get customers—pick one. If you're trying to do cold email, cold calling, content, and paid ads simultaneously, you'll get mediocre results across the board.

**Focus:** Master one channel before expanding.

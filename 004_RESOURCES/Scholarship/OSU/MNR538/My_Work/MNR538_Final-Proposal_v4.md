## Abstract

This project addresses the critical failure of urban and peri-urban forests in Central Texas due to "hotter droughts"—a novel climatic phenomenon combining extreme heat with water scarcity. Oaks (*Quercus* spp.) are uniquely vulnerable to cumulative moisture stress, resulting in a "mortality lag" of 1–3 years that creates a window of opportunity for intervention. Current management remains reactive and fragmented. This project synthesizes academic research from soil physics, plant physiology, and microbiology into a synergistic, evidence-based *Practitioner's Guide* for arborists and land managers. The goal is to develop a sequenced, fundable protocol that transitions urban forestry from reactive symptom-treatment to proactive, systems-level soil biome resilience. By integrating mechanical soil remediation, biochar amendment, microbial inoculation, and strategic mulching, this protocol directly supports USDA climate adaptation and Justice40 priorities by mitigating the urban heat island effect, protecting carbon stocks, and providing municipalities with practical tools to manage wildfire risk in the Wildland-Urban Interface. The guide will be validated through pilot-scale application in Central Texas and modeled for geographic transferability to the Pacific Northwest.

### 2. The Mechanism of Failure: Why Belowground Matters

Managed forests globally are confronting a novel and distinct class of extreme events. We have shifted from historical precipitation-driven droughts to "hotter droughts," in which record heat amplifies atmospheric water demand (Vapor Pressure Deficit, or VPD). This phenomenon creates a "hotter-drought fingerprint" that is driving non-linear increases in tree mortality worldwide (Hammond et al., 2022).

In the Central Texas "living laboratory," this crisis manifests as a "mortality lag." Trees often survive the acute heat event but succumb 1 to 3 years later. This delay indicates a catastrophic, invisible failure occurring belowground. Recent modeling confirms that while many tree species suffer from acute heat stress, dominant oaks (*Quercus* spp.) are uniquely vulnerable to "cumulative long-term moisture stress," specifically measured by the 36-month Standardized Precipitation-Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI) (Lawal et al., 2024). This finding pinpoints the mechanism of failure: the belowground soil biome collapses, losing its ability to hold water and sustain fungal health.

**The Triple Threat Under Hotter Drought:**

Three physiological mechanisms drive oak mortality in high-heat, low-moisture environments. First, extreme atmospheric water demand (VPD) exceeds the hydraulic capacity of tree root systems to deliver water to foliage. Second, in response to this threat, trees close their stomata to prevent desiccation—but this also halts photosynthesis, forcing trees to survive on stored non-structural carbohydrates (NSC). This depletion typically occurs within 2–8 weeks under extreme VPD. Third, if hydraulic tension persists, xylem cavitation occurs (air embolism), causing hydraulic dysfunction analogous to a heart attack in the tree. Once a critical percentage of the hydraulic architecture is embolized, the tree cannot supply water to foliage, leading to irreversible desiccation and death (Adams et al., 2017; McDowell et al., 2008).

**The Edaphic Amplification:**

Urban soils degrade this scenario. Compaction, typically exceeding bulk densities of 1.5 g/cm³, creates mechanical impedance that forces roots to remain shallow. The shallow rooting zone heats up and dries out first, disconnecting seedlings and young trees from deeper soil moisture reserves. Destruction of soil macropores through compaction also cripples water infiltration, creating "edaphic drought"—a physiological desiccation that persists longer than meteorological drought and prevents deep root establishment (Day & Bassuk, 1994).

**The Mycorrhizal Network Collapse:**

Most critically, *Quercus* species are obligate symbionts with ectomycorrhizal (ECM) fungi forming common mycorrhizal networks (CMN). These fungal networks act as the forest's "safety net," transferring nutrients and redistributing water hydraulically between trees. Hotter drought breaks this system via "carbon cutoff": as the carbon-starved tree curtails sugar delivery to roots to conserve energy for survival, the fungal network starves and collapses. Seedlings become isolated, decoupled from collective resource pools, and critically vulnerable to moisture stress exactly when they need belowground support most. This severance of the mycorrhizal network ensures regeneration failure and threatens forest persistence (Lehmann & Joseph, 2015).

To address this systemic failure at the intersection of physiology and soil ecology, this project pursues three primary goals aligned with USDA priorities for forest resilience, climate adaptation, and landscape-scale conservation:

**Goal 1: Synthesize Knowledge.** To bridge the critical gap between academic science (soil physics, plant physiology, microbiology) and field practice by synthesizing complex, peer-reviewed research into a single, accessible, and immediately actionable *Practitioner's Guide* that practitioners can deploy without advanced degrees in soil science.

**Goal 2: Establish a Proactive Protocol.** To fundamentally shift the arboricultural industry from a reactive, piecemeal treatment model—where individual tools are deployed in isolation—to a proactive, sequenced, synergistic protocol that builds holistic, long-term resilience from the soil up, unlocking exponential benefits through integration.

**Goal 3: Create an Adaptable, Fundable Model.** To develop a practical, replicable, and fundable framework that municipalities, forest landowners, and conservation organizations can adopt as a core climate adaptation strategy, with demonstrated scalability and cost-effectiveness for landscape-level implementation.

### 4. Context: The Practitioner Gap

This project was born from front-line experience. As a practicing arborist in Central Texas, I witness daily the failure of urban trees under heat domes and flash droughts. However, this is not a local problem; fir die-offs in the Pacific Northwest and oak decline globally are regional expressions of the same systems-level failure. The core challenge is not a lack of tools but a lack of a *system*.

Practitioners have access to pneumatic soil excavation (air spades), biochar amendments, and microbial inoculants. Yet they deploy these interventions in an unsequenced, piecemeal manner, often without understanding the physiological and edaphic mechanisms these tools are meant to address. Consequently, the exponential benefits of synergistic, physics-first intervention remain untapped. Foundational literature demonstrates that "pre-charging" biochar with fungi effectively transforms it into a "microbial reef" that accelerates fungal colonization (Lehmann & Joseph, 2015). Field practice has validated that this approach is superior to applying raw biochar—yet this synergy is not systematized in practitioner guidance.

This guide shifts the field from a reactive "tree-by-tree symptomatic treatment" model to a proactive, systems-level approach to ecological stewardship rooted in rigorous science.

### 5. Conceptual Framework: The Critical Failure Cascade

The proposed intervention relies on a conceptual model identifying a "Critical Failure Cascade" (Figure 1). The project's **6-Pillar Protocol**, organized into three phases, is designed to intervene at specific leverage points to disrupt this cascade before irreversible mortality occurs.

#### Figure 1: The Critical Failure Cascade

**Figure 1 Description:** The conceptual model synthesized from the physiological literature (Adams et al., 2017; Hammond et al., 2022; Lawal et al., 2024; McDowell et al., 2008) illustrates the progression of mortality under hotter drought:

1. **Environmental Drivers (Upper Left):** The cascade begins with the convergence of two primary stressors:
   - **Hotter Drought:** Record heat coupled with precipitation deficit, creating extreme vapor pressure deficit (VPD) and cumulative moisture stress.
   - **Urban Soils:** Compaction (bulk density >1.5 g/cm³) and carbon depletion (50–80% below natural levels) resulting from legacy land-use trauma.

2. **Belowground Failure (Center):** This convergence triggers **Soil Biome Collapse**, resulting in two immediate, cascading impacts:
   - **Mycorrhizal Network Failure:** Loss of the tree's "extended root system"—the fungal hyphae that access micropore water and redistribute resources.
   - **Loss of Water Holding Capacity:** Urban soils lack the organic matter and structure necessary to retain plant-available water (PAW), meaning precipitation events do not translate into soil water availability.

3. **Specific Vulnerability (Lower Center):** This collapse creates a specific vulnerability identified by Lawal et al. (2024): **Cumulative Long-Term Moisture Stress**, where trees experience persistent water deficit across seasonal cycles, preventing recovery between drought pulses.

4. **Outcome (Lower Right):** Without buffering capacity, the tree crosses a physiological threshold. The triple threat (VPD, hydraulic failure, carbon starvation) overwhelms the tree's survival mechanisms, resulting in **Oak Decline & Mortality**.

#### The 6-Pillar Protocol as Cascade Intervention

The 6-Pillar Protocol intervenes at critical failure points to disrupt this chain:

- **Phase 1 (Foundational Remediation—Pillars 1 & 2)** breaks the physical barrier imposed by compaction and chemical limitation, restoring the basic conditions necessary for root proliferation and nutrient availability.
- **Phase 2 (Biological Activation—Pillars 3, 4, & 5)** reintroduces life and creates the biological infrastructure (biochar reef habitat, microbial consortia, food sources) necessary for the tree to develop drought tolerance and fungal symbiosis.
- **Phase 3 (Long-Term Protection—Pillar 6)** locks in gains by preventing recompaction and thermal stress, ensuring system stability over multi-year timescales.

### 6. Implementation Plan: Data-to-Dirt Methodology

The implementation follows a rigorously sequenced "Data-to-Dirt" methodology, rooted in the principle that **physical remediation must precede biological activation**. The protocol is not a one-time event but a phased reconstruction of the soil ecosystem.

#### Phase 1: Foundational Remediation (The Physics Phase)

**Objective:** Remove physical and chemical constraints on root proliferation and water infiltration.

**Pillar 1 — Mechanical Decompaction:**

*Methodology:* Site assessment begins with baseline soil core sampling at 0–30 cm depth, with cores collected radially at 1 m, 2 m, and 3 m distances from the tree stem. Bulk density is measured by drying cores to constant weight and calculating mass per volume, with a target identification of soils exceeding root-limiting thresholds (>1.4 g/cm³) (Day & Bassuk, 1994). Pneumatic excavation is then performed using an AirSpade 2000 operated at 90–100 psi, fracturing soil radially from the trunk in a pattern designed to disrupt structural continuity without damaging living roots. Post-treatment soil cores are collected at identical locations to measure efficacy.

*Measurement Endpoints:* Success is defined as: (1) bulk density reduction to <1.4 g/cm³, (2) doubled infiltration rates measured by double-ring infiltrometer, and (3) GIS documentation of treated zones. These metrics verify that mechanical impedance has been removed and that the soil pore structure has been restored to support root proliferation.

*Mechanism:* By restoring aerobic conditions and fracturing the compacted matrix, decompaction enables roots to access deeper, more stable soil layers where moisture availability and temperature volatility are less extreme. This is a prerequisite for subsequent biological interventions, as anaerobic, compacted soils are hostile to beneficial aerobic fungi and favor pathogens.

**Pillar 2 — Chemical Remediation:**

*Methodology:* Baseline soil chemical analysis includes pH, organic matter (OM), extractable macronutrients (N, P, K), and micronutrients (Fe, Zn, B). pH adjustment targets the optimal range of 6.0–7.0 for nutrient availability (species-dependent). Elemental sulfur or organic amendments are applied based on laboratory recommendations and incorporated into the upper 15 cm via the decompaction process.

*Measurement Endpoints:* Post-treatment soil chemistry report documenting pH, OM, and NPK levels. Success is defined as pH within target range and OM >3%.

#### Phase 2: Biological Activation (The Biology Phase)

**Objective:** Reintroduce a metabolically active soil biome and create the necessary infrastructure to combat long-term moisture stress.

**Pillar 3 — "Pre-Charged" Biochar:**

*Methodology:* High-temperature (>600°C), hardwood-derived biochar is selected for mesoporosity >50% (pore diameter 2–50 nm), which efficiently retains plant-available water (PAW). The biochar is "pre-charged" by incubating it for 2–4 weeks in an aqueous microbial solution (described below) to saturate pores with beneficial microorganisms before soil application. This approach is superior to raw biochar amendment because it accelerates the establishment of a stable microbial community, reducing colonization lag time from weeks to months (Lehmann & Joseph, 2015).

*Measurement Endpoints:* Application rate (lbs/sq. ft.), documented pre-charging protocol, and subsequent biochar-mediated improvements in soil water holding capacity. Secondary measurement: microbial community analysis (16S rRNA sequencing) at 0, 3, 6, and 12 months post-application to confirm fungal and bacterial establishment.

*Hydraulic Modeling:* The expected improvement in soil hydraulic conductivity is modeled using HYDRUS-1D, a one-dimensional water flow and solute transport simulator (Šimůnek et al., 2008). The model incorporates measured soil texture (via hydrometer analysis), organic matter content (before and after biochar amendment), and biochar properties (porosity, water retention characteristics). The simulation targets a quantitative increase in unsaturated hydraulic conductivity of 5x–8x in the upper 30 cm, compared to compacted control soils, creating the "sponge" necessary to buffer against precipitation variability and buffer trees against the critical stress threshold during drought pulses.

**Pillar 4 — Microbial Inoculation:**

*Methodology:* A microbial consortium is applied as a liquid drench, containing three functional groups: (1) arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF), specifically *Rhizophagus irregularis*, which establish symbiotic relationships and enhance nutrient uptake; (2) ectomycorrhizal fungi (EMF), specifically *Pisolithus tinctorius*, which establish the obligate symbiosis required by oak species; and (3) plant growth-promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR), specifically *Bacillus* spp., which produce ACC deaminase (an enzyme regulating stress-induced ethylene) and exopolysaccharides (which bind soil particles and increase water retention).

*Measurement Endpoints:* Volume and species composition of inoculants applied; qPCR quantification of microbial abundance at 3, 6, and 12 months post-application; and root colonization rates measured via microscopy (% root length colonized).

*Drought-Tolerance Mechanism:* Meta-analyses demonstrate that PGPR are 45% more effective at enhancing shoot biomass under drought conditions compared to only 28% under optimal conditions (Rubin et al., 2017). The mechanism: PGPR produce ACC deaminase, which reduces stress-induced ethylene production, delaying senescence and maintaining photosynthetic capacity even under persistent water deficit. Additionally, exopolysaccharide production increases soil water retention, providing continuous hydraulic support to roots.

**Pillar 5 — Quality Compost and Organic Matter:**

*Methodology:* The treated area is top-dressed with 1–2 inches of locally sourced, high-quality compost or humates, providing immediate, high-quality food source for the soil biome. This "jump-starts" the microbial community in its new habitat, accelerating heterotrophic respiration and nutrient cycling.

*Measurement Endpoints:* Cubic yards of compost applied; compost C:N ratio and decomposition rate; soil respiration rate (mg CO₂/g soil/hr) at 0, 3, 6, and 12 months, indicating biome metabolic activity.

#### Phase 3: Long-Term Protection (The Sustainability Phase)

**Pillar 6 — Coarse Woody Mulch:**

*Methodology:* A 3–4 inch layer of coarse, woody mulch (chipped tree wood, 5–15 cm fragments) is applied over the entire treated area, extending to the drip line.

*Measurement Endpoints:* Depth and area of mulch application (cubic yards); soil temperature monitoring at 5 cm and 15 cm depth; soil moisture retention curves (measured via tensiometer) at 3, 6, and 12 months; and visual assessment of mulch persistence (no recompaction to bulk density >1.4 g/cm³ within 3 years post-application).

*Protection Mechanisms:* Mulch insulates soil from extreme heat, reducing thermal stress on newly established roots and microbes. Critically, mulch prevents the destructive recompaction that typically occurs within 1–3 years post-intervention due to rain impact and gravity (Scharenbroch & Watson, 2014). A persistent mulch layer acts as the "armor" of the system, maintaining the physical and biological gains achieved in Phases 1 and 2.

#### Monitoring and Expected Outcomes

**Primary Outcome Metric: Stomatal Conductance Under Drought Stress**

The ultimate test of protocol efficacy is whether treated trees maintain photosynthetic function—and thus survival—under hotter drought. This is measured via stomatal conductance, a physiological parameter reflecting the degree to which stomata remain open and allowing gas exchange.

*Methodology:* Stomatal conductance is modeled for *Quercus fusiformis* (an oak species endemic to Central Texas) using the Farquhar-Leuning photosynthesis model coupled with the hydraulic vulnerability curve of the species. The model incorporates: (1) soil water potential under drought conditions (modeled via HYDRUS-1D output), (2) leaf water potential as a function of soil water potential and transpirational demand (VPD), (3) stomatal opening as a function of leaf water potential and photosynthetic demand, and (4) photosynthetic rate as a function of stomatal opening.

*Expected Outcome (Figure 4):* During a simulated 14-day hotter drought event (high VPD, low soil moisture), treated trees are expected to maintain stomatal conductance above the "critical stress threshold"—the minimum conductance necessary to continue gas exchange and photosynthesis—while untreated control trees rapidly decline in conductance, close stomata, and enter carbon starvation. This demonstrates that the restored soil biome allows the tree to continue energy production when environmental conditions would otherwise force closure and starvation.

*Validation Strategy:* Field measurements of stomatal conductance will be conducted using a portable photosynthesis system (LI-6400XT) at treatment and control sites during natural drought pulses in Year 1, and results will be compared to model predictions to validate or refine the Farquhar-Leuning framework.

### 7. Broader Impacts: Societal and Policy Benefits

#### Public Health and Climate Resilience

By proactively improving soil health and ensuring the viability of the urban tree canopy, this project constitutes a direct public health intervention. Urban tree canopy is the most effective natural mitigator of the urban heat island effect, which creates mortality risks during extreme heat events. Healthy, well-hydrated urban forests reduce peak summer ambient temperatures by 2–5°C in adjacent neighborhoods, providing documented cooling benefits that reduce heat-related hospital admissions and mortality, particularly among elderly and vulnerable populations (Pouyat et al., 2020).

Preserving mature trees also provides long-term carbon storage. A mature oak sequesters approximately 20 kg of carbon annually. Municipal tree loss due to drought stress represents an irreversible release of that stored carbon, accelerating climate destabilization. By maintaining canopy resilience, this protocol protects a critical natural climate solution.

#### Wildfire Risk Management in the Wildland-Urban Interface

In the Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI), where residential communities abut forests and woodlands, this protocol functions as critical risk management infrastructure. Drought-stressed, "zombie" trees become physiologically vulnerable to pest outbreaks and are highly flammable due to elevated moisture stress and deadwood accumulation. Conversely, biologically healthy, well-hydrated trees maintain turgor pressure, produce fewer dead branches, and have lower foliar moisture content, making them inherently less prone to ignition and crown fire propagation. By maintaining tree vitality, the protocol directly mitigates the risk of catastrophic wildfire that threatens both community safety and critical infrastructure.

#### Economic Reframing: Prevention vs. Emergency Response

Current municipal forestry budgets treat tree management as a reactive cost center—emergency removals of dead trees, liability mitigation, and pest control consume 60–80% of urban forestry budgets. This protocol reframes tree care as a preventative investment. Because urban soils are significantly depleted of organic carbon (50–80% below natural levels) and cannot recover naturally over human timescales, active restoration preserves critical ecosystem services and delivers long-term financial value. A single mature tree provides approximately $300/year in ecosystem services (cooling, air filtration, stormwater absorption). Protecting that tree from mortality via $500–1,000 in proactive soil treatment yields a 5–10 year simple payback and decades of sustained benefit.

#### Engaging Underserved Landowners: Equity and Access

To adhere to the USDA NOFO requirement for Justice40 equity, this project explicitly incorporates a strategy for Underserved Landowners (ULOs). We will target outreach to communities in High Poverty Areas using the Climate and Economic Justice Screening Tool (CEJST) to identify neighborhoods where climate vulnerability, environmental burden, and demographic disadvantage overlap.

**Specific ULO Engagement Strategy:**

1. **Cost-Share and Payment Model:** The project will structure technical assistance and materials support to overcome financial barriers. For municipalities and nonprofits serving high-poverty neighborhoods, the guide will include protocols for sourcing materials at reduced cost (e.g., municipal wood chips, homemade compost teas, salvaged biochar from local pyrolysis operations).

2. **"Frugal Innovation" Module:** The *Practitioner's Guide* will include a dedicated module demonstrating how to adapt the 6-Pillar Protocol using low-cost, locally sourced materials without sacrificing efficacy. This ensures that high-quality climate resilience is not a luxury good available only to affluent neighborhoods.

3. **Training and Workforce Development:** The project will coordinate with Texas A&M AgriLife Extension to develop train-the-trainer workshops targeting municipal crews and community tree-care organizations that serve underserved neighborhoods. This builds local capacity and ensures the protocol is disseminated through trusted institutions.

4. **Measurement of Equity Outcomes:** The project will track demographic characteristics of treatment sites, monitoring whether underserved communities receive equitable access to the protocol. Success is defined as: (1) ≥40% of treatment sites located in high-poverty tracts, (2) ≥20% of sites managed by community-based organizations, and (3) documented cost reductions through frugal innovation (target: 30% reduction from standard implementation cost).

Three field trips to Central Texas Edwards Plateau field sites are budgeted at $600/trip (gas, lodging, per diem for 2 technicians). Approximately 300 miles from OSU Corvallis to the treatment area in Travis County, Texas. This budget allows for flexibility in trip timing (to coordinate with moisture availability and seasonal windows for treatment application) and ensures adequate time for site preparation, treatment application, and post-treatment monitoring.

### Software Licenses – $1,200

HYDRUS-1D and HYDRUS-2D licenses enable rigorous simulation of water flow and solute transport in soil, essential for validating the hydraulic improvements claimed in the protocol. GIS software (ArcGIS Pro) enables geospatial analysis, targeting, and mapping of priority intervention zones. These tools are industry-standard in soil hydrology and landscape planning and are essential for the technical credibility of the project.

### Soil Laboratory Services – $200

Baseline and post-treatment soil analysis (bulk density, pH, organic matter, NPK) is contracted to a third-party, USDA-accredited soil laboratory. This ensures analytical consistency, removes potential bias from in-house analysis, and provides documentation acceptable to USDA reviewers. The modest cost reflects a reduced rate for multiple samples analyzed as a batch.

### Indirect Costs (De Minimis Rate) – $3,644

OSU does not have a Negotiated Indirect Cost Rate Agreement (NICRA) for this funding opportunity; therefore, the de minimis rate of 10% of modified total direct costs is applied. This rate is the maximum allowable for non-federal entities without a formal agreement and reflects institutional administrative costs (facility overhead, human resources, compliance).

---

### Scalability and Contingency

The budget is designed with flexibility. If funding is reduced by 10% ($3,607), the project would prioritize HYDRUS modeling and literature synthesis over field materials testing. The guide would still be completed and validated against published studies, though secondary field validation would be deferred. If funding is increased by 10% ($4,009), the project would expand field validation to a secondary site in the Pacific Northwest (Oregon or Washington) to test geographic transferability of the protocol beyond the Central Texas Edwards Plateau. This geographic extension is critical for demonstrating that the protocol is not regionally specific but represents a generalizable approach to addressing hotter drought across diverse forest types and soil conditions.

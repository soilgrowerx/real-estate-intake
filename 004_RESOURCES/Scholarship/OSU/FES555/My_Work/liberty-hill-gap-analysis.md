# Liberty Hill, Texas Tree Ordinance Gap Analysis

## 1. Purpose and Scope

This document provides a **technical gap analysis** of Liberty Hill, Texas’s tree protection framework, with emphasis on § 9A-6.07 (Tree Inventory and Protection) and related Unified Development Code (UDC) provisions. It is structured for:

- Academic analysis in an **urban forestry policy** context.
- Strategic groundwork for **Soil Grower** and **Arboracle STIM** integration.
- Policy input to Liberty Hill’s **2025–2026 UDC rewrite** process.


## 2. Current Regulatory Framework Overview

### 2.1 Measurement Standard

- Trees are measured at **4 feet above ground level** using standard DBH (Diameter at Breast Height) methodology.
- The **inventory threshold** begins at **>10 inches diameter**; trees below this threshold are not counted in required inventories.

### 2.2 Protection Classes

**Standard / Protected Trees (10"–18.9" DBH)**
- Included in the required inventory.
- Up to **40%** of these trees may be removed on a site.
- Remaining trees must be protected from construction damage.

**Hardwood Protected Trees (≥19" DBH)**
- Subject to **clear-cutting prohibition** for residential and commercial development.
- Removal requires **Planning and Zoning Commission** (P&Z) approval.
- City Administrator may approve removal with an “acceptable replacement plan.”

### 2.3 Heritage Tree Program

- Liberty Hill maintains a **Heritage Tree Permit Application** process.
- Heritage trees require a permit for:
  - Health inspection
  - Pruning
  - Chemical treatment
  - Root pruning
  - Removal
- However, **no explicit diameter threshold** or species list for heritage trees is codified in the publicly accessible ordinance text.

### 2.4 Exemptions

Exemptions are provided for:
- Trees posing a **documented safety risk**.
- Trees within **right-of-way expansion areas**.
- Trees removed as part of **fire prevention or emergency operations**.
- Certain species identified as **problematic for health/safety**: Hackberry, Mesquite, Ash Juniper/Cedar.


## 3. Eight Critical Gaps (with Severity Ratings)

This section identifies and rates the most significant gaps in Liberty Hill’s tree protection framework relative to best practices in Central Texas (Austin, Round Rock, Cedar Park, Dripping Springs) and Texas HB 7 (2017).

### 3.1 Gap 1 – No Mandatory Baseline Tree Survey (🔴 Critical)

**Issue:**
- The current ordinance does **not require a comprehensive baseline tree survey** for all development applications.
- Inventory requirements apply only once certain thresholds/triggers are met and focus on trees ≥10" DBH.

**Implications:**
- Allows **pre-application clearing** of trees without documentation.
- Encourages “cut first, permit later” behavior.
- Undermines long-term canopy tracking and data-driven urban forestry planning.

**Best Practice Comparison:**
- Austin, Round Rock, Cedar Park, and Dripping Springs require **tree surveys at the subdivision or site development stage**.

**Recommendation:**
- Mandate a **full-site tree survey** for all development over a defined size (e.g., 5,000 SF) at the **plat or preliminary plan stage**, documented via an AI-native platform like Arboracle STIM and certified by a professional arborist.


### 3.2 Gap 2 – Heritage Tree Threshold Undefined (🔴 Critical)

**Issue:**
- Heritage tree status is referenced in application forms but **not defined in ordinance text** by DBH or species.

**Implications:**
- Creates **administrative uncertainty** for developers, homeowners, and staff.
- Weakens legal defensibility of enforcement actions.
- Makes it impossible to model or predict heritage tree impacts across the city.

**Best Practice Comparison:**
- Austin: Heritage trees ≥24" DBH, species-specific.
- Dripping Springs: Heritage trees typically ≥18" DBH.
- Round Rock: Species and size thresholds established through technical manuals.

**Recommendation:**
- Codify a **heritage tree definition** (e.g., ≥18" DBH and/or specific native species such as live oak, pecan, cedar elm) and embed this in the UDC with clear protection standards and variance processes.


### 3.3 Gap 3 – Tree Replacement Fees Not Published (🔴 Critical)

**Issue:**
- The ordinance references **replacement plans** and mitigation but **does not publish a fee schedule** (e.g., $ per DBH inch).

**Implications:**
- Developers and homeowners cannot **predict financial impacts**.
- Opens the door to **ad hoc negotiation** rather than consistent policy.
- Limits the city’s ability to manage canopy through economic signals.

**Best Practice Comparison:**
- Peer cities maintain **publicly accessible fee schedules** for mitigation and allow HB 7 planting credits.

**Recommendation:**
- Adopt and publish a **transparent mitigation fee schedule**, indexed to DBH and tree class (standard vs heritage), and clearly incorporate HB 7 planting credits and offset percentages.


### 3.4 Gap 4 – Replacement Ratios Ambiguous (🟠 Major)

**Issue:**
- Liberty Hill uses the concept of an “acceptable replacement plan” without specifying **minimum ratios** (e.g., 1:1, 2:1, 3:1).

**Implications:**
- Inconsistent outcomes across projects.
- Weakens canopy recovery after large removals.
- Makes it hard to quantify long-term forest structure or enforce compliance.

**Best Practice Comparison:**
- Dripping Springs: **3:1 ratio** for heritage trees.
- Austin and Round Rock: Use **tiered replacement** (more aggressive for large or high-value trees).

**Recommendation:**
- Establish **minimum replacement ratios**, such as:
  - 1:1 for standard protected trees (8"–17.9" DBH).
  - 2:1 for large protected trees (18"–23.9" DBH).
  - 3:1 for heritage trees (≥24" DBH or designated species).
- Integrate this into a **points-based system** to allow flexible but quantifiable compliance.


### 3.5 Gap 5 – Exempted Species List Incomplete and Undocumented (🟠 Major)

**Issue:**
- Certain species (e.g., Hackberry, Mesquite, Ash Juniper/Cedar) are referenced as exempt or problem species, but there is no **comprehensive, codified Preferred Plant / Exempt Species List** linked to ecological function and invasiveness.

**Implications:**
- May unintentionally encourage removal of species that have ecological value or successional importance.
- Lacks alignment with **native plant and water conservation goals** (xeriscaping, Dark Sky/Scenic City aims).

**Best Practice Comparison:**
- Austin and some Hill Country peers maintain explicit **preferred native plant lists** and invasive species lists.

**Recommendation:**
- Develop and codify a **Preferred Plant List and Exempt Species List**, driven by ecological function, drought tolerance, and mycorrhizal associations, and integrate it directly into landscape/ tree preservation sections.


### 3.6 Gap 6 – Enforcement and Penalties Unpublished (🟠 Major)

**Issue:**
- The ordinance framework does not clearly publish **penalty structures** for non-compliance (unauthorized removal, damage, or failure to maintain replacement trees).

**Implications:**
- Weak deterrent effect.
- Inconsistent treatment of violators.
- Difficult to achieve meaningful canopy protection and long-term compliance.

**Best Practice Comparison:**
- Peer cities specify **fines, stop-work orders**, and sometimes criminal penalties for egregious violations.

**Recommendation:**
- Create a clear **penalty matrix** (per tree / per inch / per offense) with administrative tools (stop-work orders, double-fee penalties for after-the-fact permits), along with an appeals process.


### 3.7 Gap 7 – No Formal, Codified Approval Process (🟠 Major)

**Issue:**
- The ordinance references P&Z Commission approval and City Administrator discretion but does not provide **codified workflows**, submittal standards, or decision criteria.

**Implications:**
- Increases **subjectivity** in decision-making.
- Causes frustration for applicants who lack predictable timelines and requirements.
- Makes it harder to adopt technology (no defined stage to integrate digital workflows).

**Best Practice Comparison:**
- Many peer cities define **administrative versus discretionary approvals**, timelines, and documentation requirements.

**Recommendation:**
- Codify a **tree review workflow** with:
  - Clear triggers for administrative vs P&Z decisions.
  - Defined submittal requirements (survey, health assessment, mitigation plan).
  - Timelines and decision standards.
- Integrate digital submission standards (e.g., through Arboracle STIM).


### 3.8 Gap 8 – HB 7 (2017) Compliance Not Explicit (🟠 Major)

**Issue:**
- The ordinance does not explicitly reference **Texas HB 7** or its required planting credits and homeowner exemptions.

**Implications:**
- Legal ambiguity and risk if enforcement conflicts with HB 7.
- Missed opportunity to incentivize **on-site planting** and regenerative practices.

**Best Practice Comparison:**
- Many Texas cities now explicitly incorporate HB 7 planting credits and homeowner protections.

**Recommendation:**
- Amend the UDC to explicitly codify HB 7 requirements, including:
  - Homeowner exemption for trees <10" DBH.
  - Planting credit offsets for homeowners, developers, and commercial owners.
  - Administrative process for documenting mitigation through planting.


## 4. Comparative Benchmarking: Liberty Hill vs. Peer Cities

### 4.1 Protected Tree Thresholds

- **Liberty Hill:** 19" DBH threshold for “protected hardwood” trees.
- **Round Rock:** 8" DBH threshold.
- **Cedar Park:** 8" DBH threshold.
- **Dripping Springs:** 8" DBH protected; 18"+ heritage.
- **Austin:** 8"–18" protected; 24"+ heritage, species-specific.

**Conclusion:** Liberty Hill’s 19" threshold is **higher** (more permissive) than all examined peers, protecting fewer mid-sized canopy trees and diverging from regional norms.


### 4.2 Heritage Trees and Replacement Ratios

- **Liberty Hill:** Heritage framework exists administratively; diameter/species undefined; ratios undefined.
- **Dripping Springs:** Heritage ≥18"; 3:1 replacement for heritage trees.
- **Austin / Round Rock:** Use **tiered replacement systems** tied to size and species.

**Conclusion:** Liberty Hill’s heritage framework is **conceptually present** but **functionally incomplete**, limiting its effectiveness.


### 4.3 Process and Transparency

- Peer cities publish:
  - Fee schedules.
  - Replacement standards.
  - Tree technical manuals.
  - Online guidance for homeowners and developers.
- Liberty Hill lacks **equivalent published technical documentation** and fee structures.


## 5. UDC Rewrite Context and Opportunities

### 5.1 Diagnostic Themes Relevant to Trees

The UDC Diagnostic Report identifies a key theme: **“Strengthen tree/landscaping and water conservation regulations.”** Within this theme, the report recommends:

- Creating a **tree removal/clearing permit** for non-development activity.
- Requiring **tree surveys and preservation plans** at plat/subdivision stages.
- Clarifying and implementing **mitigation ratios and fee structures**.
- Expanding coverage to **single-family** properties.
- Requiring **native, drought-tolerant species** and updating planting standards.

### 5.2 Timing

- The rewrite is scheduled over **18 months**, roughly October 2025 – October 2026.
- Draft language and public hearings are expected within this window, providing a critical window for **expert input and technology proposals**.


## 6. Priority Recommendations (Tiered)

### Tier 1 – Critical (Immediate Focus)

1. **Codify Mandatory Tree Surveys**
   - Require AI-native inventories (e.g., Arboracle STIM) at plat and site plan stages.

2. **Define Heritage Tree Thresholds**
   - Adopt DBH and species-based criteria, with clear protection and variance procedures.

3. **Publish Mitigation Fee Schedule**
   - Establish per-inch DBH fees, differentiated by class (standard vs heritage).

4. **Codify HB 7 Compliance**
   - Explicitly include planting credits and homeowner protections.


### Tier 2 – Major (Near-Term)

5. **Standardize Replacement Ratios**
   - Implement tiered ratios (1:1, 2:1, 3:1) and a points system.

6. **Create a Preferred Plant & Exempt Species List**
   - Integrate into both landscaping and tree preservation standards.

7. **Create Transparent Enforcement & Penalty Framework**
   - Include fines, stop-work orders, and appeals mechanisms.


### Tier 3 – Strategic (Medium-Term)

8. **Formalize Digital Workflows and Approvals**
   - Adopt clear review processes and integrate digital tools (Arboracle STIM) for submittals, tracking, and enforcement.

9. **Integrate Cluster Development and Canopy Goals**
   - Tie tree preservation standards to cluster development incentives and long-range canopy targets.

## 9. Conclusion

Liberty Hill’s current tree ordinance is **functionally present but structurally incomplete** when measured against modern Hill Country standards and Texas HB 7. The city protects large hardwoods and recognizes heritage trees but lacks:

- Mandatory baseline inventories.
- Clear heritage thresholds.
- Published fee and replacement structures.
- Explicit HB 7 compliance.

The ongoing UDC rewrite—explicitly targeting tree and landscaping regulations—creates an exceptional window for **regenerative, data-driven urban forestry policy**, where Arboracle STIM and Soil Grower can play central roles in defining Liberty Hill’s next-generation tree protection framework.

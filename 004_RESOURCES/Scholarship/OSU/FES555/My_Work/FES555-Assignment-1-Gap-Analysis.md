## Introduction

Liberty Hill, Texas is a rapidly growing municipality in Williamson County, situated in the Hill Country region north of Austin. The city has a history of progressive planning, evidenced by its recent Dark Sky Certification (July 2025) and ongoing comprehensive planning initiatives. However, the city's urban tree protection ordinance, codified in § 9A-6.07 of the Unified Development Code, exhibits significant gaps when compared to both regional standards and the criteria necessary for sustainable urban forest management. This gap analysis evaluates the current state of Liberty Hill's tree preservation program against where the program could be within the context of the city's ongoing 2025–2026 Unified Development Code (UDC) rewrite, which explicitly identifies tree preservation as a priority theme. The analysis identifies eight core gaps, assesses root causes, and recommends specific actions to transition Liberty Hill's tree preservation program from a reactive, administratively discretionary system to a proactive, data-driven, and transparent framework aligned with best practices in Hill Country peer municipalities and contemporary urban forestry standards.

## Part 1: Current State of Liberty Hill's Tree Preservation Program

### Existing Ordinance Framework

Liberty Hill's primary tree protection regulation, § 9A-6.07 (Tree Inventory and Protection), establishes a two-tier protection system based on tree diameter at breast height (DBH), measured at four feet above ground level. Trees smaller than 10 inches DBH are excluded from inventory requirements. Standard protected trees, defined as those between 10 and 18.9 inches DBH, may have up to 40 percent removed on a development site, with remaining trees requiring protection from construction damage. Hardwood protected trees, those 19 inches in diameter or larger, are subject to a clear-cutting prohibition on residential and commercial properties and require Planning and Zoning Commission approval for removal. The city also administers a Heritage Tree Permit Application process, though the specific DBH or species criteria defining heritage tree status are not published in the accessible ordinance text.

The current framework includes limited exemptions: trees posing documented safety risks, trees within right-of-way expansion zones, trees removed during fire prevention operations, and certain designated species (Hackberry, Mesquite, Ash Juniper/Cedar) identified as problematic for health or safety reasons. The ordinance permits the City Administrator to approve tree removal if an applicant submits an "acceptable replacement plan," but the code does not define what constitutes acceptable ratios, fee structures, or standards of care.

### Program Performance and Metrics

Liberty Hill currently lacks an explicit strategic plan or annual scorecard for the tree preservation program. The 2023 LHTX2040 Comprehensive Plan identifies sustainability and environmental stewardship as core values, and Resolution 2023-R-001 establishes that 50 percent of developed property shall employ drought-resistant landscaping, linking tree preservation to broader water conservation goals. However, no quantitative metrics are published for canopy coverage targets, species diversity, or tree inventory outcomes. The city does not maintain a published baseline inventory of trees, making it impossible to measure progress toward canopy retention or restoration goals.

The Liberty Hill UDC Diagnostic Report (released September 30, 2025) explicitly identifies a programmatic gap, noting that the current ordinance contains a "loophole [that] allows an owner to cut trees with no baseline tree survey" prior to development application submission. This diagnostic recognition directly validates that the current program cannot measure or manage cumulative canopy loss across the municipality.

### Comparative Program Performance

To contextualize Liberty Hill's program performance, a comparison with three Hill Country peer municipalities provides objective benchmarking. Round Rock, Cedar Park, and Dripping Springs all establish a protected tree threshold of 8 inches DBH, substantially lower than Liberty Hill's 19-inch threshold for hardwood protection. This means that Liberty Hill permits the removal of trees in the 8–18 inch range that would be protected in peer communities. Additionally, Dripping Springs maintains a clear heritage tree definition (≥18 inches DBH or designated species) with a 3:1 replacement ratio and published mitigation fee structures. Cedar Park similarly publishes tree technical standards and replacement requirements. Austin maintains a tiered system with protected trees at 8–18 inches and heritage trees at 24 inches and above, with explicit species-based protections and board-level review authority.

Liberty Hill's 19-inch threshold, while restrictive in absolute terms (protecting large trees from clear-cutting), functionally protects fewer mid-size canopy trees than regional standards. This represents a gap between the city's stated commitment to sustainable urban forestry and its regulatory implementation.

### Root Causes of Program Limitations

The limitations of the current program are rooted in three primary factors: (1) outdated ordinance language that predates contemporary best practices and does not incorporate lessons from peer municipalities or recent research on urban forest resilience; (2) absence of a baseline tree inventory, which prevents the city from establishing quantifiable goals or measuring progress; and (3) administrative discretion in key decision-making areas (heritage tree definition, mitigation ratios, fee structures) in the absence of published standards, creating uncertainty for developers, property owners, and city staff.

## Part 2: Desired State of the Program

### Target Goals and Strategic Direction

The desired state of Liberty Hill's tree preservation program is articulated, in part, through the ongoing 2025–2026 UDC rewrite process. Theme 5 of the rewrite initiative, "Strengthen Tree/Landscaping and Water Conservation Regulations," explicitly calls for modernized tree protection standards. The UDC Diagnostic Report recommends: (1) creation of a tree removal/clearing permit system for non-development activity; (2) mandatory tree surveys and preservation plans at the plat or preliminary site plan stage; (3) clear definition and protection standards for heritage trees; (4) clarified mitigation ratios and published fee schedules; (5) extension of tree protection to single-family residential properties; and (6) requirement that replacement plantings employ native, drought-tolerant species aligned with the city's water conservation resolution.

The desired state is further informed by contemporary standards in urban forest management. Kenney et al. (2011) define the essential elements of a sustainable urban forest program as including: (1) a documented inventory of existing vegetation resources; (2) published relative canopy cover goals; (3) a prioritized species suitability list; (4) measurable goals for survival and growth; and (5) clear management and preservation standards. Applying these criteria, the desired state for Liberty Hill would include: a complete tree inventory, a published canopy cover target (e.g., 25% citywide by 2035), a Preferred Plant List emphasizing native species and ecological function, survival rate targets for planted trees (85%+ at three years), and codified protection and mitigation standards eliminating administrative discretion.

Additionally, the desired state incorporates alignment with Texas House Bill 7 (2017), which mandates that municipalities permit property owners to offset mitigation fees through on-site tree planting and explicitly protect homeowners from tree removal fees for trees below specified thresholds. The current Liberty Hill ordinance does not explicitly reference HB 7 compliance, creating legal ambiguity.

### Timeframe for Achieving Desired State

The UDC rewrite process provides a clearly defined implementation window. The rewrite is scheduled from October 2025 through October 2026 (18 months). Draft ordinance language and public hearings are anticipated during the Q1–Q2 2026 period. This timeline aligns with Kenney et al.'s (2011) recommendation that sustainable forest management programs establish 3–5 year implementation targets. A reasonable timeframe for Liberty Hill to adopt revised tree preservation standards is the end of the UDC rewrite process (October 2026), with a 24-month implementation and pilot period extending through October 2028.

## Part 3: Identified Gaps, Root Causes, and Closure Strategies

### Gap 1: No Mandatory Baseline Tree Survey (Critical Priority)

**Current State:** The ordinance does not require a comprehensive tree survey at the plat or site plan stage for development applications. Inventory requirements apply only to trees already on a site at the time of application, allowing pre-application clearing of unregulated trees.

**Desired State:** All development applications affecting sites above a defined threshold (e.g., 5,000 square feet) must submit a certified tree survey identifying all existing trees 8 inches DBH or larger at the time of preliminary plan submission.

**Root Cause:** The ordinance was drafted without provisions for a mandatory survey baseline, reflecting an era when tree inventories were conducted manually and were cost-prohibitive. The current language does not contemplate digital inventory tools (e.g., AI-native platforms) that now make comprehensive surveys feasible and cost-effective.

**Closure Strategy:** Amend § 6.07 to require that all development applications include a certified tree survey prepared using a City-approved digital tree inventory platform. Specify that surveys must be certified by a professional arborist and shall identify species, DBH, health condition, and proposed disposition for each tree. Implementation cost is low to moderate (survey tools now range from $500–$2,000 per site); no significant fiscal impact to the city itself, as survey cost is borne by the applicant. Target closure: Adoption in Q3 2026 (final ordinance); implementation by Q4 2026 for all new applications.

---

### Gap 2: Heritage Tree Threshold Undefined (Critical Priority)

**Current State:** Liberty Hill administers a Heritage Tree Permit Application process, but the specific DBH or species criteria defining what constitutes a heritage tree are not codified in the public ordinance.

**Desired State:** The UDC shall explicitly define heritage trees by DBH and/or species (e.g., "Heritage trees are trees with DBH ≥18 inches or any native species listed on the City's Preferred Plant List with DBH ≥8 inches").

**Root Cause:** Heritage tree status was added administratively through permit forms rather than being codified into the ordinance text. This creates asymmetry between the formal code and administrative practice, weakening legal defensibility and creating uncertainty for applicants.

**Closure Strategy:** Define heritage trees in amended § 6.07 using both size and species criteria. Use the Preferred Plant List (developed in parallel) to specify which species receive heritage protection at 8 inches DBH. For example, native live oak, pecan, and cedar elm would qualify as heritage trees at 8 inches; all other protected species at 18 inches. This approach aligns with Dripping Springs and Austin models. Target closure: Adoption Q3 2026; species list finalization by Q4 2026.

---

### Gap 3: Tree Mitigation Fees Not Transparently Published (Critical Priority)

**Current State:** The ordinance references mitigation calculations and fee concepts but does not publish a transparent, standardized tree mitigation fee schedule in an accessible code location or fee schedule document.

**Desired State:** The city publishes a Tree Mitigation Fee Schedule (e.g., $X per DBH-inch for standard trees; $Y per DBH-inch for heritage trees), updated annually and available on the city website and in the UDC.

**Root Cause:** Historical practice relied on case-by-case negotiation rather than transparent fee schedules. Additionally, HB 7 (2017) permits applicants to offset fees through planting credits, which requires a published fee baseline to calculate credits accurately.

**Closure Strategy:** Develop and adopt a Tree Mitigation Fee Schedule indexed to DBH, differentiated by tree class (standard vs. heritage), and explicitly incorporating HB 7 planting credit offsets (100% for homeowners, 50% for residential developers, 40% for commercial property owners). Fee schedule should be reviewed annually and adjusted for inflation or policy changes. Estimated fiscal impact: minimal (city staff time to maintain schedule). Target closure: Fee schedule finalization Q2 2026; adoption and publication Q3 2026.

---

### Gap 4: Replacement Ratios Ambiguous (Major Priority)

**Current State:** The ordinance requires an "acceptable replacement plan" but does not specify minimum replacement ratios (e.g., 1:1, 2:1, 3:1).

**Desired State:** Amended ordinance specifies ratios: 1:1 for standard protected trees (8–17.9 inches DBH); 2:1 for large protected trees (18–23.9 inches); 3:1 for heritage trees (≥24 inches or designated species). Ratios may be satisfied through on-site planting, off-site mitigation, or fee payment per HB 7.

**Root Cause:** Absence of codified ratios enabled discretionary decisions, reducing consistency and predictability. The current "acceptable plan" standard lacks objective criteria for comparison across projects.

**Closure Strategy:** Embed tiered replacement ratios in amended § 6.07. Additionally, consider adopting a points-based system (as used by Austin and Round Rock) allowing flexibility in how trees are replaced (e.g., fewer large trees vs. more small trees) while maintaining ecological equivalency. Ratios should reflect Dripping Springs and Austin precedent. Target closure: Adoption Q3 2026.

---

### Gap 5: Exempted/Preferred Species List Incomplete (Major Priority)

**Current State:** The ordinance names a few problem species (Hackberry, Mesquite, Ash Juniper/Cedar) but does not maintain a comprehensive, ecologically grounded Preferred Plant List or invasive species list.

**Desired State:** The city adopts and codifies a Preferred Plant List emphasizing native, drought-tolerant species with documented ecological function and mycorrhizal associations. The list is integrated with the existing Grow Green Guide (water conservation guidance) and referenced in all landscape and tree preservation sections of the UDC.

**Root Cause:** Historical ordinances did not incorporate ecological criteria into species selection. Additionally, the city's commitment to xeriscaping (Resolution 2023-R-001) and water conservation has evolved faster than the tree ordinance language.

**Closure Strategy:** Develop a Preferred Plant List (50–100 native and drought-tolerant species suitable for Hill Country climate) specifying DBH thresholds for heritage protection, survival expectations, and soil/mycorrhizal requirements. Coordinate with Grow Green Guide and update the list every 5 years based on research and performance data. Integration with AI-native tree inventory platforms (e.g., Arboracle STIM) allows dynamic species classification and recommendation. Target closure: List development Q1–Q2 2026; adoption Q3 2026.

---

### Gap 6: Enforcement Tools and Penalties Not Clearly Codified (Major Priority)

**Current State:** The ordinance prohibits clear-cutting and requires approvals but does not specify penalty structures (fines, stop-work orders, double-fee penalties) for violations.

**Desired State:** Amended ordinance includes a dedicated enforcement section specifying penalties by violation type: fines per tree removed without permit, per DBH-inch penalties, stop-work order authority, requirement for above-mitigation-ratio replacement in cases of willful violation, and appeals process.

**Root Cause:** Early ordinance drafts lacked enforcement language, relying instead on general municipal code authority. Absence of specific penalties weakens deterrent effect and provides little leverage to address after-the-fact violations (e.g., removal completed prior to permit denial).

**Closure Strategy:** Add enforcement section to § 6.07 specifying: (1) violation categories and associated fines (e.g., $500–$5,000 per unpermitted tree removal, scaled by DBH); (2) City authority to issue stop-work orders; (3) requirement for violators to plant additional trees at 150–200% of standard ratio; (4) escalating penalties for repeat violations; (5) appeals process. Model language can be adapted from Dripping Springs and Cedar Park ordinances. Target closure: Enforcement language adoption Q3 2026.

---

### Gap 7: No Formal, Digital-Friendly Approval Workflow (Major Priority)

**Current State:** The ordinance references Planning and Zoning Commission review and City Administrator approval but does not codify stepwise workflows, required documents, timelines, or decision criteria. Approvals rely on internal staff knowledge and discretion.

**Desired State:** Amended ordinance includes a clearly defined tree review and approval process: administrative pre-review (staff completeness check), applicant survey and mitigation plan submission, P&Z review (for heritage tree removals or variance requests), decision with published rationale, and inspection/compliance verification. Workflows are compatible with digital platforms for intake, tracking, and decision documentation.

**Root Cause:** Ordinance language was drafted before digital workflow tools existed. Current practice relies on institutional knowledge and informal guidance, reducing transparency and making it difficult to scale processes or audit decisions for consistency.

**Closure Strategy:** Codify tree review workflows in amended § 6.07 or add a new subsection. Specify: (1) triggers for administrative vs. P&Z review (e.g., heritage trees require P&Z); (2) required documents (certified survey, mitigation plan, arborist certification); (3) review timelines (e.g., 15 days for administrative review, 30 days for P&Z); (4) decision criteria (consistency with Preferred Plant List, replacement ratio achievement, HB 7 compliance); (5) appeals pathway. Design language to reference "digital tree inventory platform" or similar to allow integration with tools like Arboracle STIM without vendor lock-in. Target closure: Workflow language adoption Q3 2026; system implementation by Q4 2026.

---

### Gap 8: HB 7 (2017) Compliance Not Explicitly Codified (Major Priority)

**Current State:** The ordinance does not explicitly reference or incorporate Texas House Bill 7 (2017) requirements for planting credits and homeowner protections.

**Desired State:** Amended ordinance includes a dedicated HB 7 compliance section specifying: (1) homeowners can satisfy mitigation 100% through on-site planting; (2) residential developers can offset 50% of fees via planting; (3) commercial owners can offset 40% via planting; (4) homeowners are exempt from mitigation fees for trees smaller than specified threshold (per HB 7 prohibition on homeowner removal fees); (5) administrative process for documenting planting credits.

**Root Cause:** HB 7 was enacted in 2017; Liberty Hill's ordinance predates modernization efforts and does not explicitly codify state law requirements. Absence of explicit language creates legal ambiguity and reduces incentive for applicants to choose on-site planting over fee payment.

**Closure Strategy:** Add a new subsection (e.g., § 6.07.3) to amended ordinance explicitly addressing HB 7 compliance. Specify credit percentages, documentation requirements, and process for tracking planting fulfillment. Coordinate with Tree Mitigation Fee Schedule to ensure fee structure aligns with state law. Legal review recommended to ensure full compliance. Target closure: Adoption Q3 2026.

## Synthesis and Critical Analysis

The eight gaps identified in Liberty Hill's tree preservation program form a cohesive pattern: **the current ordinance establishes philosophical protection (clear-cutting prohibition) without providing the operational infrastructure necessary to implement or measure that protection**. The absence of a baseline inventory, defined thresholds, published fees, codified workflows, and explicit state law compliance creates a system that is administratively discretionary and operationally opaque. This pattern aligns with Staudhammer (2018), who documents how fragmented, reactive urban forest management approaches create a "human legacy" of canopy loss that becomes increasingly difficult and expensive to remedy as cities grow and development accelerates.

The 2025–2026 UDC rewrite presents a clear window of opportunity to address all eight gaps simultaneously, shifting Liberty Hill from a reactive, discretionary system to a proactive, transparent, data-driven program. By the end of Q3 2026, Liberty Hill can align its tree preservation ordinance with regional best practices and state law. Implementation and performance verification can occur through Q4 2026 and beyond, with measurable outcomes visible by 2028.

The closure of these gaps will position Liberty Hill not merely as a municipality with tree protection rules, but as a leader in regenerative, transparent, and sustainable urban forestry management—consistent with its existing commitments to Dark Sky certification, water conservation, and comprehensive planning.

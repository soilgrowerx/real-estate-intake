## The Situation (One Page)

Liberty Hill maintains a **tree protection framework that is conceptually sound but administratively incomplete**. The city's current § 9A-6.07 protects hardwood trees ≥19 inches in diameter from clear-cutting and administers a heritage tree permit program, yet lacks:

- **Published fee schedules** (makes financial impact unpredictable)
- **Defined heritage tree thresholds** (creates administrative uncertainty)
- **Mandatory baseline inventories** (allows pre-application clearing)
- **Codified replacement ratios** (results in inconsistent outcomes)
- **Explicit HB 7 (2017) compliance language** (legal ambiguity)

**Result:** Liberty Hill lags regional peers—Round Rock, Cedar Park, and Dripping Springs all protect 8-inch diameter trees and maintain transparent, standardized processes. Liberty Hill's 19-inch threshold protects fewer trees and creates friction in the development review process.

**The Opportunity:** The current UDC rewrite (Oct 2025 – Oct 2026) explicitly identifies tree preservation as **Theme 5 Priority**, with the diagnostic report calling for:
- Mandatory tree surveys at plat stage
- Clear mitigation ratios and fee structures
- Heritage tree definition
- Single-family property protection
- Native species requirement

**This window closes in October 2026.** The time to act is now—Q1 2026 (draft phase).

## Comparative Context: How Liberty Hill Ranks

### Protected Tree Thresholds

| City | Standard Threshold | Heritage Threshold | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|-------|
| **Liberty Hill** | 19" | Undefined | Most restrictive; protects fewer mid-size trees |
| Round Rock | 8" | Species-specific | More protective; transparent taxonomy |
| Cedar Park | 8" | Not specified | Aligns with Hill Country standard |
| Dripping Springs | 8" | 18" | Clear two-tier system |
| Austin | 8"–18" | 24" | Most granular; board review layer |

**Implication:** Liberty Hill's 19-inch threshold means trees in the 8–18" range—often the most valuable for canopy continuity and succession—are **unprotected**. This is the opposite of regional best practice.

### Process Transparency

- **Liberty Hill:** No published fees, ratios, or timeline standards → High uncertainty for applicants
- **Peer Cities:** Published technical manuals, fee schedules, online guidance → Predictable process
- **Winner:** Peers (by design consistency and developer predictability)

### Heritage Tree Programs

- **Liberty Hill:** Program exists; threshold unknown; process unclear
- **Dripping Springs:** Clear definition; 3:1 replacement; codified process
- **Austin:** Species-based tiers; board review; published standards

## Strategic Recommendations (Ranked by Priority)

### 🔴 Immediate (Q1 2026 – Before Draft Phase)

**1. Adopt Mandatory Tree Survey Requirement**
- All development >5,000 SF must submit certified tree survey at plat/site plan stage
- Survey shall use AI-native platform (e.g., Arboracle STIM), certified by professional arborist
- Outcome: Closes the "cut first, permit later" loophole

**2. Define Heritage Tree Thresholds**
- Heritage trees = ≥18" DBH AND/OR designated native species (live oak, pecan, cedar elm ≥8" DBH)
- Codify definition directly in UDC § 6.07
- Outcome: Removes administrative ambiguity; enables consistent enforcement

**3. Publish Transparent Mitigation Fee Schedule**
- Establish per-DBH-inch fees; differentiate standard vs. heritage
- Explicitly incorporate HB 7 planting credits (homeowner 100%, developer 50%, commercial 40%)
- Outcome: Predictability; compliance with state law

### 🟠 Near-Term (Q2 2026 – During Draft/Hearing Phase)

**4. Standardize Replacement Ratios**
- Standard protected trees (8"–17.9"): 1:1 ratio
- Large protected trees (18"–23.9"): 2:1 ratio
- Heritage trees (≥24" or designated species): 3:1 ratio
- Allow points-based flexibility tied to species, health, and successional value
- Outcome: Consistency across projects; quantifiable compliance

**5. Create Preferred Plant & Exempt Species List**
- Integrate with existing Grow Green Guide and xeriscaping requirements
- Emphasize native, drought-tolerant species with mycorrhizal associations
- Outcome: Aligns with Dark Sky, water conservation, and regenerative forestry goals

**6. Formalize Approval Workflow**
- Codify triggers for administrative vs. P&Z approval
- Define submittal requirements, timelines, and decision criteria
- Design for digital integration (Arboracle STIM workflow automation)
- Outcome: Reduced applicant friction; increased staff efficiency

### 🟡 Medium-Term (Post-Adoption, 2027+)

**7. Establish Enforcement & Penalty Framework**
- Create penalty matrix (per tree, per inch DBH, per offense)
- Include administrative tools: stop-work orders, double-fee penalties
- Outcome: Meaningful deterrent; consistent enforcement

**8. Integrate Cluster Development Incentives**
- Tie tree preservation credits to cluster development approvals
- Enable on-site retention over off-site mitigation for qualified projects
- Outcome: Supports canopy continuity; advances Dendritic Forest Management philosophy

## Why This Matters Now

1. **The UDC rewrite window closes October 2026.** After that date, any changes require a separate amendment process (slower, more expensive).

2. **Liberty Hill is already forward-thinking** (Dark Sky certification July 2025; downtown master plan; comprehensive planning). Tree modernization aligns with existing city direction.

3. **Regional peers have already moved.** Round Rock, Cedar Park, and Dripping Springs have transparent, 8-inch thresholds. Liberty Hill can leapfrog by adopting **AI-native inventory standards**—a first for Hill Country municipalities.

4. **Developer predictability = faster approvals.** Clear rules and published fees reduce negotiation time and legal risk.

5. **Canopy resilience = climate adaptation.** Mandatory surveys, strategic replacement, and species diversity strengthen long-term forest health.

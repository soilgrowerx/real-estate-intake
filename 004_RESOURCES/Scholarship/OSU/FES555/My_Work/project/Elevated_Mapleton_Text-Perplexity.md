## 1. Management → Responsibilities

The Mapleton Tree Board sets direction and reflects community values, but operational authority and liability have to be unambiguous.

- **Risk and removals:** A designated City Arborist or Urban Forestry Coordinator has final authority on hazard assessments and removals in the public right‑of‑way and on city property. This role holds the city’s duty of care and signs off on decisions that materially affect public safety, infrastructure, and legal exposure.
- **Data and inventory stewardship:** The Urban Forestry division is the steward of the city’s living tree inventory, the operational “Digital Twin” of Mapleton’s canopy. This includes maintaining current spatial data, standardizing attributes, and ensuring that management decisions draw from recent inspections rather than decades‑old records.
- **Policy development and adoption:** The Tree Board, advised by technical staff and consultants, develops urban forestry policy recommendations and ordinance updates. The City Manager and City Council are responsible for adopting those recommendations, funding implementation, and integrating them with broader city plans and regulations.
- **Coordination with other departments:** Public Works, Planning, Parks, and Emergency Management each have defined responsibilities for trees within their scope (e.g., utilities, development review, park operations, storm response), but all tree‑related actions are coordinated through the Urban Forestry division to keep risk, data, and standards aligned.

This division of responsibility keeps risk management and technical judgment in the hands of qualified practitioners, while policy direction and funding decisions remain with elected officials and the community.

## 2. Management → Plans, Policies, Regulations

### Public Safety and Tree Risk Management Program

Mapleton will manage public trees as core infrastructure, not amenities. Large, aging trees in streetscapes, near parks, and around critical facilities present both high value and high risk; ignoring them is a direct threat to public safety and a foreseeable liability.

- All formal risk assessments on public trees will be performed or overseen by ISA TRAQ‑qualified personnel using ISA Best Management Practices and ANSI A300 standards.
- Risk ratings will drive maintenance cycles, removal decisions, and budget requests, and will be documented in the tree inventory so that the city can demonstrate a reasonable, systematic duty of care if an incident occurs.
- When a conflict arises between aesthetics and structural integrity, Mapleton will prioritize risk reduction and public safety, while committing to replanting and restoration that maintain long‑term canopy goals.


### Tree Protection Ordinances (TPOs) and Development Mitigation

Mature trees are not interchangeable with saplings; a single large canopy tree often provides the stormwater, shade, and carbon benefits of dozens of new plantings. Mapleton’s ordinances will reflect that reality in how they protect trees and price removals.

- The city will codify Critical Root Zone (CRZ) protections for qualifying public and regulated private trees, using a baseline of 1 foot of CRZ radius per inch of DBH, refined by site and species where appropriate.
- All public and private development projects affecting regulated trees must comply with ANSI A300 Part 5 (Management of Trees During Site Planning, Construction, and Development), including pre‑construction assessment, protection fencing, and prohibited activities within the CRZ.
- Where removal of a regulated tree is unavoidable, mitigation will be based on a valuation method (such as CTLA or similar accepted approaches) that accounts for lost ecological function and asset value, rather than a simple 1‑for‑1 stem replacement.
- Mitigation payments will be directed into an urban forestry fund that supports planting, establishment care, and long‑term maintenance, linking development impacts directly to reinvestment in the community forest.


### Sustainability, Diversity, and Species Selection

Mapleton’s historic reliance on maple (*Acer*) has created a brittle canopy that is vulnerable to pests, storms, and climate stress. To build a resilient, dendritic canopy, the city will manage toward explicit diversity targets and site‑appropriate species choices.

- Mapleton will use the 10–20–30 guideline as a long‑term target: no more than 10% of any single species, 20% of any genus, and 30% of any family in the managed public tree population.
- Species selection will prioritize structural soundness, expected service life, pest and disease resistance, climate adaptability, and soil volume needs, rather than short‑term aesthetics alone.
- In response to the current over‑representation of *Acer* and recent pest concerns, the city will temporarily pause planting of highly over‑represented or high‑risk genera (e.g., a near‑term moratorium on additional *Prunus* street trees) while diversifying the palette.
- Recommended species lists and planting standards in the appendices will be updated at least every five years based on monitoring data, inventory trends, and emerging regional pest and climate information.

Together, these policies move Mapleton away from reactive, case‑by‑case decisions and toward a clear, long‑term management framework grounded in risk, resilience, and measurable outcomes.

## 3. Management → Issues and Trends

Mapleton’s community forest shows several structural weaknesses that must shape how the city manages risk and invests over the next five years.

- **Monoculture and age structure risk:** The 1997 inventory and subsequent observations show a heavy concentration of aging maples in key streetscapes and parks. This leaves the city exposed to a single‑pest scenario or storm event that could remove a large share of the canopy in a short period, with cascading effects on shade, stormwater, and public safety.
- **Reactive maintenance and liability:** Recent incidents, including storm damage and limb failures near public areas, highlight that Mapleton’s default approach has been reactive rather than planned. Without a documented risk program and prioritized work list, the city’s ability to demonstrate reasonable care is limited.
- **Data and infrastructure gaps:** The existing tree inventory is decades old and incomplete, making it difficult to answer basic questions such as “Where are our highest‑risk trees?” or “Which neighborhoods lack canopy?” A modern, mobile, GIS‑enabled inventory is needed to serve as the operational “Digital Twin” of the community forest.
- **Uneven canopy and equity:** As the city grows and diversifies economically, canopy cover and tree condition are unlikely to be evenly distributed without deliberate planning. Under‑served neighborhoods and high‑heat areas may have fewer, smaller, or poorer‑condition trees, increasing vulnerability during extreme weather.

These trends justify the plan’s emphasis on a formal risk management program, clear diversity targets, modern inventory tools, and explicit links between tree management, public safety, and community equity.

## 3.5 Management Practices

Mapleton’s management practices are built around a simple idea: use current data and accepted standards to decide what work gets done, when it gets done, and how it gets done in the field. Instead of reacting after trees fail, the city will follow a risk‑based, inventory‑driven approach that treats the community forest as long‑lived infrastructure.

- **Risk‑based maintenance cycles:** Routine inspections and risk ratings will determine how often trees are pruned, monitored, or scheduled for removal. Higher‑risk zones, such as school routes, busy streets, and parks, will move on shorter inspection and pruning cycles than low‑use areas. When a tree’s risk rating crosses a defined threshold, it is either mitigated (through pruning, cabling, or site modification) or removed and replaced. This keeps limited staff time and budget focused where failure would have the greatest impact on people and property.

- **Data‑informed planting and removals:** Planting and removal decisions will be guided by the inventory and by the plan’s diversity and equity targets, not just by short‑term requests. When a tree is removed, staff will look at neighborhood canopy gaps, species composition, and available soil volume before choosing whether, what, and where to replant. Over‑represented genera will be phased down over time, while under‑used, climate‑appropriate species are phased in. This approach slowly shifts Mapleton away from its current maple‑heavy structure toward a more balanced and resilient canopy.

- **Standards of care:** All pruning, removals, planting, and construction‑related tree protection will follow ANSI A300 standards and ISA Best Management Practices, with worker safety aligned with ANSI Z133 and relevant state regulations. Planting stock will meet ANSI Z60.1 nursery standards, and all city tree work will either be performed or overseen by an ISA Certified Arborist. Writing these standards into contracts, specifications, and ordinances ensures that every crew—internal or external—is operating at the same professional baseline.

- **Adaptive management:** Monitoring results will be used to adjust these practices over time. If metrics show that certain pruning cycles are not reducing risk incidents, or that diversity targets are not being met, staff and the Tree Board will revisit priorities, timelines, and budget allocations. New pests, extreme weather events, or development patterns may also trigger targeted updates to the management playbook. In this way, Management, Monitoring, and Implementation stay linked, and the plan remains a living document instead of a static report.

## 4. Monitoring Plan → Metrics

A management plan is only useful if it can be tested against reality and adjusted over time. Mapleton’s monitoring framework will follow a simple cycle—plan, implement, monitor, evaluate, and adjust—and will rely on the living inventory as its primary data backbone.

**Core indicators**

At minimum, Mapleton will track the following indicators, with data stored in and reported from the tree inventory system:

- **Inventory completeness and update rate:** Percentage of public trees with current records, and the average time between field inspections for each asset class.
- **Diversity metrics:** Proportion of the population by species, genus, and family, tracked against the 10–20–30 guideline to monitor progress away from monoculture.
- **Risk mitigation performance:** Number and percentage of high‑risk trees (by defined risk rating) mitigated or removed each year, and associated changes in risk‑related incidents, near‑misses, or claims.
- **Ecosystem services indicators:** Modeled annual benefits such as carbon sequestration, stormwater interception, and air pollutant removal for the public tree population, using accepted tools that draw on the inventory data.
- **Community and equity indicators:** Measures such as canopy cover by neighborhood, participation in planting and stewardship events, and survey responses about tree benefits and concerns.

These metrics will be tied directly to the plan’s Goals and Objectives, so that each major action has one or more indicators to show whether it is working. If diversity metrics stall, risk incidents increase, or inspection cycles slip, the Tree Board and City staff will adjust priorities, budgets, and practices rather than waiting for the next planning cycle.

## 5. Appendix E → Management Plans (excerpt)

### Construction Impact and CRZ Protection Protocol

Development and utility work can silently destroy tree roots and destabilize otherwise healthy trees. Mapleton will use the following protocol for all regulated trees affected by construction:[^4][^1]

1. **Pre‑construction assessment:** Before any excavation or grading begins, a qualified arborist will identify regulated trees, determine their DBH, and map the Critical Root Zone (CRZ) using a radius of at least 1 foot per inch of DBH, adjusted for site conditions and species.[^1][^4]
2. **Physical protection:** The CRZ will be enclosed with rigid, high‑visibility fencing anchored in place for the duration of the project. Inside this zone, contractors may not store materials, park vehicles, operate heavy equipment, or discharge wash‑out or chemicals.[^1]
3. **Activity restrictions:** Trenching, grade changes, and utility installation will be routed outside the CRZ wherever feasible. If root cutting is unavoidable, it must be done under arborist supervision, with clean cuts and immediate follow‑up care.[^1]
4. **Enforcement and mitigation:** Violations that result in significant root damage, instability, or decline will trigger penalties linked to the tree’s appraised value using an accepted valuation method. In addition to fines, the responsible party will fund post‑construction monitoring and any required mitigation or replacement.[^4][^1]
5. **Post‑construction monitoring:** Trees subjected to construction impacts will be inspected periodically for at least three growing seasons for signs of decline, instability, or dieback, with findings recorded in the inventory and used to inform future standards.[^1]

This protocol gives contractors clear expectations, gives staff clear authority, and protects the city’s long‑term investment in mature canopy.

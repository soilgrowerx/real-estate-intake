### 1. Management → Responsibilities

The Mapleton Tree Board drives the vision and translates public intent into urban forestry policy. But when it comes to execution and liability, authority has to be clear.

*   **Risk and Removals:** The City Arborist (or designated Urban Forestry Coordinator) has the final say on hazard tree assessments and removals. They hold the legal duty of care and act as the city's shield against liability.
*   **Data Sovereignty:** The Urban Forestry division owns and maintains the city’s tree inventory—the "Digital Twin" of the canopy. This ensures operational decisions rely on real-time spatial data, not ten-year-old paper records.
*   **Policy Adaptation:** The Tree Board recommends policy changes. The City Manager and City Council hold ultimate accountability for adopting those changes and allocating the budget. This setup separates risk management (handled by certified technicians) from community values (handled by public representatives).

### 2. Management → Plans, Policies, Regulations

**Public Safety and Tree Risk Management Program**
Mapleton will manage its trees as critical municipal infrastructure. Ignoring large, aging trees near roadways and pedestrian zones creates an unacceptable liability. TRAQ-qualified personnel will conduct all risk assessments according to ISA Best Management Practices. When conflicts arise, we prioritize structural integrity and public safety over temporary aesthetics.

**Tree Protection Ordinances (TPOs) and Development Mitigation**
The city will enforce strict Critical Root Zone (CRZ) boundaries to protect existing mature canopy from construction impacts. A mature tree provides exponentially more ecosystem value than a newly planted sapling. All development projects must follow ANSI A300 (Part 5) standards for managing trees during site planning. If removal is unavoidable, mitigation fees will be based on the lost ecological volume and asset value (using CTLA or CAVAT methods), not just a 1-to-1 stem replacement.

**Sustainability, Diversity, and Species Selection**
Mapleton will break its historical over-reliance on maple (*Acer*). Moving forward, we enforce the 10-20-30 rule: no more than 10% of any species, 20% of any genus, and 30% of any family. This is how we build a resilient, dendritic canopy that can survive the next major pest outbreak or climate shift without collapsing.

### 3. Management → Issues and Trends

Mapleton’s urban forest is brittle. Decades of repetitive planting have created a severe monoculture risk. Our heavy reliance on aging maples means a single pest or severe storm could wipe out a massive percentage of the canopy at once. This is a public safety liability. As these mature trees fail, the city's duty of care requires us to act proactively, rather than just cleaning up after failures.

The data gap is just as dangerous. Relying on a static, decade-old inventory means we are flying blind. Mapleton needs to move to a modern, GIS-enabled infrastructure. Building a mobile 'Digital Twin' of the urban forest lets us track ecosystem services, calculate stormwater runoff capture, and find equity gaps in canopy coverage. We have to stop treating trees as landscaping and start managing them as a living utility.

### 4. Monitoring Plan → Metrics

A plan without a feedback loop is just a wish list. Mapleton’s monitoring framework operates on a continuous cycle: plan, implement, monitor, evaluate, and adjust. We track success through our living inventory system, measuring outcomes instead of just counting stems.

**Core Indicators:**
*   **Inventory Fidelity:** How often the spatial database is updated.
*   **Diversity Indices:** How close we are to hitting the 10-20-30 resilience target.
*   **Risk Mitigation ROI:** The number of high-risk trees pruned or removed compared to the reduction in public safety incidents and liability claims.
*   **Ecosystem Service Output:** The annual carbon sequestration, stormwater retention, and air quality improvements we can calculate to justify our budget requests.

These metrics drive the work. If diversity stalls or risk incidents go up, the Tree Board and City Arborist will change the operational playbook and reallocate resources.

### 5. Appendix E → Management Plans

**Construction Impact & CRZ Defense Protocol**
1.  **Pre-Construction Assessment:** Before any excavation starts, crews must calculate the Critical Root Zone (CRZ)—1 foot of radius per 1 inch of DBH—and fence it off with rigid, high-visibility barriers.
2.  **Soil Protection:** Contractors cannot store materials, park heavy machinery, or wash out chemicals inside the CRZ. Soil compaction and root asphyxiation kill trees just as surely as chainsaws do.
3.  **Mitigation Penalties:** Any violation of the CRZ that causes structural root damage triggers an immediate fine. The fine is tied to the tree’s calculated asset value (CTLA), and the contractor must pay for mandatory post-construction monitoring to watch for canopy dieback.

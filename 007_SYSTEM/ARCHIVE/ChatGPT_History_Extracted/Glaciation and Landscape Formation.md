In a cross-sectional view of a glacier, the ice flows internally (ductile deformation) in the lower part of the glacier where stresses exceed 100 kilopascals. This is because the pressure and stress at greater depths make the ice more plastic and capable of flowing.

Glacial ice is formed by recrystallized snow. Over time, accumulated snow compresses and the air is expelled, leading to the formation of dense, ice crystals that make up a glacier.

**ASSISTANT**:
Isostasy relates to glaciation in that the weight of ice depresses the crust, and after melting, the crust rebounds and rises. This is a response to the removal of the ice load and is known as isostatic rebound.

When a glacier &quot;advances,&quot; this means the terminus of the glacier is moving forward; the glacier is growing. This occurs when the glacier accumulates more snow in the accumulation zone than is lost in the ablation zone, resulting in the glacier&#x27;s forward movement.

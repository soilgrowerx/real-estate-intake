**ASSISTANT**:
The terms &quot;skills&quot; and &quot;abilities&quot; are often used interchangeably, but they have subtle differences. Skills are generally considered to be learned and acquired through training and experience. They are specific, teachable, and measurable. Abilities, on the other hand, are innate or developed capacities to perform tasks. They&#x27;re broader and less easy to quantify than skills.

As for the idea of retiring the phrases &quot;hard skills&quot; and &quot;soft skills,&quot; your alternative of &quot;natural skills&quot; and &quot;built skills&quot; introduces an interesting perspective. &quot;Natural skills&quot; could refer to innate talents or predispositions, essentially abilities that come &#x27;naturally&#x27;. &quot;Built skills&quot; would then represent skills honed through training, experience, and deliberate practice.

The benefit of this new terminology is that it acknowledges the fluidity and dynamism in the development of skills. However, it also blurs the line between what can be developed and what is innate, which may or may not be advantageous depending on the context.

In a world that constantly evolves, your terms could be more adaptive and less rigid, giving people room to grow and adapt. Just like water can both crash and flow, these terms acknowledge the multifaceted nature of human capabilities.

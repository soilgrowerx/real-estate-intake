**How did crossing over affect the genetic content in the gametes?**

- **Recombinant Chromosomes**: As a result of crossing over, the chromosomes in the gametes are not exact copies of those in the parent cell. Instead, they are recombinant chromosomes with a mix of alleles from both homologous chromosomes.

**ASSISTANT**:
**3. Two Differences Between Meiosis I and Meiosis II:**

1. **Separation of Chromosomes:**
   - **Meiosis I:** Homologous chromosomes are separated. This reduces the chromosome number by half and results in two haploid cells.
   - **Meiosis II:** Sister chromatids are separated. This does not change the chromosome number but produces four genetically unique haploid cells from the two haploid cells formed in Meiosis I.

2. **Genetic Recombination:**
   - **Meiosis I:** Crossing over occurs during Prophase I, which leads to genetic recombination and increased genetic diversity.
   - **Meiosis II:** No crossing over occurs, as this phase is similar to mitosis, focusing on the separation of sister chromatids.

**4. Severity of Nondisjunction in Meiosis I vs. Meiosis II:**

Nondisjunction is the failure of chromosomes to separate properly during cell division. 

- **Nondisjunction in Meiosis I:** All resulting gametes are affected. Two gametes will have an extra chromosome (n+1), and two will be missing a chromosome (n-1). This is generally more severe because it affects all gametes produced.

- **Nondisjunction in Meiosis II:** Only half of the resulting gametes are affected. One gamete will have an extra chromosome (n+1), one will be missing a chromosome (n-1), and two will have the normal chromosome number (n). This is less severe compared to nondisjunction in Meiosis I because it affects fewer gametes.

**5. Necessity to Reduce the Number of Chromosomes in the Formation of Gametes:**

- **Reduction in Gametes:** It is necessary to reduce the number of chromosomes in the formation of gametes to maintain a constant chromosome number across generations. During fertilization, two gametes (each haploid, n) combine to form a diploid (2n) zygote. If gametes were not haploid, the chromosome number would double with each generation, leading to genetic instability.
  
- **Not in Somatic Cells:** In somatic (body) cells, the chromosome number is maintained at the diploid level (2n) because these cells are involved in growth, repair, and normal cellular functions, rather than reproduction. Somatic cells undergo mitosis, which preserves the chromosome number.

**6. Chromosome Numbers in Blue Whales:**

Blue whales have 44 chromosomes in each diploid cell (2n = 44).

a. **Daughter Cells from Mitosis:**
   - Each daughter cell from mitosis will have 44 chromosomes. Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid cells.

b. **Daughter Cells from Meiosis:**
   - Each daughter cell from meiosis will have 22 chromosomes. Meiosis produces four genetically unique haploid cells, each with half the number of chromosomes of the original diploid cell.

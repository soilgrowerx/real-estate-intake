**ASSISTANT**:
In a hypertonic solution, such as the 12.5% salt solution, blood cells will exhibit crenation, which means they shrink as water moves out of the cells to the surrounding solution where the solute concentration is higher. You would expect the cells to appear smaller and somewhat spiky.

In an isotonic solution, like the 0.9% salt solution, which is close to the salt concentration of blood, the red blood cells maintain their normal shape and size. This solution does not create a gradient for water movement, so cells retain their biconcave disc shape.

In a hypotonic solution, such as distilled water, blood cells will swell and possibly lyse (burst) due to osmosis. Water moves into the cells where the concentration of solutes is higher. Here, you&#x27;d observe the cells to be more swollen, and they may eventually burst, appearing as ghost cells (clear cells with no visible interior structures).

The appearance of blood cells changes in each solution due to osmosis, the passive movement of water across a semi-permeable membrane from an area of lower solute concentration to an area of higher solute concentration to equalize solute concentrations on both sides of the membrane.

Water moves passively in and out of these cells; it does not require energy from the cell (active transport) because it is moving down its concentration gradient.

Intravenous (IV) solutions must be isotonic to blood to prevent the cells from shrinking (in a hypertonic solution) or swelling and bursting (in a hypotonic solution). An isotonic IV solution ensures that the blood cells maintain their shape and functionality, which is crucial for carrying oxygen and performing other cellular activities. If IV solutions were not isotonic, they could cause harm to the patient by disrupting the osmotic balance of the blood cells.

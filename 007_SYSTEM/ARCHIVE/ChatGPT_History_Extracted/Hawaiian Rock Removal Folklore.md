In Hawaiian culture, there is a well-known belief tied to not removing volcanic rocks, sand, or other natural elements from the islands. This is deeply rooted in respect for the land, or *ʻāina*, which is considered sacred. Hawaiian folklore holds that taking rocks or other natural objects from Hawaii could anger Pele, the goddess of volcanoes and fire. As a result, people who take these items may experience bad luck, misfortune, or illness. Many tourists who have taken rocks or sand from Hawaii have claimed to experience such misfortune, and there are numerous stories of individuals mailing the rocks back to Hawaii in an attempt to reverse the bad luck.

Pele&#x27;s role in this belief is important—she embodies the volcanic energy of the islands, and her domain is the land itself. Taking something from her realm without permission is seen as a disruption to the natural balance, which is why it is believed to result in negative consequences.

Traditionally, offerings like flowers, food, or *ʻawa* (kava) can be left at a site dedicated to her, such as a volcanic area or the ocean. You can also do this in spirit, if you are not in Hawaii, with the intention of restoring harmony and showing respect.

2. **Return to the Islands (if possible)**: If you are able to return to Hawaii, you could make an offering in person. 3. **Reach out to a Hawaiian Cultural Practitioner**: Some Hawaiian cultural practitioners specialize in resolving issues around taking rocks or other natural elements. They may offer guidance or perform a ceremony on your behalf to make things right with Pele and the land.

By giving back to the *ʻāina* in this way, you symbolically restore balance and respect.

5. **Acknowledge and Educate**: You can also make things right by acknowledging the importance of these cultural practices and sharing the knowledge with others. By spreading awareness, you help protect Hawaii’s natural resources and show respect for its cultural traditions.

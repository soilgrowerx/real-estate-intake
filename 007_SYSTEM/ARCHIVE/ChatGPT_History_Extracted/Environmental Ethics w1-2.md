### Summary of the Environmental Ethics Chapter

The chapter on environmental ethics delves into the development and significance of environmental ethics as a discipline within philosophy. Environmental ethics extends traditional ethical concerns, which are primarily human-centered, to include the natural world—animals, plants, species, ecosystems, and Earth itself. It begins by questioning whether humans are the only agents of moral concern and explores various schools of thought that argue for a broader ethical scope.

1. **Human-Centered Ethics**: Classical ethics is traditionally anthropocentric, concerned mainly with justice and love between people. However, environmental ethics challenges this by suggesting that non-human entities and ecosystems also deserve moral consideration.

2. **The Environmental Turn**: The rise of environmental philosophy in the mid-20th century marked a shift in the understanding of ethics, prompted by increasing awareness of environmental degradation. This shift emphasized that the Earth and its ecosystems possess intrinsic value, beyond their utility to humans.

3. **Human Responsibilities**: While humans are seen as the only reflective moral agents, environmental ethics asserts that humans have duties not only to each other but also to non-human entities. This includes recognizing the moral value of animals, plants, and ecosystems.

4. **Animals and Sentient Beings**: The chapter discusses animal welfare and rights, highlighting that animals are capable of valuing their own lives, which grants them moral standing. This leads to a moral obligation to protect their well-being, although not all life forms are sentient or capable of self-awareness.

5. **Biocentrism and Ecocentrism**: These perspectives extend ethical concern to all living organisms and ecosystems, arguing for respect toward life as a whole. The concept of biocentrism holds that all life forms have intrinsic value, while ecocentrism includes ecosystems and natural processes as part of the ethical framework.

6. **Land Ethic and Ecosystem Health**: Building on Aldo Leopold&#x27;s land ethic, the chapter stresses that ethical considerations should include entire ecosystems and the land, recognizing their integral role in supporting life. It also discusses the idea of ecojustice, which links environmental sustainability to social justice and equitable distribution of resources.

7. **Global Perspective and Sustainability**: The chapter concludes by addressing global environmental ethics, emphasizing the need for sustainable development that balances human needs with the health of the planet. It argues that future development must prioritize ecological balance and fairness across nations and generations.

4. **What is biocentrism, and how does it differ from anthropocentrism?**
   - Biocentrism is the view that all living organisms have intrinsic value and deserve moral consideration, while anthropocentrism holds that only humans are morally significant.

   - Animal rights ethics argue that animals have inherent rights that must be respected, while animal welfare ethics focus on ensuring animals are treated humanely and not subjected to unnecessary suffering.

What is Ethics?
Ethics, sometimes called moral philosophy, “involves systematizing, defending, and recommending concepts of right and wrong behavior” (Fieser “Ethics”). Ethics is a broad category, and incorporates the study of many different issues and ideas. In this course, you will focus on environmental ethics. Environmental ethics may, according to Rolston, be defined as “theory and practice about appropriate concern for, values in, and duties regarding the natural world” (1).

Traditional Theories of Ethics
There are three basic divisions within the field of ethics. These are metaethics, normative ethics, and applied ethics:

Metaethics studies the meaning and origin of ethical principles.
Normative ethics seeks to define ethical standards of moral behavior.
Applied ethics examines controversial issues or those with many different possible ethical interpretations.
Most studies and philosophical discussions of ethics can fit into one of these three divisions.

Metaethics is the least clear-cut of these; it deals with metaphysical issues and questions, like “Is there a God?,” as well as psychological issues and questions, like “What is human nature?” Issues of psychological gender morality, conflicts between emotion and reason, and the differences between objectivism and relativism all are essential to the study of metaethics. Metaethics is the least relevant to this course, but you should be aware that it exists as a field of study.

Virtue theories or ethics is focused on the importance of good habits and good character. Examples of virtues include courage, wisdom, justice and temperance. Christian theorists add other virtues, like faith, hope and charity. Embracing virtue and avoiding vices, like gluttony or sloth, is the key, for these theorists, to living an ethical life.

Duty theories are based on principles of obligation. These suggest that these obligations are essential, even when the end result of that obligation worsens conditions for the individual. There are several basic types of duty theories, each with distinct ideas about duty.

Prima Facie Duty
A more recent philosopher W.D. Ross emphasized prima facie duty, or duties that are accepted as correct unless proven otherwise (Fieser “Ethics”). For Ross, there is a relatively short list of duties that guide ethical behavior; these include:

The duty to keep promises or fidelity.
The duty to increase our own intelligence or virtue; self-improvement.
The duty to help others or beneficence.
The duty to recognize and acknowledge merit, or justice.
The duty not to harm others; nonmaleficence.
The duty to compensate others if they are harmed, or reparations.
The duty to give thanks to those who offer assistance, or gratitude.
Ross addressed times and situations in which two of these duties could come into conflict with one another. In this case, according to Ross, it would become quite clear which of the duties was relevant and important, and which one should be deemed a prima facie duty, rather than an actual duty. For instance, if you promise to help change your neighbor’s tire, this is a duty of fidelity. If your neighbor is clearly drunk, repairing his car will result in a negative outcome; you have a responsibility not to do harm. In this case, the most ethical choice is to focus on nonmaleficence and prevent harm. You can keep your promise at a later date when your neighbor is sober.

The first, defined by the seventeenth-century German philosopher Samuel Pufendorf, divides duties into duties to God, toward oneself, and toward others. This is the most straightforward interpretation of duty theory. The philosopher Immanuel Kant built on this basic idea of duty theory by developing the idea of the categorical imperative. The categorical imperative is a basic rule that can apply to nearly any situation or environment and is sometimes considered a third type of duty theory. One of the clearest categorical imperatives suggested by Kant is, “Treat people as an end, and never as a means to an end” (Fieser “Ethics”).

Another example of a duty theory, called rights theory, is likely familiar to you. Rights theory, or the idea that people are naturally entitled to certain basic rights, and that these cannot be ignored outside of extreme circumstances, is foundational to the basic structure and ideas of the United States’ Constitution; specifically, life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness. You might have heard that your rights end where mine begin. Rights theory is closely connected to duty theory, and has its origins in Enlightenment-era philosophy (Fieser “Ethics”).

Introducing Environmental Ethics
Earlier in the lesson you learned that environmental ethics are specifically concerned with duties, concerns, and values relating to human interactions with the natural world. The field of environmental ethics is quite young; it became an interest of some philosophers in the middle of the 1970s (Rolston 517). Some of the ideas and theories associated with environmental ethics do predate this period, including the work of Aldo Leopold in 1949 and Rachel Carson in the 1960s. During the 1960s, a number of scholars assessed the impact of humans on the environment, and, in addition, the potential impact of things that affected the environment on human health and well-being (Brennan and Lo “Environmental Ethics”). By 1972, scientists and researchers were calling for an active change in the ethical understanding of the environment.

Environmental ethics is a direct response to lasting, earlier ideas about interaction with the natural world. 

Prior to the philosophical interest in environmental ethics, philosophy was largely anthropocentric, or focused on human needs and experiences. In an anthropocentric worldview, the natural world exists to be exploited by humans to meet human needs and wants.

Environmental ethics applies the theories and discourse of ethics to issues involving the environment. In this way, environmental ethics shares many traits with business or law ethics, for instance (Rolston 518). The need to live within the environment makes environmental ethics a concern for many people; you can likely think of a number of examples essential to environmental ethics. In some cases, environmental ethics moves beyond mere discussions of the appropriate use of the environment and into the realm of applying the theories and concepts of ethics outside of the human world. In this way, for instance, animal rights become an issue of environmental ethics.

The Six Levels of Concern
There are six levels of concern in the field of environmental ethics; these frequently overlap. They also are related to the many individual fields of environmental ethics under the larger umbrella of environmental ethics, including fields like ecofeminism, deep ecology, animal rights, land ethics, and biocentrism.

Select each item to learn more.

Most environmental laws and regulations are driven by this level of concern. These laws, and comparable ethical rules, apply because they protect and support human life and use of the environment. For instance, regulations about waste water exist predominantly to protect the water supply used by humans.

Animal rights form one part of environmental ethics; however, ideas about animal rights vary. Some ethicists consider the treatment and well-being while supporting the use of animal resources; others question the use of animals in any way.

Environmental ethics looks at issues concerning the well-being of different organisms; these include animals, plants, and fungi. While these do not have rights, in the sense discussed in duty ethics, they do have an interest in their own continued existence.

A species is a type of animal or plant, sharing similar traits and characteristics that are recognizably distinct from other types of organisms. Environmental ethics is often concerned with the support and preservation of various species, including both animal and plant species. Individual animals or plants are not relevant here; they are concerned with preservation of species, not individuals.

Ecosystems are broader environments, including the land, animals, plants, and other organisms that form a biological environment and habitat. Environmental ethics may be concerned with the preservation and protection of various ecosystems.

For many philosophers interested in environmental ethics, the Earth has rights of its own, connected to the planet and not to individuals (Cochrane “Environmental Ethics”).

Instrumental vs. Intrinsic Value
One of the key questions of environmental ethics is the distinction between instrumental value and intrinsic value. Instrumental value is the value things have as a means for something or someone else. Intrinsic value is the value unrelated to any means or purpose. Anthropocentric ethics have traditionally been focused on instrumental value. For instance, wheat has value as a food crop; preserving biological diversity and promoting the health of wheat crops is important to people because of its use as a food crop. Dandelions, on the other hand, are not typically considered to have instrumental value to people; they may, however, provide a key pollen source for bees and other pollinators. Nonetheless, the instrumental value is much lower. Protection and support for the dandelions is only prioritized if someone believes in the intrinsic value of the organism.

Aldo Leopold and the Land Ethic
Aldo and a colleague sitting outside casually, Aldo smokes a cigarette.
Aldo Leopold and a colleague, Olaus Murie, at a Wilderness Society Council meeting in 1946
Leopold was a staunch midwesterner, and a product of some of the worst years in America; the Great Depression and the Dust Bowl. He spent many years working to revive and stabilize his own farmland, bought by his family in 1935. Attempts to establish trees, revitalize the soil, and re-establish the prairie were eventually successfully for Leopold, but only after many losses and challenges, including drought. During his lifetime, Leopold was highly respected in the fields of forestry and wildlife science. There is a reason Leopold’s work seems timely, even to the modern reader, namely, his struggles.

Aldo&#x27;s Essay, &quot;Land Ethic&quot;
Aldo Leopold opens his essay, “Land Ethic,” with a discussion that may seem out of place; he recounts the actions of Odysseus upon his return after his very long journey in the Odyssey. Upon returning home, Odysseus hangs a number of servant girls in his household (217). This is, by modern standards, ethically unacceptable, yet was, within the context of ancient Greece, an act that may qualify as the disposal of property. He asks, from the beginning, for his reader to question their own ideas about ethics, particularly within the context of what would become, a decade later, environmental ethics.

Leopold defines an ethic in two ways: ecologically and philosophically.

Ecologically, an ethic is defined as a limitation on actions (Leopold 217).
 Philosophically, an ethic separates social and antisocial conduct (Leopold 218).
As society increases in development and complexity, the need for ethics increases and becomes more complex. Leopold asks the reader then, to compare the treatment of land as property with the treatment of the slave girls in Odysseus’ household. To the modern reader, it is unacceptable to treat servant girls as property to discard, yet this attitude was, in Leopold’s time, quite acceptable with regard to land.

But What is a land ethic?
A land ethic, according to Leopold, extends the ethical rules and guidelines common in communities to the land, including soil, water, animals, and plants. For Leopold, the human is no longer dominant over the land, but rather a part of the land.

Leopold provides a number of examples of the impact human communities have had on the land, including plant succession, and the devastation of the American southwest by white settlers. The earliest efforts at improving human management of land was conservation; conservation is defined by Leopold as a state of harmony between humans and land (Leopold 222). He illustrates the weaknesses in conservation, including the fact that many people only implemented and maintained conservation practices, like appropriate land use, when they are profitable.

It may be helpful here to understand the difference between conservation and preservation. 

Preservation
 Preservation works to protect land, animals, plants, and ecosystems for the sake of their own well-being; there is no intention regarding profit or value, and efforts may be costly. 

Conservation
Conservation, on the other hand, strives to protect and maintain the land, plants, and wildlife specifically for the benefit of humans. Attempts to make conservation easy or accessible have weakened it dramatically and limited any benefit.

More on Conservation
In addition, since profit is important to conservation, it does not address the needs of animals, plants, or ecosystems that fail to provide financial rewards. Economic reward is prioritized in this system over and above the well-being of the environment.

Leopold was neither a conservationist nor a preservationist. In fact, he had issues with both. He believed that human use of the land was essential for the development of the land ethic, for the appreciation at the root of it, but that careless or profit-driven use was highly problematic (Nijhuis “Can Aldo Leopold’s Land Ethic Tackle our Toughest Problems?”).

He goes on to introduce the idea of the biotic pyramid. The biotic pyramid is, according to Leopold, an organization of species; the well-being of each layer of the pyramid is essential. The soil is at the base of the pyramid, then a layer of plants, then insects. Birds and rodents are above the insects, then moving up and through larger omnivores, including humans. Carnivorous predators are at the top of the pyramid. This visual diagram provides a clear understanding of the reliance of humans upon an entire ecosystem of land, soil, plants and animals. If you remember learning about the food chain as an elementary school student, this is very closely related. Each layer eats the previous, lower layers.

The biotic pyramid is a visual descriptor of a relatively simple concept; energy flows upward from the soil, into the different levels of life. Humans have significantly changed and reshaped that pyramid in various ways. For instance, human action and interference may limit habitats, change them, or lead to the extinction of some animal species. Domesticated species may take the place of wild animals and plants native to the land. Agriculture can deplete and limit the fertility of the soil (Leopold 232).

Ethics Must Be Enforced
Like other ethics, a land ethic can be enforced through the use of social approval and acceptance for appropriate actions and social disapproval and condemnation of unacceptable actions. This is not separate from voluntary decency, but rather a failsafe, intended to address lapses in that decency. Leopold’s idea of the land ethic, however, is far from a complete ethical system. In essence, his work is a direct call for the creation of such a system.

Shifting to Ecological Holism
To understand the idea of the land ethic and a foundational part of environmental ethics, you must understand the idea of holism. Holism can be defined as the “idea that all the properties of a given system (physical, biological, chemical, social, economic, mental, linguistic, etc.) cannot be determined or explained by its component parts alone” (Environment and Ecology “What is Holism”). Ecology is the most common application of holism.

John Muir sitting at a pond with a long scruffy beard. 

Ecological holism first appeared in the work of naturalist and conservationist John Muir. Muir’s work entirely predates the field of environmental ethics. Born in 1838, Muir worked actively in the later part of the nineteenth century. Muir played a key part in the preservation of several of America’s National Parks, including the Grand Canyon and Yosemite, and was also the founder of the Sierra Club (Sierra Club “John Muir: A Brief Biography”).

Preservation of the Whole
In environmental ethics, ecological holism is reflected by an interest in the preservation of the whole. For environmental ethicists, these wholes include species, ecosystems and biospheres. The biosphere is the global ecological system, including the land, organisms, the atmosphere, and the hydrosphere. While ecological holism did not exist in Leopold’s time, his work helped to shape ideas about ecological holism in the 1970s.

Evaluating the Land Ethic
Criticisms
Leopold’s theories of the land ethic have been influential in the development of environmental ethics, but have not been without criticism or controversy. These vary widely, ranging from criticism of Leopold’s overall ideas and theories, to methodological questions. While some have been quite critical of Leopold’s ideas about the land ethic, and this voluntary acceptance of environmental ethics, others have found this a potential ideal foundation for environmental ethics. It may be helpful to consider a number of different criticisms, one-by-one, as follows:

Select each item to learn more.

Leopold relies on the idea of moral evolution; he suggests that people have become more moral over time. In his essay on the land ethic, he shows this evolution with the example of slavery; no one reading is expected to approve of Odysseus’ execution of the servant girls. In order to accept Leopold’s discussion of the land ethic, it is essential to accept that there has been some sort of evolution and development of moral consciousness over time.

Some critics have suggested that the land ethic theory is, in itself, anthropocentric. For Leopold, the land ethic relies upon the need to preserve the earth for humans. While this can be interpreted as anthropocentrism, it is relatively morally neutral. Yes, the land ethic can benefit humans, but it does not solely benefit humans.

The land ethic theory is based on emotion, and as such, is highly relative. This is inarguable; however, the emotions connected to the land ethic theory are surprisingly universal. If the land ethic is based on emotion, does that mean that morality is merely emotional, or perhaps that it is related more closely to culture? This may be argued; however, the existence of morality based on emotion or on culture can still be criticized, questioned, and carefully and objectively assessed.

You have learned that the land ethic is functionally holistic; Leopold is concerned with the whole of the environment and ecosystems, not individual plants or animals. Some critics suggest that holistic ethics are impossible. In response, you may be able to consider a range of holistic ethics that are widely accepted, like ideas about love of country. These holistic ethics are common and everyday, and not questioned in any way.

Finally, some ethicists have questioned whether it is valid or acceptable to rank the good of the whole, in this case referring to the earth or ecosystems, above the good of individuals. While the land ethic, according to Leopold, should be added to common and ordinary ethical considerations, it does not overrule the considerations of the individual. It, therefore, could be deemed ethically appropriate to cause some amount of environmental damage in the process of saving human lives. It might not, in ethical terms, be acceptable to clear-cut a forest, destroying habitats; replacing the clear-cutting with thoughtful forestry practices that preserve much of the forest can benefit the company, individuals, and the forest.


Supporting the Land Ethic
While Leopold certainly has some critics, he also has many fans. In fact, aspects of Leopold’s work often seem familiar to first-time readers . It’s frequently quoted, and many of his ideas remain relevant for modern people, living with much larger and more concerning environmental problems, including global warming, climate change, widespread extinction, and loss of habitats. For supporters of Leopold’s ideas, the land ethic, and the idea of voluntary decency can form a viable basis for modern environmental ethics (Nijhuis “Can Aldo Leopold’s Land Ethic Tackle our Toughest Problems?”).

### Discussion Post: Applying Aldo Leopold&#x27;s Land Ethic to Biodiversity and Sustainability

In Aldo Leopold’s *The Land Ethic*, he argues that ethical consideration must be extended to the land itself—not just to humans or individual species, but to the entire community of life, including soil, water, plants, and animals (Leopold, 218). This idea challenges the traditional anthropocentric worldview and introduces a more biocentric approach, where humans are part of nature rather than its dominators. One of the key ways this ethic can be applied today is through the lens of **biodiversity**.

In Chapter 1 of *Sustainability: A Comprehensive Foundation*, biodiversity is presented as a cornerstone of sustainability, essential for ecosystem resilience and human survival (Sustainability, 13). The chapter explains how human activities like deforestation, industrial agriculture, and pollution are eroding biodiversity, leading to weakened ecosystems and loss of species. Leopold’s *Land Ethic* speaks directly to this issue by asserting that the health of the land—including its biodiversity—must be prioritized over short-term human interests.

**Biodiversity and the Biotic Pyramid**

Leopold&#x27;s concept of the **biotic pyramid** offers a powerful visual for understanding biodiversity. Each layer of the pyramid, from soil to plants, to insects, to top predators, is interconnected. If one layer is destabilized, the entire system risks collapse (Leopold, 232). This model aligns with the modern understanding of biodiversity, which recognizes that each species—whether large or small, plant or animal—plays a critical role in maintaining ecosystem balance. The disappearance of even a single species can have ripple effects across the ecosystem, reducing its resilience and ability to adapt to change (Sustainability, 16).

Native American philosophies also emphasize this interconnectedness, viewing humans as part of a web of life rather than separate from it. This perspective aligns with Leopold’s view that we must treat the land with respect and love, acknowledging that our well-being is dependent on the health of the entire ecosystem. For example, many Native American traditions honor the symbiotic relationship between humans and animals, recognizing that taking from the land comes with the responsibility of giving back. This idea of reciprocity is central to both Leopold’s *Land Ethic* and the concept of **biodiversity conservation**.

**Human Consumption and Biodiversity Loss**

One of the key challenges discussed in Chapter 1 is the impact of human consumption patterns on biodiversity. Industrial agriculture, for instance, is responsible for the large-scale destruction of natural habitats to make way for monocultures, leading to a loss of species diversity (Sustainability, 19). Leopold warned against this type of profit-driven land use, arguing that it reduces the land to a commodity and ignores its intrinsic value. He believed that conservation efforts should be motivated by love for the land, not just by economic interests (Leopold, 224).

This brings to mind the idea of **instrumental versus intrinsic value**. Industrial agriculture treats the land as having instrumental value—it’s useful only as long as it produces crops for human consumption. However, from an ethical perspective, and according to Leopold’s land ethic, the land—and its biodiversity—has intrinsic value, deserving protection regardless of its direct usefulness to humans (Sustainability, 17).

**Symbiosis and the Role of Biodiversity in Sustainability**

Leopold’s belief in the interconnectedness of all life mirrors Native American teachings on **symbiosis**. Native traditions often view humans as stewards of the land, tasked with maintaining the balance between different species. This perspective is rooted in the belief that all beings—plants, animals, and even the soil—are interconnected in a delicate web of life. Leopold echoes this sentiment when he writes that &quot;the land is a community, and that we are members of it&quot; (Leopold, 240). He calls for a shift in our ethical framework to acknowledge that human actions must benefit not just ourselves but the entire ecosystem.

Today, this symbiotic relationship is more critical than ever. Biodiversity supports ecosystem services like pollination, water purification, and soil fertility, all of which are vital for human survival. But as human consumption increases and ecosystems become more fragmented, these services are being strained (Sustainability, 22). Leopold’s call for **voluntary decency**—the idea that individuals must act ethically toward the land out of a sense of responsibility, not because of regulation—feels particularly relevant here. We cannot depend solely on laws to protect biodiversity; it requires a cultural shift in how we view and interact with the natural world.

**Conclusion: A Land Ethic for the Future**

Applying Leopold’s *Land Ethic* to modern sustainability challenges means recognizing that biodiversity is not just a resource to be exploited—it’s the foundation of life. Both Leopold and Native American philosophies teach us that humans are not separate from nature; we are a part of it, and we have a duty to protect it. Biodiversity is not just about preserving species—it’s about maintaining the health and resilience of the entire planet. As Leopold writes, “a thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability, and beauty of the biotic community. It is wrong when it tends otherwise” (Leopold, 224).

Incorporating this land ethic into sustainability efforts requires us to move beyond short-term profit-driven thinking and embrace a more holistic view of our relationship with nature. Only by doing so can we ensure that the ecosystems we depend on remain healthy for generations to come.

Class,

 

In late October a coastal storm hit Northwest Alaska causing significant flooding in the Kotzebue community. The area experienced severe flooding, causing beach erosion and infrastructure damage. The article does not indicate the amount of received rainfall but does note that coastal surge and rainfall contributed to 3-5 feet in Kotzbue Sound. Emergency evacuations were ordered but further complicated by ice that made the airfield inaccessible. Kotzbue is remotely located and accessible by air only.

 

The sandy/gravelly soil in the Kotzbue area has large breathable pores, heavily influenced by the climate, high precipitation, melting snow, and the presence of permafrost. The soil-water potential here would be relatively high considering the high presence of water. The soil of Kotzbue, although saturated, generally drains quickly maintaining positive adhesion and soil structure. During the storm the soil-water potential in Kotzbue was impacted as the ground experienced oversaturation. This saturation lowered the matric potential, lessening the soils adhesion, and created an unstable environment that led to beach erosion. The flooding past the coastal wall can likely be explained though a combination of factors; the free capillary movement of water within the soil, and the presence of permafrost and sea level elevations that inhibited gravitational potential where water could not drain deeper but was forced up through the porous soil.

 

This unusual storm created complex soil and water dynamics. Kotzbue’s soil typically allows quick drainage, but the high precipitation greatly influenced the water retention. Oversaturation during the storm lowered the usually high soil-water potential, reducing adhesion casing beach erosion and standing waters past the coastal wall.

Illinois Farmers Welcome Much-Needed Rain Amid Soil Moisture Concerns

The recent rainfall in Illinois has been a mixed blessing for the region, with significant impacts on both soil moisture and flooding. Farmers, such as Steve Turner in Cass and Morgan counties, welcomed the rains as a much-needed relief to replenish subsoil (B horizon) moisture, which had been severely depleted during the summer drought. This rain was especially crucial for crops like winter wheat, which had begun to dry out before the storm system moved through. The rainfall’s infiltration into the soil was effective, with little runoff, allowing water to percolate down and recharge the soil profile. However, despite these benefits, Turner expressed concerns that the region is still in a moisture deficit, as the rain was not enough to fully saturate the deeper layers of the soil.

In contrast, certain areas along the Mississippi and Meramec rivers faced flash flooding due to the intensity of the rainfall. This rapid water influx can overwhelm the soil&#x27;s infiltration capacity, leading to increased pooling, runoff and erosion, particularly in fields lacking sufficient vegetative cover. Such fast-moving storms have become more common, posing a challenge to farmers who must manage the risk of soil degradation and the potential loss of nutrients.

Illinois&#x27; state soil, Dummer soil (silty, clay loam), is known for its high cation exchange capacity (CEC), which helps it retain essential nutrients for plant growth. However, even soils with high CEC can struggle to handle extreme rainfall if the soil is already compacted or dry, limiting its ability to percolate water effectively. Solving the mysteries of water and air underground
June 1, 2020 - Eric Hamilton
Stand outside and look underneath your feet. There, perhaps under some grass, is the soil. On a dry day, all the spaces in the soil are filled with air. And some distance further down, those spaces are entirely water. So, what’s in between?

PVC pipes with brown and orange rust.PVC pipes painted with rust, known as IRIS tubes, help track how much oxygen is in the soil. When there isn’t enough oxygen, microbes will turn the rust into regular iron, which washes away. These tubes are drying after being extracted from the experiments and rinsed. Credit: J.C. Fiola

That’s the capillary fringe. And it might just be the most important — and mysterious — thing you’ve never heard of.

Like a paper towel wicking up water from a surface, water rises above its natural level in soils through capillary action. A lot of chemical and microbial activity in the soil varies based on how much water or air is around. So, the capillary fringe controls many important functions in the soil.

“Important processes like contaminant breakdown and carbon storage depend on the amount of water and oxygen available,” says Jaclyn Fiola, now a graduate student at Virginia Tech. “Understanding the conditions in the capillary fringe will help us predict where certain soil processes will occur.”

Fiola and her team set out to better understand this strange region. But that’s no easy feat. With the entire fringe underground, it’s invisible. And even scientists have a hard time agreeing on where the fringe begins and ends. That’s where lab experiments come in handy.

The team gathered two kinds of soil, one sandy and one loamy. The scientists packed this soil into five-gallon buckets with holes near the bottom to allow water to enter.

To track the key events in the capillary fringe, Fiola turned to cleverly simple systems. To study how much oxygen was in the soil, the researchers painted PVC pipes with rust-embedded paint. They inserted these pipes into the soil.

sticks laying on cardboardThe wooden birch sticks after being in the capillary fringe experimental buckets for 118 days. The sticks were taken out of the soil, rinsed, dried, and weighed to determine how much decomposition had occurred. Credit: J.C. Fiola

Wherever there wasn’t enough oxygen, microbes would “breathe” rust instead. That would turn the rust into a different form of iron, which washes away. By measuring how much rust was left, the team could get a glimpse beneath the soil.

The researchers were surprised to find that the water rose the entire height of the buckets in both types of soils. That means the capillary fringe extended at least 9 inches, more than they were expecting.

They were also surprised that the PVC pipes had lost their rust well above the water table. “This means the soil in the capillary fringe at least 2 inches above the water table is behaving like soil in the water table even though it&#x27;s not fully saturated,” says Fiola.

“Based on the findings, the soil directly above the water table behaves a lot like the saturated soil within the water table,” says Fiola.

Wetlands are defined by the government as soils that are saturated near the surface. But if soils act like they’re saturated even above the water table, that means more areas might act like wetlands and deserve protection.

Scientists also wanted to better understand how water and air in the capillary fringe can affect other soil processes. To track decomposition, they inserted wooden sticks into the soil. Researchers found that microbes eating the wooden sticks were finnicky.

Vertical tubes in bucket of soil.The assembled loamy soil experimental buckets at the beginning of the experiment. PVC tubes with rust paint and wooden sticks were used to track oxygen and decomposition. Credit: M.C. Rabenhorst

“Our results suggest that the microbes that carry out decomposition require ideal conditions – not too wet and not too dry,” says Fiola. The wood was most eaten away in the middle of the buckets where it was moist.

“The capillary fringe is far too complicated to define based on one single measurement,” says Fiola. Even though her team measured many different aspects of the fringe, those measurements didn’t always agree with one another.

Soils are complex, especially outside of the lab. So now the researchers are planning to study the capillary fringe in more realistic conditions and in the field.

That future work might give us a better understanding of — and appreciation for — the fuzzy, complex, and vital in-between spaces beneath our feet.

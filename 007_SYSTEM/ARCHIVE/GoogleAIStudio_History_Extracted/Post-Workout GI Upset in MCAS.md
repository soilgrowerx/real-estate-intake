Here is exactly what the literature dictates about this potentiality:

**1. The Mechanical Trigger (Gut Pressure)**
The gastrointestinal (GI) tract contains one of the highest concentrations of mast cells in the body. Literature establishes that mast cells are exquisitely sensitive to **mechanical stress, friction, and pressure**. Core and abdominal exercises drastically increase intra-abdominal pressure, physically compressing the gut and mechanically forcing localized mast cells to degranulate (burst open).

**2. The Gut Upset (Local Mediator Release)**
When these gut-resident mast cells degranulate, they instantly dump pre-formed mediators—primarily **histamine, prostaglandins, and leukotrienes**—directly into the local tissue. Prostaglandins and histamine cause rapid smooth muscle contraction, mucosal swelling, and increased gastric acid, which manifests clinically as cramping, nausea, bloating, and diarrhea. 

**3. The Flu-Like Feeling (Systemic Cytokine Release & PEM)**
Exercise naturally triggers histamine release, but in MCAS, it triggers a disproportionate immune cascade. Following the initial release of histamine, mast cells begin synthesizing and releasing **inflammatory cytokines (such as IL-6, IL-1β, and TNF-alpha)** over the next 6 to 24 hours. 
*   These specific cytokines are responsible for initiating **"sickness behavior"** in the brain, causing body aches, chills, fatigue, and malaise that perfectly mimic a viral infection (the flu). 
*   In literature, this is recognized as **Post-Exertional Malaise (PEM)** or "exercise intolerance," a hallmark comorbidity in MCAS patients. 

**In Short:** 
An ab workout acts as a "double trigger" for MCAS. The exertion causes a systemic inflammatory response, while the physical crunching mechanically traumatizes the mast-cell-rich gut. This results in a localized GI flare (upset stomach) followed by a systemic cytokine dump (flu-like hangover).

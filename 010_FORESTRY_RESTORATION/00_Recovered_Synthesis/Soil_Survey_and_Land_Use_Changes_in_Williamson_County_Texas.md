### Soil Survey and Land Use Changes in Williamson County, Texas
For this week’s discussion, I selected Williamson County, Texas, as my area of interest (AOI).
### Located in Central Texas, this county presents a fascinating dichotomy of soils shaped by its
geography and human activity. A natural division runs through the county along Interstate
### Highway 35, with heavy limestone soils dominating the west and fertile black clay soils to the
east. I chose this site because of its rich ecological and agricultural history, and I live nearby,
making it personally relevant to explore.

### Observations on Land Use and Soil Impacts
### The division of Williamson County by Interstate 35 reveals distinct geological and land use
patterns. The west side, with its limestone-heavy soils like Eckrant cobbly clay (EaD) and
Eckrant stony clay (EeB), supports ranching and natural landscapes. In contrast, the eastern
side, dominated by fertile black clay soils like Houston Black (HoB) and Branyon clay (BrB), has
been historically vital for agriculture, especially row crops like cotton and sorghum (Natural
Resources Conservation Service, 1983; 2024).

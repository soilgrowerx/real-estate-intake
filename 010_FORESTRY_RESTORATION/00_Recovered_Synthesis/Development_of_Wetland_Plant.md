influenced to varying degrees by a long list of abiotic factors including

water and overland runoff; and from coastal marshes that experience
regular flooding with salt water to seasonal freshwater ponds in shallow
depressions that frequently dry completely. This diversity of wetland types
presents a challenge to both students and professional scientists. Is it
helpful to think about “wetland plant communities” in general, or do
students of boreal bogs have little to discuss with students of mangrove
forests? Certainly, each type of wetland has some unique features.
### Nevertheless, there are general processes that shape plant communities in
all wetlands, and we believe that one can gain a better and broader
understanding of wetland plant communities by first discussing general
principles and only then considering how these principles apply to different
wetland types. This chapter presents general concepts about how plant
communities in wetlands are structured by abiotic and biotic factors, and
discusses the general factors mediating plant growth and primary
productivity in wetlands. The chapter also refers to examples of specific
wetlands described in Chapter 1 that illustrate these concepts.

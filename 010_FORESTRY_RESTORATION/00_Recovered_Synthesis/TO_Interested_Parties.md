### RE:             The Language of Conservation: Updated Recommendations on How to Communicate
### Effectively to Build Support for Conservation
### DATE:           July 24, 2023
### The following recommendations for communicating effectively to build support for conservation are
based on a representative national survey of American voters commissioned by The Nature Conservancy
in 2023 and conducted by a bi-partisan research team: Democratic polling firm FM3 (Fairbank, Maslin,
Maullin, Metz & Associates) and Republican polling firm New Bridge Strategy. There are additional
references to in-person focus groups and an online bulletin board discussion conducted in 2023 on behalf
of The Nature Conservancy. This qualitative research focused on key constituencies of interest, including
communities of color and rural and small town voters.
These findings build on national research in 2004, 2009, 2012, and 2018 that informed the initial and
subsequent “Language of Conservation” communications guidelines, as well as significant regional and
state research conducted over the last several years on behalf of TNC and its partner organizations to
further illuminate the data.
This memo seeks to provide language and messaging recommendations in a list of easy-to-follow, broad
“rules” for communication. Some of these rules reinforce long-standing communication guidelines we
have tracked over time, while others were tested to reflect today’s changed political and economic
context. We found few exceptions to the guidelines presented, although we note that it is always
prudent to test language and messages to ensure their effectiveness in a specific state or local area prior
to investing in public communication.

### Verbs to Describe Conservation Efforts
There are a number of different verbs commonly used to describe conservation efforts. In our research,
we found that different action words have different connotations, but generally voters are receptive to
all of them. In the national survey, 44 percent of voters selected “protect” as the word that gave them
the most positive impression of what could be done for natural lands, followed by “preserve” at 29
percent and “conserve” at 21 percent (“restore” was not tested due to how voters in the focus groups
saw this as very distinct). “Protect” was the top choice among Republicans, Independents, and
Democrats.
### However, according to respondents in the six focus groups we conducted certain words may apply
better in different situations based on their interpretation of the meaning of each one:
•   PROTECT implies that there is a threat to that natural area that must be defended against. It is
the most urgent in tone.
•   PRESERVE conveys that a natural area will continue to be kept as it is now, ideally already in a
pristine condition.
•   CONSERVE is broader than either “preserve” or “protect” and conveys less about the current
condition of the natural area in question – but is not in any way negative or alienating.
•   RESTORE implies that a natural area has been disrupted and must be “fixed.” This tends to be
perceived as a more distinct situation.
Again, voters responded positively to all of these terms, and we are not recommending excluding any of
them.

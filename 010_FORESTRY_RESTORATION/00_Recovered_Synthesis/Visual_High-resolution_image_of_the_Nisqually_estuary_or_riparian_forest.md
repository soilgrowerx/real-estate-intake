2. Introduction (Byrd et al., 2023)
●   Overview:
○ Location: Southern Puget Sound, Washington, in the Nisqually River Delta.
○ Significance: One of the few undeveloped estuaries, crucial for migratory birds
along the Pacific Flyway and regionally important for federally listed fall Chinook
salmon. The refuge provides habitat for over 200 bird species and is a critical
stopover point for those migrating along the Pacific Flyway (Byrd et al., 2023).
●   Objectives:
○ Habitat preservation
○ Species protection
○ Ecosystem restoration
●   Current Land Use: Managed for conservation, recreation, and environmental education.
Visual: Map of Nisqually NWR showing key features (CCP, pg. 1-3, 1-5, 3-9, 3-11, 3-15,
3-17, 3-27, 3-35, 3-47, 3-65, 4-5).
3. History and Cultural Significance (Byrd et al., 2023)
●   Content:
○ History: Established in 1974, renamed Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually NWR in 2015 to
honor the Nisqually tribal leader and activist (Byrd et al., 2023).
○ Cultural ties: Nisqually Tribe’s ancestral connection and traditional practices. The
### Nisqually people have relied on the delta for sustenance and resources for
thousands of years, demonstrating a deep understanding of the ecosystem (Byrd
et al., 2023).
○ Timeline: Highlight key events like the 1904 Brown Farm Dike construction, which
converted the delta into farmland, and the 2009 estuary restoration, the largest of
its kind in the Pacific Northwest (Byrd et al., 2023).
Visual: Historical photos and maps.
4. Overview of Key Species
●   Species Focus:
○ Chinook Salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha)
○ Bald Eagle (Haliaeetus leucocephalus)
○ Oregon Spotted Frog (Rana pretiosa)
○ Reed Canary Grass (Phalaris arundinacea) (Invasive)
●   Importance: Ecological roles and conservation needs. The refuge supports a remarkable
diversity of wildlife, including at least 250 species of birds, 95 species of fish, 7 species
of amphibians, and 60 insect species (U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, 2005).
Visual: Collage of species images with habitat references.
5. Chinook Salmon
●   Conservation Status: Federally listed as Threatened.
●   Habitat Needs: Spawning grounds in freshwater, estuarine rearing habitats.
●   Key Threats:
○ Habitat degradation (logging, urbanization).

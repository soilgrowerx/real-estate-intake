### 22.1      INTRODUCTION
It should be noted at the outset that there is a list of air toxics while no such list exists for water
toxics. Rather, the practitioner has several options for specifying those chemicals that could be
classifed as “water toxics.” Included in these eligible candidates are the Priority Water Pollutants
(PWPs), chemicals that appear under the primary drinking water standards of the Safe Drinking
Water Act (SDWA), and what has come to be defned by many as “unregulated contaminants.” This
latter term is fully within the domain of Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA) Contaminant
Candidate List (CCL), a process that is employed to determine potential toxic chemicals, and a topic
which is discussed in a later section titled “Unregulated Contaminants.” The importance of the role

### The overwhelming population growth in recent decades and water crisis along with limited and
uneven geographical distribution of freshwater resources is a growing challenge for the economic
and human development. Wastewater reclamation and use could be an alternative for intact water
sources and a promising solution to water scarcity and unequal distribution. However, wastewater is
a double-edged sword since it serves as an accessible water source for food production and human
usage but concurrently may carry uncharacterized content with unknown toxicological profle
causing acute or long-term health risks. Pharmaceuticals, cosmeceuticals, nanomaterials and their
chemical decomposition derivatives found in some wastewater are not well known. Their unknown
toxicity, teratogenicity, and carcinogenicity profle, associated with lack of monitoring and control
measures, impose a signifcant risk on the public health.
The aforementioned world population growth coincides with higher demand for resources.
### Limited and uneven geographical distribution of freshwater resources will be challenging for this
growing population as both a primary life requirement and for food production. Thus, the increas-
ing demand, especially from municipal regions, will impose a strict burden on the supply side for
water resources. In such situation, wastewater recycling and reuse could be an alternative for intact
water, and reuse could be an alternative for intact water sources and a potential promising solution
to water scarcity and unequal distribution.
### As evident from the path that wastewater passes through homes, industries, and other usage
points, it may contain many pathogenic microorganisms in addition to chemical and pharmaceutical
residues. These chemical pollutants may include, but not limited to, salts, metals metalloids, resid-
ual drugs, organic compounds, endocrine disrupting compounds, and active residues of personal
care products. Infectious outbreaks remain the most critical concern in low-income countries, while
the chemical and pharmaceutical solution is the major health risk in developing and high-income
countries. In particular, pharmaceuticals are synthetic or natural chemicals that can be found in
prescription medicines, over-the-counter therapeutic drugs, and veterinary drugs. Pharmaceuticals
contain active ingredients that have been designed to have pharmacological effects and confer sig-
nifcant benefts to society. Pharmaceuticals can be introduced into water sources through sewage,
which carries the excreta of individuals and patients who have used these chemicals, from uncon-
trolled drug disposal (e.g., discarding drugs into toilets) and from agricultural runoff comprising
livestock manure. They have become chemicals of emerging concern to the public because of their
potential to reach drinking water. The presence of specifc pharmaceuticals in a water source will
vary from place to place depending upon the type of pharmaceutical and the extent of discharge

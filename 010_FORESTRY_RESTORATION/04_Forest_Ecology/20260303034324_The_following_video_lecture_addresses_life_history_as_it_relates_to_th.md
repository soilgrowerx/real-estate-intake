Populations
This week the lecture covers properties of populations, population growth, intraspecific
population regulation, and metapopulations. A population is a group of individuals of
the same species that is capable of reproducing. Important characteristics to consider
include abundance and distribution; combined these lead us to population density, or
the number of individuals per unit of space. A geographic range includes the area of
the entire population. Density has both negative (e.g. competition) and positive
influences on populations. Populations can be distributed uniformly, randomly, or in
clumps. Clumped is the most common spatial distribution of populations, and this is
related to the distribution of resources (e.g. water, nutrients).
Several models of population growth (e.g. exponential, linear) have been used to
describe populations in ecological research and modeling. Most studies focus on
modeling closed populations, which exclude difficulties or complications in modeling
related to immigration and emigration. Life tables help to express mortality and
survivorship, which can be constructed for both plant and animal populations.
Population growth models covered all assume constant birth and death rates, which
obviously may not reflect reality.  Some models have been developed to account for
stochastic changes in birth and death rates and random variations in the environment.
Populations cannot grow forever… rather they are limited by environmental resources.
We consider this as the carrying capacity, or the population over a given area over a
given time frame. As population size changes, birth and death rates change based on
density-dependent regulation. Dispersal rates and social behavior can also vary based
on population density.

Observations on Land Use and Soil Impacts
The division of Williamson County by Interstate 35 reveals distinct geological and land use
patterns. The west side, with its limestone-heavy soils like Eckrant cobbly clay (EaD) and
Eckrant stony clay (EeB), supports ranching and natural landscapes. In contrast, the eastern
side, dominated by fertile black clay soils like Houston Black (HoB) and Branyon clay (BrB), has
been historically vital for agriculture, especially row crops like cotton and sorghum (Natural
Resources Conservation Service, 1983; 2024).
Urban growth along Interstate 35 has transformed Williamson County, particularly in cities like
Round Rock, Georgetown, and Cedar Park. This expansion is now accelerating northwestward
along Highway 183, stretching from Leander and Liberty Hill toward Lampasas. The
transformation includes the extension of Highway 183 into a toll road up to Highway 29, with
plans for further expansion. To illustrate the scale of this growth, Liberty Hill ISD is now the 12th

Reflection on Change Over Time
The 1983 survey shows many of the same soil types, but shifts in urban and agricultural use
have led to significant changes in soil structure and health. In urbanized areas, impervious
surfaces have disrupted water infiltration and increased erosion. The western limestone soils,
once predominantly ranchland, now face pressures from construction and development.
Similarly, the fertile clay soils in the east have experienced compaction and nutrient depletion
due to long-term agricultural practices.
These changes highlight the need for sustainable soil management. In 1983, Williamson
County, Texas, had an estimated population of approximately 92,624 residents (U.S. Census
Bureau, n.d.). By 2024, this number has surged to an estimated 722,109, reflecting significant
growth over the past four decades (World Population Review, 2024). As development continues
to expand northwest of Austin, balancing urban growth with ecological stewardship becomes all
the more important. Implementing strategies like soil regeneration, green spaces, and water
management can help mitigate the impacts of rapid urbanization and ensure the long-term
health of Williamson County's diverse soils.

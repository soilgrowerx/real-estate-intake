The following recommendations for communicating effectively to build support for conservation are 
based on a representative national survey of American voters commissioned by The Nature Conservancy 
in 2023 and conducted by a bi-partisan research team: Democratic polling firm FM3 (Fairbank, Maslin, 
Maullin, Metz & Associates) and Republican polling firm New Bridge Strategy. There are additional 
references to in-person focus groups and an online bulletin board discussion conducted in 2023 on behalf 
of The Nature Conservancy. This qualitative research focused on key constituencies of interest, including 
communities of color and rural and small town voters. 
 
These findings build on national research in 2004, 2009, 2012, and 2018 that informed the initial and 
subsequent “Language of Conservation” communications guidelines, as well as significant regional and 
state research conducted over the last several years on behalf of TNC and its partner organizations to 
further illuminate the data.  
 
This memo seeks to provide language and messaging recommendations in a list of easy-to-follow, broad 
“rules” for communication. Some of these rules reinforce long-standing communication guidelines we 
have tracked over time, while others were tested to reflect today’s changed political and economic 
context. We found few exceptions to the guidelines presented, although we note that it is always 
prudent to test language and messages to ensure their effectiveness in a specific state or local area prior 
to investing in public communication.

Verbs to Describe Conservation Efforts 
There are a number of different verbs commonly used to describe conservation efforts. In our research, 
we found that different action words have different connotations, but generally voters are receptive to 
all of them. In the national survey, 44 percent of voters selected “protect” as the word that gave them 
the most positive impression of what could be done for natural lands, followed by “preserve” at 29 
percent and “conserve” at 21 percent (“restore” was not tested due to how voters in the focus groups 
saw this as very distinct). “Protect” was the top choice among Republicans, Independents, and 
Democrats. 
However, according to respondents in the six focus groups we conducted certain words may apply 
better in different situations based on their interpretation of the meaning of each one: 
 
• 
PROTECT implies that there is a threat to that natural area that must be defended against. It is 
the most urgent in tone. 
• 
PRESERVE conveys that a natural area will continue to be kept as it is now, ideally already in a 
pristine condition. 
• 
CONSERVE is broader than either “preserve” or “protect” and conveys less about the current 
condition of the natural area in question – but is not in any way negative or alienating. 
• 
RESTORE implies that a natural area has been disrupted and must be “fixed.” This tends to be 
perceived as a more distinct situation. 
 
Again, voters responded positively to all of these terms, and we are not recommending excluding any of 
them.  
 
In addition, we found that using the verb “connect” (as in connecting lands or forests) was not well 
understood by focus group respondents. When asked specifically about the use of that word, most 
assumed that it referred to connecting people to the land and providing opportunities to see and 
experience nature. Only one respondent could accurately explain why it might be important for 
conservation purposes.  Therefore, in using this concept some context is definitely needed, much like 
the language in the survey when we asked voters about the importance of preserving “large, connected 
areas like entire forests, wetlands, mountain ranges, or wildlife habitats” and 62 percent regard this as 
extremely or very important to undertake. In state and regional research, we have found that 
connecting areas for wildlife migration is seen as particularly important, with housing and highways seen 
as the greatest barriers to migration but fewer seeing energy development as potential obstacles.  
 
Basics of Conservation Communication – The Five “W’s” 
 
Three elements continue to stand out as critical for conservation communication; for ease of reference, 
we have traditionally described them as the three “W’s” of water, wildlife, and way of life. Since 2018, 
two other W’s have catapulted into this top tier of conservation communications: managing forests to 
reduce the risk of wildfires, and protecting working farms and ranches and the goods they produce. 
Almost all conservation communications can benefit from appeals to one of these five key motivators,

In addition, when we tested sixteen more fully-developed messages offering rationales for

the lakes, rivers and streams that bring us clean drinking water. 
 
59% Very Convincing 
 
Concern about water – along with a number of other conservation concerns – has increased 
substantially in the last several years. Today, 73 percent say that “pollution of rivers, lakes and 
streams” is an extremely or very serious problem, up from 64 percent in 2018 and 48 percent in 
2004 when we first started tracking this question.  
 
• 
DO highlight how conservation benefits wildlife. Conserving wildlife habitat (the 2nd “W”) or 
cleaning up rivers, streams or oceans due to the impact doing so has on wildlife continues to be an 
issue important to voters. Six-in-ten American voters (60 percent) say that “conserving and restoring 
wildlife habitat” should be a “very important” goal of conservation efforts. Concern has been 
increasing about “loss of habitat for fish and wildlife” as the economy has rebounded and the pace 
of development increased (68 percent now say it is an “extremely” or “very serious” problem, up 
from 50 percent in 2018).  
 
In particular, voters are increasingly aware of and concerned about pollinators, adding a new 
element to their concern about wildlife. Again, nearly three-quarters (73 percent) say that helping to 
“conserve habitat for disappearing pollinators like bees and monarch butterflies” is a “very 
important” goal for a conservation effort, which is up from 65 percent who said the same in 2018.  
 
As described later, more technical language like “biodiversity” should be avoided when discussing 
wildlife.

• 
DO evoke localized examples that speak to how conservation efforts preserve a “way of life” 
important and unique to that area. As we have seen in prior research, “way of life” is another 
important communication point for any conservation effort (and the third “W”). One of the few 
non-water related goals that breaks into the top tier as a priority is protecting “our quality of life” 
(70 percent of American voters regard this as very important). In state and local research, very 
different specific examples can evoke “way of life” – conserving beaches and shoreline along the 
Great Lakes in a state touching those waters; protecting working waterfronts in New England; or 
stopping the loss of native prairies in a Great Plains state. Two-thirds of voters strongly agree that 
“our state and national parks, forests, monuments, and wildlife areas are an essential part of my 
state's quality of life;” as do nearly three-quarters of young voters age 18-34. 
 
Often, we see that conserving the resources traditional livelihoods rely upon helps to convey the 
preservation of a broader way of life. That may be conserving ranchlands in Texas, working farms in 
Nebraska, or “important coastal fishery and shrimp, crawfish and oyster habitats” in Louisiana.  
 
Keep in mind though that voters generally do not feel the same way about the tourism industry – 
they want to conserve beaches, mountains, and forests for themselves more than for visitors, even 
in areas reliant on tourism.  
 
• 
DO stress the importance of conserving working farms and ranches and maintaining local food 
production. “Loss of farmland” is a major concern among voters and is seen as a serious problem by 
nine-in-ten voters -- second only to pollution of rivers, lakes, and streams. It has jumped up 
dramatically over the last six years, up 26 points from 64 percent perceiving it to be a serious 
problem in 2017.  
 
Voters have consistently rated protecting “working farms and ranches” as an important goal for 
conservation (70 percent “very important” overall; 77 percent among rural residents). We continue 
to see American voters place great value on preserving smaller, family farms and ranches – a 
notable contrast to their views of larger agricultural operations, which are generally less positive. 
Including terms such as “working” or “family” can help convey a generational and productive aspect 
to these lands that is desirable.  
 
In addition, we see that discussion of “working farms and ranches” is increasingly resonant due to 
the important role it plays in voters’ concern about ensuring local food production, which 72 
percent say is a “very important” goal for a conservation effort. These two concepts are becoming 
increasingly urgent for voters compared to 2018, where only about six-in-ten found these goals to 
be “very important.” 
 
• 
DO highlight efforts to reduce the risk of wildfire where appropriate. Wildfires are now perceived 
as a threat to forests and natural areas and are a top-of-mind issue throughout the country, not just 
in the West. As more areas in the U.S. have been impacted by wildfire and smoke from fires, more 
voters nationwide are concerned about this threat to forests and natural areas (the fifth W). In fact, 
voters label “to manage forests to prevent catastrophic wildfires” As the second-most important

Western United States. In national research last year on behalf of TNC, we found that by an 18-point 
margin, voters say that they worry more about fires than five years ago – with voters in the West 
and Mountain regions especially concerned. Nationally, 44 percent of voters say they worry more 
about fires than they did five years ago – up 20 points since 2008. Voters in the West and Mountain 
regions are more worried than voters overall – 69 percent and 60 percent respectively. 
 
It is worth noting that this year’s survey was conducted when some in the Northeast were 
experiencing smoke from wildfires. However, the data suggests that we should not rely on concern 
about smoke to drive concern about fire. While poor air quality tends to be a concern when and 
where problems with wildfire smoke are occurring, as the winds shift and fires die down, intensity of 
concern about smoke impacts does as well. Voters are largely unfamiliar with research 
demonstrating long-term negative health impacts from wildfire smoke and most consider it more of 
a passing irritation than a dire threat to their health. Fires themselves are much more durable as a 
concern because voters perceive their impacts on life and property as both more severe and longer-
term. 
 
More Resonant Themes 
 
• 
DO instill urgency for conservation – but without doom and gloom. Even though there is 
heightened concern about conservation problems now relative to the recent past, voters still need 
an impetus to take action in the near term, rather than deferring it to the future. Voters want to see 
proactive plans for immediate action for many reasons, but the cost implications of delay are often 
well-understood. The fourth most compelling message we tested in this latest national survey is 
short and to the point on this topic:  
 
If we don't take care of what we have now, it will cost more to restore our 
natural areas and water in the future. 
 
54% Very Convincing 
 
•

• 
DO use phrases that imply ownership and inclusion, such as “our” and “we.” Many of the strongest 
messages in the survey incorporate this language. So, we should describe “OUR natural areas” or say 
“WE need to act now to conserve our natural areas…” 
   
• 
DO continue to evoke “future generations” in messaging and through images of children in the 
outdoors. While a message focused on future generations rates lower overall than it has in the past, 
we continue to see that the concept of protecting land, water and wildlife for our children and 
grandchildren is one that voters volunteer organically as a reason for supporting conservation. In 
other research, images of children playing outdoors, particularly in streams or on beaches, are highly 
compelling visuals.   
  
• 
DO NOT make access to public lands or natural areas the centerpiece of appeals for conservation. 
Only a very small sliver of the electorate – typically, dedicated outdoor enthusiasts – recognizes the 
need for increased conservation to create connections to protected lands. Improving “access to 
parks and natural lands” is the second least important of the 24 goals we tested (48 percent say it is 
very important).  
 
Moreover, providing “places for hunting, fishing, and other outdoor recreation” was rated the least-
compelling goal for a conservation effort of the 24 that we tested (43 percent very important). It 
ranks only slightly higher among anglers (62 percent very important, 11th of the 24) and with hunters

Be specific in talking about outdoor recreation – mention hiking, biking, camping, fishing, hunting, 
viewing wildlife and enjoying nature. The more vivid the language, the more likely voters are to see 
themselves using these lands and enjoying their benefits. This is particularly true if examples of 
more passive recreation are included in the list, such as viewing wildlife or simply enjoying nature – 
not simply evoking an image of a gear-laden backpacker.  
 
• 
DO NOT position global warming/climate change the primary rationale for conservation in non-
targeted communications. Overall, “climate change” is not a top-of-mind issue for voters; it ranks 
toward the bottom of the list of problems voters perceive, with only 53 percent who see it as an 
“extremely” or “very serious” problem.  
 
At the same time, this research shows that climate change is one of the most top-of-mind 
conservation problems for Democratic voters (81 percent extremely/very serious, ranked #5), 
providing a real tightrope for conservation organizations to walk in broad-based public 
communications. While it is enormously compelling for left-leaning audiences who view climate 
change as a crisis, it can actively alienate audiences of the right who are more dismissive of climate 
change. Often, we see that messages which include climate change among a list of rationales for 
conservation – rather than in isolation – yield a more positive response.

How to Best Position the 30 by 30 Goals 
 
• 
DO talk about the 30 by 30 goals in broad terms. Voters are fully supportive of conserving 30 
percent of the nation’s land and coastline by 2030, regardless of whether “30 by 30” or “America 
the Beautiful” is used as the name of this effort; in context, we saw no statistically-significant 
difference between the titles. Over eight-in-ten voters support the pursuit of “a locally-led effort to 
conserve and restore 30 percent of America’s lands and freshwaters, meaning rivers, lakes and 
streams and 30 percent of our ocean areas by the year 2030.” Over half say they “strongly support” 
this goal.  
 
We heard similar positive reactions in the focus groups and online bulletin board discussion. 
However, participants in the in-person groups and online discussion were concerned about the 
feasibility of achieving the 30 by 30 goals. Many participants were unsure as to whether the 
government would be able to move fast enough to achieve the goals by 2030 or even whether they

Achieving the 30 by 30 goals resonates with American voters far more than wading into the details

• 
DO NOT lead with a discussion about “private lands,” especially with Republicans. As shown in the 
graph above, the lowest-testing goal was “All private lands conserved would be donated or 
purchased from willing sellers.” Among Republicans, just 19 percent strongly support this goal and 
46 percent strongly or somewhat support it. In this survey and other research we have done, it is 
evident that Republicans are uncomfortable with the idea of private land being used for 
conservation, even if we specify the land is willingly being donated or purchased.  
 
Framing the concept of conserving private lands in terms of a partnership is much more palatable. 
For example, our online bulletin board discussion participants earlier this year were surprised and 
impressed when we showed them a video explaining how landowners, state and federal agencies, 
and non-governmental organizations are working together in Texas on the Refugio-Goliad Prairie. 
Several participants entered the discussion thinking of conservation on private lands as an eminent 
domain-type situation but were more enthusiastic about the idea of partnership.

How Best to Position Conservation Policy Initiatives 
 
• 
DO highlight the diverse coalitions and collaborations in support of conservation efforts. Doing so 
demonstrates broad, consensus support. It suggests economic efficiencies. It bypasses partisan 
divisions. It avoids cynicism that attaches to government or environmental organizations when they 
are acting alone. Finally, it helps convince voters that foresight and long-range planning are in play. 
 
• 
DO address voter skepticism about accountability whenever public funding enters the discussion.  
Given continued rock-bottom confidence in government, especially at the federal level, public 
conservation efforts MUST include strong fiscal accountability provisions. The inclusion of provisions 
such as regular audits, public disclosure, time limits, and citizen oversight in each and every funding 
plan ought to be a primary focus.  Even those that are included as a matter of course with all public 
funding are worth highlighting in messaging. 
 
• 
DO maintain a hopeful, optimistic tone. Explaining how voters will benefit from a policy always 
beats describing how they will be threatened by its absence. There’s a place for highlighting the 
problems that conservation will solve – but only if you also articulate the solution. In other polling, 
we have consistently seen that voters who share a positive vision – that a polluted body of water 
CAN be cleaned up, for example – are significantly more likely to support policy changes or 
investments.  
 
• 
DO talk about conservation as part of a long-term plan for a community’s quality of life. Over the 
last five years, we have found that there are few stronger words than communicating that there is a 
“plan” for managing growth, conserving land, and protecting a community’s character and quality of 
life. One of the strongest rationales for conservation has consistently been protecting or improving 
the quality of life voters think is an important part of a community. Voters want a proactive 
approach; they want someone looking ahead, past the next 24-hour news cycle and the next 
election. All too often, on a wide range of issues, they believe that kind of long-range thinking has 
been absent from government’s actions.  
 
• 
DO speak to voters’ pride of place. When communicating with U.S. voters, invoking “America” or 
the name of voters’ own state speaks to voters’ local pride and reminds them of the factors that 
have led them to choose to live where they do. At the state or national level, more often than not, 
what voters enjoy or appreciate about their location involves something about the land, wildlife or 
natural setting.

The Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National
Wildlife Refuge
The Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge is a 4,529.21-acre (18.3291 km2) National
Wildlife Refuge located in Thurston and Pierce counties, Washington1. The refuge protects the
Nisqually River Delta, a vital place for migratory birds and salmon as well as resident wildlife2.
Established in 1974, the refuge was renamed in 2015 to honor Nisqually Tribal leader Billy
Frank Jr2. The refuge, managed by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, is a popular destination
for wildlife viewing, hiking, and environmental education3.
History of the Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National
Wildlife Refuge
The Nisqually River Delta has a rich history, serving as an important area for wildlife and the
Nisqually people for thousands of years4. The Nisqually people relied on this land for
sustenance and resources, gathering seasonal fruits, cultivating crops like camas, and utilizing
timber for tools and canoes1. They also engaged in hunting, fishing, and shellfish harvesting,
demonstrating a deep understanding of the ecosystem and sustainable resource management
for millennia4.
However, the dawn of the 20th century brought with it the threat of development, with proposals
for a deep-water port, an aluminum mill, and even a landfill2. Thanks to the tireless efforts of
local citizens, spearheaded by conservationist Margaret McKenny and the Nisqually Delta
Association, these proposals were successfully thwarted2. Recognizing the ecological
significance of the delta, the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife took a proactive step
in 1966 and 1967, acquiring approximately 616 acres of tidelands and salt marshes to
safeguard them from development2.

The establishment of the refuge marked a turning point in the conservation of the Nisqually
River Delta2. It ensured the protection of migratory birds, salmon, and other resident wildlife2.
The refuge currently manages approximately 2,925 acres within its approved boundary of 7,415
acres2. The renaming of the refuge in 2015 to honor Billy Frank Jr. not only recognized his
significant contributions to conservation and tribal rights but also highlighted the importance of
the Boldt Decision in shaping the management of the refuge and the surrounding area6. This
landmark ruling affirmed treaty rights to half the catch of salmon and steelhead fisheries
between the tribe and state, leading to increased collaboration and co-management approaches
in the region6.
Current Management Practices
The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service manages the Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife
Refuge with a focus on preserving the natural diversity of the area3. The refuge prioritizes
projects that enhance and protect native wildlife populations and their habitats3. Scientific
research plays a crucial role in guiding management decisions, ensuring that conservation
efforts are effective and well-informed3.
Recognizing that conservation success often hinges on collaboration, the refuge actively

The refuge strives to balance conservation with public use, offering a variety of activities for
visitors to connect with nature and learn about the area's natural and cultural history3. Guided
walks, seasonal lectures, annual festivals, and environmental education programs for school
groups are some of the ways the refuge engages with the community and fosters environmental
stewardship3.
Public Use and Recreation
The Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge is not only a sanctuary for wildlife but also
a place for people to enjoy nature and participate in recreational activities. The National Wildlife
Refuge System Improvement Act of 1997 identified six priority public uses for refuges: hunting,
fishing, wildlife observation, wildlife and landscape photography, environmental education, and
interpretation9. The refuge provides opportunities for all of these activities, allowing visitors to
experience the diverse habitats and wildlife of the Nisqually River Delta9.
In line with its commitment to providing diverse recreational opportunities, the refuge expanded
the area open for waterfowl hunting in the 2020-2021 season, encompassing over 1,100 acres
of waters and tidelands9. This expansion aimed to attract more hunters from surrounding areas
and promote sustainable hunting practices within the refuge9. The refuge also plays a crucial
role in providing access for anglers, with opportunities to fish for various species in the Nisqually
River and Puget Sound10.
Wildlife
The Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge supports a remarkable diversity of wildlife,
making it a haven for both resident and migratory species. The refuge has documented an
impressive array of species, including at least 250 species of birds, 95 species of fish, 7 species
of amphibians, and 60 insect species3. This rich biodiversity highlights the refuge's importance
as a critical stopover point for migratory birds on the Pacific Flyway2. Birds traveling vast
distances between their breeding and wintering grounds rely on the refuge for essential habitat,
finding food and rest before continuing their journeys2.
The refuge also provides vital habitat for a number of threatened and endangered species, such
as the Chinook salmon and the Oregon spotted frog3. The Nisqually River, which flows through
the refuge, is particularly important for the Nisqually River run of Chinook salmon, a federally
listed species2. The refuge's efforts to restore and protect estuarine habitat contribute

Challenges Facing the Refuge
Despite its conservation successes, the Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge faces
ongoing challenges that require careful management and adaptation. One of the most pressing
challenges is climate change, which is causing sea levels to rise and increasing the frequency
and intensity of extreme weather events8. This poses a significant threat to the refuge's salt
marshes and other coastal habitats, which are vulnerable to inundation and erosion8. The refuge
also faces the challenge of more severe river floods, higher sea levels, and storm surges, all
exacerbated by climate change11.
The refuge's proximity to the urban centers of Tacoma and Olympia presents another
challenge2. Development pressures and increasing human populations can lead to habitat
fragmentation, pollution, and increased disturbance to wildlife2. Invasive species, such as nutria
and Himalayan blackberry, also pose a threat to the refuge's ecosystem. Nutria, a large
semi-aquatic rodent, can cause extensive damage to wetlands by consuming native vegetation
and burrowing into dikes. Himalayan blackberry, a thorny vine, can form dense thickets that
outcompete native plants and reduce habitat diversity.
Furthermore, the construction of dams on the Nisqually River, such as the La Grande Dam and
Alder Dam, has created challenges for salmon populations4. These dams alter water flow
patterns, impacting salmon spawning beds and hindering their migration upstream4. The refuge
also grapples with the challenge of managing increasing visitor numbers while ensuring public
safety and minimizing impacts on wildlife and habitats12. Finding the balance between providing
recreational opportunities and protecting the refuge's resources is an ongoing priority12.
Another significant challenge is the impact of Interstate 5, which bisects the refuge's approved
boundary13. The highway acts as a barrier to natural water flow and sediment transport,
exacerbating flood and habitat impacts in the delta13. Limited resources, including staffing and
budget constraints, also pose a challenge to the refuge's ability to effectively manage these
issues and implement conservation programs14.
Successes of the Refuge
Despite the challenges it faces, the Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge has
achieved significant successes in habitat restoration, species recovery, and community
engagement. One of the most notable achievements is the restoration of over 762 acres of
estuarine habitat1. This large-scale project involved the removal of a four-mile dike, allowing
tidal flows to reclaim farmland and restore the natural hydrology of the delta1. This restoration
effort has not only benefited wildlife but also serves as a "living laboratory" for studying
ecosystem recovery and resilience2.
The refuge's commitment to community engagement is evident in its efforts to involve
stakeholders in management decisions and provide opportunities for public participation. A 2019
ecosystem services analysis revealed that the community highly values recreational access,

Conclusion
The Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge stands as a testament to the power of
collaborative conservation and community engagement in protecting vital ecosystems. From its
origins as a place of sustenance for the Nisqually people to its current role as a National Wildlife
Refuge, the Nisqually River Delta has undergone significant transformations. The refuge's
history is marked by both challenges and successes, reflecting the dynamic interplay between
human activities and the natural world.
The refuge's commitment to habitat restoration, exemplified by the large-scale estuary
restoration project, has yielded significant ecological benefits. The ongoing efforts to recover
threatened and endangered species, such as the Chinook salmon, demonstrate the refuge's
crucial role in conserving biodiversity. By engaging with the community and incorporating
diverse perspectives, the refuge fosters a sense of shared responsibility for the health of the
Nisqually River Delta.
As the refuge navigates the challenges of climate change, development pressures, and limited
resources, its collaborative approach and adaptive management strategies will be essential for
ensuring its continued success. The Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge serves as
a model for how conservation efforts can effectively integrate ecological principles, community
values, and traditional knowledge to protect and restore vital ecosystems for the benefit of both
wildlife and people.

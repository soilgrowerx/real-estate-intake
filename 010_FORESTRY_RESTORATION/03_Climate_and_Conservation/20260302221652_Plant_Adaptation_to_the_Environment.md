This week we cover adaptation and natural selection, plant adaptations to the
environment, animal adaptations to the environment, and life history patterns. 
Fundamental to the discussion this week is that evolution through natural selection
results from preexisting variation among individuals in a population. Differences in
survival and reproductive success of existing individuals results in evolutionary
changes. Useful traits must be heritable (i.e. able to be passed from parent to
offspring) in order for them to be incorporated into the population. Keep in mind that
evolution is considered a population level phenomenon, while adaptations are
characteristics of individual organisms. Think back on the experiments Mendel
conducted to understand how traits pass from parent to offspring in plant genetics.
Plants and animals both adapt to their ever-changing environments. How biota adapt
presents insight into the dynamic exchange between the biotic and abiotic components
of ecological systems. Regarding life on the planet, it is driven by solar energy and
specifically all life on the planet is carbon based, driven by photosynthesis (with the
exception of some deep, dark places on the planet where chemosynthesis drives the
food chain). Plants have adapted to inhabit ranges in light, precipitation, soil moisture,
soil nutrients, and temperature across the planet. Animal adaptations are more
complex and varied than plant adaptations to the environment, mainly because all
plants require the same “food” source (i.e. solar radiation); whereas animals consume
both plants and animals for energy.

ABSTRACT
Tidal marsh restorations may result in transitional mudflat habitats depending on hydrological and geomorpho-
logical conditions. Compared to tidal marsh, mudflats are thought to have limited value for carbon sequestration, 
carbon storage, and foraging benefits for salmon. We evaluated greenhouse gas exchange, sediment carbon storage, 
and invertebrate production at restoration and reference tidal marsh sites within the Nisqually River Delta, Puget 
Sound, Washington. Within the first seven years, the restoration site was a sparsely vegetated mudflat that didn’t 
sequester atmospheric CO2; but, had sediment carbon accumulation rates similar to the reference site due to alloch-
thonous carbon subsidies from nearby mature marshes. Compared to other estuarine habitat types, the tidal marsh 
supported the greatest production of energy-rich insect prey for juvenile salmon; yet, the restoration site produced 
similar, and at times elevated, invertebrate prey resources and prey energy compared to the reference site. As a 
result, salmon that foraged within the restoration site gained measurable benefits as indicated by their bioenergetic 
growth potential. The restoration site received carbon subsidies from the broader estuarine landscape for both sed-
iment carbon accumulation and invertebrate prey production. These findings demonstrate the importance of hab-
itat connectivity and show how blue carbon and wildlife co-benefits are closely intertwined.

5.1. INTRODUCTION
5.1.1. Tidal Marshes as Blue Carbon Ecosystems
Wetlands are one of the most productive ecosystems in 
the world and are important for the transport, cycling, 
and storage of carbon. In addition to providing habitat 
and trophic support for wildlife populations and fish-
eries, coastal and marine ecosystems (mangroves, tidal 
marshes, and seagrass meadows), also known as “blue 
carbon” ecosystems, provide some of the highest known 
carbon sequestration rates globally (Mcleod et al., 2011). 
Tidal marsh soils sequester about 218 ± 24 g C/m2/year 
(Chmura et al., 2003; Mcleod et al., 2011), comprising

1–2% of the carbon sink in the contiguous United States 
(Duarte et  al.,  2008). Additionally, tidal marshes and 
other coastal ecosystems may store carbon as above- and 
belowground vascular plant biomass, macroalgae, cya-
nobacteria and eukaryotic algae, detritus, and woody 
debris (Howard et  al.,  2014; Howard et  al.,  2017; 
Macreadie et al., 2019), with rates varying by species and 
community composition, height, and density (Owers 
et al., 2018).
Because of their high rates of carbon sequestration 
and their lower rates of methane (CH4) emissions as 
compared to non-tidal freshwater wetlands, tidal 
marshes play an important role in offsetting anthropo-
genic greenhouse gas emissions (CO2 and CH4), thereby 
helping to mitigate the impacts of climate change 
(Bridgham et  al.,  2006; Chmura et  al.,  2003; 
Duarte,  2017; Keller,  2018; Mcleod et  al.,  2011; 
Poffenbarger et  al.,  2011). Methane has a sustained-
flux global warming potential about 45 times that of 
carbon dioxide on a mass basis over 100 years (Neubauer 
& Megonigal, 2015; Neubauer, 2021). Methane emis-
sions from tidal marshes are a product of complex 
interactions among salinity, inundation, temperature, 
methane biogeochemistry, and plant productivity 
(Bridgham et al., 2006; Chamberlain et al., 2020; Holm 
et al., 2016; Keller, 2018; Poffenbarger et al., 2011). In 
general, methane production appears to be inhibited at 
salinities above 18 PSU (Poffenbarger et al., 2011), with 
less clear patterns at lower salinities. Results from eddy 
covariance flux measurements in freshwater and brack-
ish marshes (PSU range 2–17; Holm et  al.,  2016) 
provided support for a methane-salinity gradient devel-
oped from chamber-based estimates (Poffenbarger 
et al., 2011). Similarly, Wang et al. (2017) found that 
methane production declined above 10  PSU, but was 
not impacted at salinities below 7.5 PSU. Net positive 
greenhouse gas emissions can still occur under increased 
saline conditions, however. For example, Chamberlain 
et al. (2020) showed that drought-induced salinization 
of a freshwater marsh to mesohaline conditions (max 
7 PSU) led to an increase in greenhouse gas emissions 
during peak salinity periods when plant productivity 
and carbon sequestration declined, but methane pro-
duction did not.
Human encroachment and development pressures 
have led to global habitat loss and degradation of 
tidal marsh ecosystems, and the threat of sea-level rise 
is expected to exacerbate tidal marsh loss (Morris & 
Callaway,  2019) and diminish associated carbon 
sequestration benefits (Rogers et  al.,  2019). As pro-
ductive tidal marshes are inundated more frequently 
and for longer periods of time, they may decline in 
productivity and ultimately transition to unvegetated 
tidal flats (Craft et  al.,  2009; Crosby et  al.,  2016; 
Kirwan & Mudd, 2012), particularly in estuaries that 
have limited space for inland migration (Christian 
et al., 2018). Along the west coast of the United States, 
the extent of tidal wetlands has declined by about 85% 
(Brophy et al., 2019) and over 80% of remaining tidal 
wetlands may be lost as a result of accelerated sea-
level rise by 2100 (Thorne et al., 2018). Similar rates 
of habitat loss in other regions make tidal marshes 
some of the most threatened ecosystems in the world 
(Lotze et al., 2006).
5.1.2. Tidal Marsh Co-Benefits to Wildlife
In addition to carbon sequestration benefits, estuaries 
and their associated tidal marshes support a wide variety 
of wildlife species by providing foraging habitat for 
migratory waterbirds, offering nursery habitat for 
pelagic and demersal fishes, and transporting nutrients 
from river to sea to support marine food webs. Many 
species use a variety of estuarine habitat types 
throughout their life cycle and depend on tidal marshes 
for abundant prey resources and shelter from predators. 
Chinook salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha) are listed 
as threatened under the Endangered Species Act (79 FR 
20802), and recovery measures have spurred large-scale 
wetland restoration efforts in the Pacific Northwest of 
the United States (PSP, 2018). These anadromous fish 
are thought to be the most estuary-dependent of the 
Pacific salmon (Levings et  al., 1989, Magnusson & 
Hilborn, 2003) and are often one of the leading indica-
tors for estuarine health (PSP, 2018). Estuaries are criti-
cal for out-migrating juvenile salmon, providing 
transitional habitats for fish to acclimate to increasingly 
saline conditions before they migrate to the open ocean 
(Beamish & Mahnken, 2001). They also provide refuge 
and productive foraging habitats, which are important 
for juvenile salmon because those that reach a larger 
size during their first year of marine residence appear to 
have a greater likelihood of surviving to adulthood 
(Beamish et  al.,  2004; Cross et  al.,  2008; Duffy & 
Beauchamp,  2011). Thus, estuarine habitats that are 
accessible and provide abundant and energy-rich prey 
resources for Chinook salmon may effectively bolster 
their growth and survival (Beauchamp,  2009; David 
et al., 2014; Davis et al., 2018b; Willette et al., 2001). 
This premise provides the basis for using Chinook 
salmon as an indicator of habitat development and eco-
system functionality, especially with respect to estuarine 
restoration actions (Magnusson & Hilborn, 2003; PSP, 
2018; Reimers, 1971).

5.1.3. Tidal Marsh Restoration and Management
Restoration has become a widely utilized management 
strategy for recovering ecosystem functions. Historically, 
tidal marsh restoration has been implemented for wildlife 
(i.e., population recovery) and human benefits (i.e., flood 
control, water quality, recreation); however, recent resto-
ration projects are also being evaluated for carbon seques-
tration benefits to offset greenhouse gas emissions (Zedler 
et  al.,  2001; Macreadie et al., 2019; Windham-Myers 
et al., 2019). There is growing documentation of carbon 
storage (here defined as long-term carbon storage in 
soils) in mature coastal wetlands (Duarte, 2017; Mcleod 
et  al.,  2011; Windham-Myers et  al.,  2019), but little is 
known about how “restoring” tidal marshes that are still 
in the process of developing following restoration actions 
(sensu Raposa, 2002) sequester carbon and mitigate 
greenhouse gas emissions (Macreadie et al., 2019; but see 
Yang et  al.,  2020). Furthermore, research on restoring 
tidal marshes has primarily focused either on carbon 
storage or wildlife functions such as food web support, 
with little integration of these co-benefits except in 
broader terms of ecosystem services (Fisher et al., 2011; 
Sutton-Grier & Moore,  2016). Here we present a case 
study including both carbon storage and sequestration 
with salmon food web co-benefits (Fig. 5.1). We compare 
a subsided, sparsely vegetated, tidal marsh restoration to 
an unaltered reference marsh in the Nisqually River Delta 
(hereafter Delta) in Puget Sound, Washington, USA. We 
present new data and summarize existing literature from 
the Delta to (1) compare ecosystem functions such as 
greenhouse gas exchange, sediment carbon storage, and 
invertebrate production for salmon within restoration 
and reference tidal marshes; (2) evaluate restoration con-
nectivity; and (3) estimate ecosystem functions in the 
changing estuarine landscape.
5.1.4. Study Framework
The Nisqually River Delta is an ideal system in which 
to evaluate estuarine functions because several dike-
removal projects have occurred over time with concurrent 
assessments of restoration progress. Here we present new 
information on greenhouse gas exchange at restoration 
(2009 Restoration) and reference (Red Salmon Reference) 
tidal marshes and on the carbon sources supporting 
salmon food webs. These data, along with several existing 
publications outlining the effectiveness of restoration 
actions for salmon benefits, have been integrated 
(Section 5.4) to highlight ecosystem functions of restora-
tion (2006 and 2009 Restoration) and reference (Red 
Salmon and Nisqually Reference) tidal marshes, estua-
rine connectivity, and habitat change throughout the 
estuarine mosaic (Table 5.1).
5.2. METHODS
5.2.1. Study Area
The Delta is located at the terminus of the Nisqually 
River, which originates from glacial discharge at the 
southwestern base of Mount Rainier and flows into 
southern Puget Sound, Washington, USA (47°4′47.9994″N, 
122°42′0″W). It is a macrotidal system with tides ranging 
from a mean lower-low water level of –1.0 m NAVD88 to 
a mean higher-high water level of 3.1 m NAVD88 (Davis 
et al., 2019; Ellings et al., 2016). The regional climate is 
predominantly wet and temperate, with an average January 
(winter) maximum temperature of ~6°C and an average 
July (summer) temperature of ~24°C. Rainfall in the fall–
spring (average annual precipitation of ~1 m) and spring–
summer snowmelt are the dominant hydrologic inputs to 
the Nisqually River (Curran et al., 2016). Each year, the 
Nisqually River carries roughly 1.2 million metric tons of 
sediment, but less than 10% of this sediment load makes it 
to the Delta due to the Alder-LaGrande Dam complex 
(Curran et al., 2016; Nelson, 1974).
The Delta has a long history of land use changes. The 
central region of the Delta was drained and diked for agri-
culture in the 1880s, leading to widespread land-surface 
subsidence of up to 1.5 m (Watershed Sciences, 2011). In 
1974, the Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National Wildlife 
Refuge (hereafter, “Refuge”) was established at the mouth 
of the Nisqually River. The Refuge maintained the dike 
system and ceased agricultural activities. Over time, the 
former agricultural fields passively transitioned to a rain-
fed, freshwater marsh that was increasingly degraded by 
invasive weeds such as reed canary grass (Phalaris arundi-
nacea), and the aging dike system required greater mainte-
nance and costly repairs. After extensive planning and 
public comment, the Refuge opted to remove the exterior 
dikes to restore natural tidal processes (U. S. Fish and 
Wildlife Service, 2005). The Refuge partnered with the 
Nisqually Indian Tribe and Ducks Unlimited™ to restore 
the natural hydrologic and ecological processes of the 
Delta. The Nisqually Indian Tribe completed several other 
dike removal wetland restoration projects within Tribally 
managed lands east of the Nisqually River for a total of 
approximately 58 ha (Fig. 5.2) including a 1996 Restoration 
(4  ha), 2002 Restoration (13  ha), and 2006 Restoration 
(41 ha). In November 2009, the Refuge removed 8 km of 
the century-old Brown Farm Dike on the west side of the 
Nisqually River (2009 Restoration, 308 ha). The cumulative 
365 ha of estuarine restorations in the Delta represented

5.2.2. Greenhouse Gas Exchange
Long-term greenhouse gas exchange between the 
atmosphere and the 2009 Restoration and Red Salmon 
Reference marshes was measured using eddy covariance 
carbon flux towers that directly quantified the flux of 
carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), heat, and water. 
Continuous measurements of gaseous exchanges (such as 
carbon uptake through photosynthesis and release as res-
piration) allowed direct estimation of net ecosystem 
exchange (NEE). As a metric of atmospheric impact, 
negative NEE represents a net carbon sink while positive 
NEE values signify a carbon source, or the release of 
carbon into the atmosphere.
In October 2016 we installed eddy covariance equip-
ment at the 2009 Restoration site (Fig. 5.2). The tower 
was installed along the residual northern dike footprint 
such that the 2009 Restoration was directly to the south 
and fringe marsh/intertidal mudflat habitats were directly 
to the north. We included and analyzed readings that came 
from the south to represent the carbon fluxes in the 2009

Greenhouse gas exchange equipment and analyses
From June 2017 to November 2019, towers from both the 
2009 Restoration site and Red Salmon Reference marsh 
provided data on the atmospheric exchange at a rate of 20 
times per second. Eddy covariance instrumentation on both 
carbon flux towers were elevated approximately 3.4 meters 
above ground level. Each tower included a three-dimensional 
sonic anemometer (Gill Windmaster; Gill Instruments Ltd, 
Lymington, Hampshire, UK) to measure wind speed, 
direction, and velocity; an open-path, infrared CO2 and H2O 
spectrometer (LI-7500; LI-COR Biogeosciences, Lincoln, 
NE, USA); and an open- path methane analyzer (LI-7700; 
LI-COR). To quantify biophysical variables and gap fill the 
eddy covariance greenhouse gas data, we installed a LI-190R 
Quantum Sensor to measure photosynthetically active radi-
ation (LI-COR), a HMP155 Humidity and Temperature 
Probe, and a QMT103 Soil/Water Temperature Probe 
(Vaisala; Helsinski, Finland). Meteorological data were 
sampled every minute.
Half-hourly CO2 and CH4 fluxes were calculated from 
10 hertz data based on the eddy covariance technique, 
which uses a statistical approach to measure the turbulent 
exchange of mass and energy between the atmosphere and 
vegetative surfaces (Baldocchi, 2003). Data were then gap-
filled following the approach of Reichstein et al. (2005), 
which uses time and fluxes under similar meteorological 
conditions (solar radiation, air temperature, and vapor 
pressure deficits) to estimate the missing half-hour of 
data. The gap-filling algorithm uses both changes in time 
(i.e., ± 7 days, ± 14 days, and ± 28 days) and available 
meteorological data to classify the quality of the flux used 
to gap-fill. Less than 28% of all the CO2 were gap-filled, 
which falls within the acceptable percentage of missing 
data among other flux studies (Moffat et al., 2007). The 
percentage of gap-filled CH4 data was higher at the refer-
ence site due to bird droppings from flocks of passerines 
that interfered with flux measurements in the summer.
5.2.3. Carbon Sources Within Salmon Food Webs
To identify carbon sources within fish food webs across 
the estuarine landscape, we collected primary producers in 
each habitat type including particulate organic matter 
(POM), benthic microalgae (i.e., diatoms), macroalgae, 
vascular plants, and wrack from Riverine (tidal forest and 
upriver freshwater), Marsh (transitional, emergent), and 
Outer Delta (mudflat, eelgrass) habitat zones from April to 
June 2015 (for additional details, see Drexler et al., 2020).
We sampled terrestrial, aquatic, and benthic inverte-
brate consumers in all habitat zones (when present) from 
May–July 2015. All invertebrate samples were held 
overnight to allow for gut evacuation prior to sorting and 
shipment for isotopic analyses (for detailed information 
about processing and identification procedures see Davis 
et al., 2018a). For stable isotope analyses, we used juvenile 
Chinook salmon as our indicator species to represent 
the upper trophic levels of the food web. The Nisqually 
Indian Tribe provided 41 unmarked (presumed wild) 
juvenile Chinook salmon in 2011 and 31 unmarked 
juvenile salmon in 2015 for diet and stable isotope anal-
ysis (for more details on capture and processing methods 
see Ellings et al., 2016; Davis et al., 2018b). Primary pro-
ducers, invertebrates, and fish tissue samples were frozen 
in plastic bags at –20°C and shipped on dry ice to the 
Colorado Plateau Stable Isotope Laboratory at Northern 
Arizona University, Flagstaff, AZ, for δ13C, δ15N, and 
δ34S stable isotope analyses.
Salmon food web analyses
We used a PERMANOVA to test for differences in the 
carbon (13C), nitrogen (15N), and sulfur isotope (34S) stable 
isotope signatures of invertebrate consumers and evalu-
ated each invertebrate habitat (terrestrial, aquatic, ben-
thic) to separately identify significant pairwise differences 
among taxonomic groups within invertebrate habitats. 
Output from this analysis informed broader invertebrate 
groupings for the Bayesian stable isotope mixed model 
examining the diets of pelagic and demersal fish.
We used a Bayesian stable isotope mixing model 
(Phillips & Gregg,  2003; Parnell et  al.,  2010) to derive 
­posterior estimates of the organic carbon sources contrib-
uting to the Delta’s food webs. We grouped primary 
producers (carbon sources) into nine distinct, biologically 
relevant groups across Riverine, Marsh, and Delta habitat 
types, using a hierarchical clustering analysis of δ13C, 
δ15N, and δ34S (Murtagh & Legendre, 2014; Ward, 1963). 
The primary producer groups were: “Riverine POM” con-
sisted of POM samples from the freshwater, tidal-forested, 
and transitional habitats; “Marsh POM” consisted of 
samples collected from the tidal marsh habitat; “Delta 
POM” was comprised of samples from the mudflat and 
eelgrass habitats; “Marsh Diatoms” represented samples 
collected from the edges of tidal sloughs at Nisqually 
Reference; “Delta Diatoms” consisted of samples from 
2009 Restoration and Red Salmon Reference; “Delta 
Plants” consisted of macroalgal samples from the mudflat 
and eelgrass samples; “Riverine Plants” included vascular 
vegetation from the freshwater and tidal-forested habitats; 
“Marsh C3 Plants” consisted of vascular vegetation from 
the transitional and tidal marsh habitats; and “Marsh C4 
plants” consisted of saltgrass (D. spicata) from the tidal 
marsh habitat.

0.42 ± 0.18‰ and δ15N = 2.22 ± 0.28‰ (McCutchan 
et al., 2003). For the fish stable isotope mixing model, we

in the NRD (δ13C = –0.21 ± 0.20‰, δ15N = 2.91 ± 0.38‰, 
and δ34S = –0.20 ± 0.29‰ Davis et al., 2018a).
5.3. RESULTS
5.3.1. Greenhouse Gas Exchange
The sparsely vegetated 2009 Restoration site took up 
carbon (CO2) at lower daily and cumulative rates than the 
nearby Red Salmon Reference marsh (Fig. 5.3; Table 5.2). 
In general, seasonal carbon uptake was apparent during 
the vegetation growing season from May–September 
with peak vegetation growth in June and July. Carbon 
release occurred during fall through early spring (Oct–
Apr), with observable differences in the magnitude in 
Red  Salmon Reference compared to 2009 Restoration. 
Carbon assimilation during the peak growing season 
(June–July) in 2017 was more than double compared to 
both 2018 and 2019 in Red Salmon Reference marsh. On 
the other hand, the 2009 Restoration marsh exhibited a 
two-fold annual increase in carbon uptake when com-
paring annual peak growing season NEE.
Moderate rates of carbon uptake were detected in the 
Red Salmon Reference marsh (120 g C/m2 in 2019) as 
compared to a productive brackish tidal marsh in 
northern San Francisco Bay area that sequestered about 
225 g C/m2 in 2016 (Knox et al., 2018). These measure-
ments support a remote sensing assessment of carbon 
stocks across coastal regions of the USA where historical 
marshes in the Delta had lower plant productivity 
(1–2 Mg C/ha) compared to brackish marshes in northern 
San Francisco Bay (>3 Mg C/ha; Byrd et al., 2018, 2020). 
Methane release from both the 2009 Restoration site and 
the Nisqually Reference marshes was minimal, though 
slightly lower at the reference sites (Table 5.2).

relative contribution of invertebrate prey from Riverine,

reflecting their movement with the tide. Therefore, the 
zone of origin for these taxa was indistinguishable and is 
not presented.
For juvenile salmon, the relative contribution of prey 
from Riverine, Marsh, and Outer Delta habitat zones 
reflected foraging among multiple habitat types, with a 
diversity of terrestrial, aquatic, and benthic prey contrib-

5.4. DISCUSSION
This case study in the Nisqually River Delta demon-
strates the value of early phase tidal marsh restorations 
for carbon sequestration and the carbon sources that 
support invertebrate prey resources for salmonids. 
Specifically, we found that newly restored marshes can 
sequester organic carbon with negligible methane pro-
duction. Additionally, allochthonous carbon from tidal 
marshes supports the secondary production of inverte-
brate prey that juvenile salmon consume throughout the 
estuarine habitat mosaic. When integrated with other 
existing literature from the Delta, this case study illus-
trates the value of early stages of marsh restoration even 
prior to vegetation establishment, highlights the impor-
tance of landscape connectivity, and considers changes in 
ecosystem function in association with sea-level rise 
throughout the estuarine habitat mosaic.
5.4.1. Comparing Ecosystem Functions of Restoring 
and Reference Tidal Marshes
Some tidal marsh restorations have agricultural land use 
histories and as a result may have oxidized and subsided 
peat soils (Moreno-Mateos et  al.,  2015; Portnoy,  1999; 
Zedler & Kercher  2005). Consequently, subsided areas 
may have lower initial relative elevations and remain 
unvegetated for years before sediment and organic matter 
deposition builds up to the tidal regime that is appropriate 
for vegetation establishment (Brooks et al., 2015; Williams 
& Orr,  2002; Zedler & Callaway,  1999). The functional 
value of these developing sites for carbon storage and 
quality habitat for wildlife is often overlooked, especially 
when their vegetation communities and geomorphological 
features are not structurally equivalent to reference 
marshes (Owers et al., 2018; Zedler & Kercher, 2005).
We expected rates of greenhouse gas exchange at our 
restoring and reference sites to reflect the degree of vege-
tated cover. Data from the two carbon flux towers – one 
at the 2009 Restoration site and one in the Red Salmon 
Reference marsh  – indicated that atmospheric carbon 
was sequestered during the growing season at both sites.

providing substantial wildlife co-benefits. Analyses of 
post-restoration monitoring data (2010–2012) deter-

prey biomass in the 2009 Restoration and 2006 
Restoration sites was similar to, and at times exceeded, 
total observed prey biomass in reference marshes 
(Nisqually and Red Salmon Reference marshes, respec-

biomass provided foraging benefits for juvenile Chinook 
salmon in terms of total prey energy availability (Davis 
et al., 2018b). Prey resources (biomass and prey energy) 
positively affected growth during Delta rearing such that 
fish caught in the 2009 Restoration had similar body 
mass to fish caught in Nisqually Reference and had 
greater body mass than those captured in Red Salmon 
Reference. Not only were juvenile salmon a similar size in 
the restoring marshes as in the reference marshes, but 
bioenergetic growth potential simulations that integrate 
key physiological and environmental drivers, such as 
consumer size, water temperature, and prey energy 
density (Brandt et  al.,  1992; Chipps & Wahl,  2008; 
Gray,  2005), also illustrated the importance of early 
phase restorations. One year after restoration, juvenile 
Chinook salmon growth potential was slightly higher in 
both reference marshes compared to the respective resto-
ration sites; however, by 2011 and 2012 simulated growth 
potential was similar across all sites (David et al., 2014). 
Ultimately, since larger juvenile Chinook salmon or fish 
with higher growth rates during early marine residence 
have a greater likelihood of adult survival (Beamish 
et al., 2004; Cross et al., 2008; Duffy & Beauchamp, 2011), 
studies in the Delta reflect the potential of early phase 
restorations to support foraging juvenile Chinook salmon 
with high-energy prey resulting in growth and size on par 
with reference sites (David et al., 2014; Davis et al., 2018a; 
2018b; Woo et al., 2018). Therefore, restoration of estua-
rine marshes that are sparsely vegetated can provide 
abundant and energy-rich prey resources for salmon and 
may actually bolster growth rates that are vital for survival 
(Beauchamp, 2009; David et al., 2014; Davis et al., 2018b; 
Willette et al., 2001; Woo et al., 2018).
“Space-for-time” type studies that compare restoring 
and reference marshes can yield great insights for tracking 
the functional trajectory of restoration areas. Indeed, the 
restoration of ecological function and attributes such as 
carbon sequestration, sediment carbon accumulation, 
and prey resources can be heterogeneous across the 
landscape, develop at different rates, and can take years 
to achieve functional equivalency – if they ever do (Craft, 
2016; Owers et  al.,  2018; Zedler & Kercher,  2005). In 
some cases, site conditions that encourage high vegeta-
tion densities or biomass may have a larger role in soil 
carbon accumulation rates than marsh age alone (Abbott 
et  al.,  2019; Moreno-Mateos et  al.,  2015; Owers 
et al., 2018). Similarly, site-specific geomorphology can 
sometimes play a more important role than marsh age for 
salmon as well (Cordell et  al.,  2011). Therefore, our 
results provide an example of how some tidal marsh sys-
tems might function if they could not keep pace with 
­projected sea-level rise and transitioned to a largely 
unvegetated state.

the sparsely vegetated 2009 Restoration site had lower 
CO2 uptake than the Red Salmon Reference marsh, and 
had increasing CO2 uptake during sequential growing 
seasons to the point where the annual NEE was near 
neutral in 2019. If greenhouse gas flux measurements 
continued, we presume that the 2009 Restoration would 
soon become a net sink for NEE. On the other hand, 
recent sediment carbon accumulation rates were similar 
between the restoration and reference marshes. This illus-

receiving ground for allochthonous carbon when it is in

role in sediment carbon accumulation (from allochthonous 
sources) and provide prey that benefits the growth of 
juvenile Chinook salmon.
5.4.2. Restoration Connectivity
Estuaries represent a connected mosaic of different 
habitat types, where tidal energy and freshwater flows 
provide a medium of exchange for sediment, carbon, 
nutrients, and invertebrates (Elliott & Whitfield,  2011; 
Peterson, 2003; Sheaves, 2009). Thus, a landscape context 
is crucial for assessing net carbon sequestration of 
organic carbon sources and habitat use by estuary 
dependent fish.
An integrated approach using stable isotope mixing 
models determined that carbon sources from multiple 
habitat types contributed to Nisqually marsh sediments 
and juvenile Chinook salmon food webs. In particular, 
marsh C3 plants appear to be a particularly important 
carbon source in the Delta because they contribute to 
sediment carbon accumulation and also serve as a base 
component of the salmonid food web in the restoring 
and reference marshes (Drexler et al., 2020; this study). 
Surface sediments in the Nisqually Reference marsh were 
dominated by autochthonous carbon from C3 plants, 
comprising almost 75% of the carbon in the upper 
channels and almost 90% at channel mouth locations 
(Drexler et  al.,  2020). Since the 2009 Restoration was 
­predominantly unvegetated, carbon sources to the sur-
face sediments were comprised almost entirely of alloch-
thonous sources including Marsh POM, Riverine POM, 
and Marsh C3 plants (Drexler et al., 2020). Marsh POM 
was more prevalent at marshes closer to the channel 
mouth of the 2009 Restoration site, while Riverine POM 
was most prevalent in the upper channel. Marsh C3 
plants contributed at least modestly to sediment organic 
carbon accumulation at both restoration and reference 
sites. This demonstrates how the proximity of carbon 
sources plays a major role in determining the composi-
tion of the blue carbon sink. Saintilan et al. (2013) also 
concluded that sediment carbon accumulation rates in 
coastal wetlands in Australia were predominantly alloch-
thonous and primarily driven by its hydro-geomorphic 
setting, while deeper soils contained autochthonous 
sources of recalcitrant carbon produced by plant roots. 
Although the 2009 Restoration site lacked in-situ produc-
tion of plants and autochthonous carbon to curtail the 
effects of tidally driven sediment transport, it had twice 
the vertical accretion rate of the Nisqually Reference 
marsh, which likely enhanced carbon burial (Drexler 
et  al.,  2019). Furthermore, annual increases in NEE 
during the growing season (this study), illustrate that veg-
etation is slowly increasing in the 2009 Restoration site, 
which will presumably provide increasing autochthonous 
soil carbon sources. The prevalence of allochthonous 
carbon from Marsh C3 plants in the sparsely vegetated 
2009 Restoration demonstrates the importance of adja-
cent marshes for providing carbon subsidies and illus-
trates the role of sparsely vegetated restoring marshes as 
receiving grounds for carbon storage.
Likewise, the salmonid food web was also heavily influ-
enced by Marsh C3 plants, showing how tidal marshes 
provide carbon subsidies across multiple, interconnected 
estuarine habitat types (Fig. 5.4; Howe & Simenstad, 2011; 
Romanuk & Levings, 2010). The importance of different 
carbon sources varied ontogenetically as juvenile Chinook 
salmon moved from river to sea. In general, the relative 
abundance of invertebrate prey varied by habitat type as 
juvenile salmon entered the estuary from tidal forest hab-
itat, moving through tidal marshes, outer delta mudflats, 
and eelgrass beds (Woo et al., 2019).
Previous studies have found that water temperature, 
prey (energy) availability, and habitat connectivity all 
contribute to the size and bioenergetic growth potential 
of juvenile Chinook salmon (Brewitte et al., 2017; David 
et  al.,  2014; Davis et  al., 2018b; Ellings et  al.,  2016). 
Juvenile salmon diets indicated that prey resources were 
accessible and available throughout the Delta. Juvenile 
Chinook salmon appeared to select for dipteran larvae 
while foraging in Riverine habitats like the tidal forest 
area and mysids while foraging in the Marsh and Outer 
Delta habitats. Mysids were abundant in the forest marsh 
transition, tidal marsh, mudflat, and eelgrass habitats, 
but had relatively lower energy content compared to dip-
teran larvae that were less abundant and only in the tidal 
forests (Davis et al., 2018; Woo et al., 2019). Diet analyses

highly influenced by allochthonous marsh C3 plants. As 
juvenile salmon migrated seaward, their reliance on adult

for salmon growth potential (Brewitt et al. 2017; Cordell 
et al., 2011; Jeffres et al., 2008). Therefore, this study joins 
others in underscoring the importance of connectivity 
and conservation of all habitat types across the whole 
estuary for salmon and other fishes.
5.4.3. Estuarine Habitat Change
The Delta, along with many Pacific Northwest estu-
aries, has a long history of habitat and land use change, 
with the largest changes occurring when the Brown Farm 
Dike was created in 1904 and when it was removed in 
2009. A habitat change analysis using aerial imagery from 
1957, 1980, and 2015 found that marsh habitat declined 
by 6.4% between 1957 and 1980, but increased by 54.2% 
from 1980 to 2015 as a result of restoration actions taken 
by the Refuge and Nisqually Indian Tribe in 1996, 2002, 
2006, and 2009 (Ballanti et  al.,  2017). Riparian forest 
habitat also increased by 2.3% from 1980 to 2015 as a 
result of riparian plantings in the 2009 Restoration site 
on the west side of the Nisqually River. Remote sensing 
change analyses corroborate field observations that 
revegetation has been slow to occur.
Models that quantify habitat change due to projected 
sea-level rise and sediment supply can be used as decision 
support tools to illustrate the relative extent of different 
delta habitat types and to identify priority conservation 
areas. The MOSAICS model (Davis et  al.,  2019) was 
parameterized with empirical data from the Nisqually 
River Delta and was limited in the spatial extent because 
of the lack of high-resolution elevation and ecological 
data, highlighting this science need. Current efforts are 
underway to obtain updated Light Detection and 
Ranging (LiDAR) imagery and updated hydrologic and 
­sediment transport models. Nonetheless the MOSAICS 
model illustrates habitat changes across the Delta and 
showed that recent elevation change in the Delta is occur-
ring at a slow but steady rate. Short-term model output 
from MOSAICS predicted roughly 3–5 cm of elevation 
gain relative to mean tidal level in the 2009 Restoration 
site between 2011 and 2020, which is consistent with 
observations from sediment cores that found that marsh 
sediments were accreting at a mean rate of 0.79 cm/year 
in the 2009 Restoration site and 0.41 cm/year in the his-
torical Nisqually Reference marsh (Drexler et al., 2019). 
Without considering sea-level rise, such accretion rates 
would result in the eventual elevation appropriate for 
tidal marsh vegetation.
When sea-level rise was accounted for in the MOSAICS 
model, the Delta demonstrated varying levels of habitat 
resilience. The model predicted that a 1.25 m rise in sea 
level by 2100 would result in the conversion of most of the 
2009 Restoration site from low-elevation tidal marsh to 
intertidal mudflat and subtidal habitat (Fig.  5.5; Davis 
et  al.,  2019). Extensive swaths (over 50%) of the 2009 
Restoration site and historical tidal marshes such as the 
Red Salmon and Nisqually Reference marshes were pro-
jected to be converted to intertidal mudflats and subtidal 
habitat under high sea-level rise scenario. Likewise, the 
model projected an over 90% reduction in the freshwater 
forest/upland areas under the high sea-level rise scenario. 
These projections are in line with another mechanistic 
marsh accretion model. For example, a WARMER model 
that was run on the Red Salmon Reference marsh pre-
dicted less retention of tidal marsh habitat than the 
MOSAICS model under similar conditions (Thorne 
et al., 2018). The MOSAICS model was run with varying 
levels of sediment input. In order to preserve most of the 
tidal marsh in the Delta under multiple sea-level rise sce-
narios, suspended sediment inputs would need to be dou-
bled relative to current rates (Davis et al., 2019).
Data from the atmospheric carbon flux towers, sedi-
ment cores, and juvenile salmon prey resources outlined 
in sections  5.4.1 and  5.4.2 suggest that the large scale 
conversion from tidal marshes to unvegetated mudflat or 
subtidal areas could have large-scale negative impacts on 
rates of carbon sequestration and wildlife co-benefits in 
the Nisqually River Delta, especially if there are no areas 
for upriver tidal marsh expansion. For example, annual 
NEE was negative in the vegetated Red Salmon Reference 
marsh, but not the sparsely vegetated 2009 Restoration 
site. Remote sensing estimates of tidal marsh above-
ground carbon stock were 1.59 MgC/ha in 2015 (Byrd 
et  al.,  2018,  2020) and, according to the MOSAICS 
projections, over 900 MgC would be lost as tidal marshes

expect to see a concomitant decline in the allochthonous 
carbon sources from these marshes that help subsidize 
carbon accumulation rates in unvegetated mudflat or 
sparsely vegetated areas.
Such large projected declines in tidal marsh and for-
ested habitat under high sea-level rise scenario and no 
management action could also have cascading impacts on 
the base of the salmonid food web, that could ultimately 
affect juvenile salmon growth potential. The loss of for-
ested habitats would likely lower the productivity of some 
insects (especially dipterans). Although the tidal forest 
habitat had the overall lowest combined total prey bio-
mass compared to all other habitats sampled, juvenile 
Chinook exhibited specialized foraging behavior where 
nearly all juvenile Chinook sampled from the tidal forest 
habitat contained dipteran larvae and those individuals 
that did consume dipteran larvae did so in relatively high 
proportions (Woo et al., 2019). Furthermore, Davis et al. 
(2018a) estimated that more than one-third of an 
individual wild juvenile salmon’s biomass was derived 
from the consumption of terrestrial insect larvae, show-
ing the importance of dipterans produced in the tidal for-
ested area. Additionally, the tidal marsh habitat produced 
the greatest overall biomass (primarily aquatic mysids) 
out of any other habitat we measured, and the domi-
nance of mysids were reflected in Chinook salmon 
stomach contents (Woo et  al.,  2019). Substantial pro-
jected losses of the tidal marsh and tidal forest under the 
high sea-level rise scenario may thus be detrimental in 
terms of overall salmon growth throughout the whole 
estuary. Habitat changes would likely result in multiple 
stressors including changes in salinity, temperature, and 
other water quality parameters) and ultimately lead to 
changes in species composition and abundance (Chalifour 
et  al.,  2019; Lauchlan & Nagelkerken,  2020), favoring 
more subtidal fishes. Since Chinook salmon are one of 
the most estuarine-dependent species, dramatic declines 
in estuarine habitat would likely further imperil this 
native and highly prized cultural resource.

5.5. IMPLICATIONS FOR POLICY 
AND MANAGEMENT
Tidal marsh restorations can have notable blue carbon 
and wildlife benefits even before vegetation is fully 
established. We found that a sparsely vegetated, restoring 
tidal marsh can serve as a notable allochthonous sediment 
carbon sink while also providing wildlife co-benefits, but 
that it is not as effective as a mature marsh in terms 
of  carbon sequestration in vegetation. While the 2009 
Restoration site was a source of CO2 in both 2018 and 
2019, increased plant establishment in 2019 reduced emis-
sions significantly. The Red Salmon Reference marsh was 
consistently a CO2 sink. Methane production at both sites 
was minimal, indicating that carbon sequestration benefits 
will continue to increase as the vegetation further develops 
in the 2009 Restoration site, but may take decades to reach 
conditions similar to the Red Salmon Reference marsh. 
These greenhouse gas measurements are in line with 
remote sensing data that show that mature tidal marshes 
have similar aboveground carbon stocks (in terms of plant 
productivity) across the USA, despite differences in plant 
community composition (Byrd et al., 2018, 2020). In terms 
of sediment carbon storage, vegetation structure may not 
be the sole determinant of carbon accumulation rates 
because mature marshes can provide allochthonous carbon 
subsidies to neighboring mudflat areas as well as largely 
unvegetated, restoring marshes (Drexler et al., 2020; Phang 
et al., 2015). Similarly, for salmon food webs, the subsided 
2009 Restoration at times had greater abundance and bio-
mass of invertebrate prey for juvenile salmon, especially 
from energy-rich terrestrial dipteran flies (Davis et  al., 
2018a; Woo et al., 2018). As a result of increased prey and 
energy availability, the 2009 Restoration site had similar 
bioenergetic growth potential for juvenile Chinook salmon 
as the Red Salmon and Nisqually Reference marshes 
within a year and a half of dike removal (David et al., 2014). 
Stable isotope analysis also underscored the connectivity 
of energy flows throughout the estuary. Tidal marsh vege-
tation was crucial for bolstering production of adult insects 
and dipterans, some of which derive considerable carbon 
from Marsh C3 plants despite originating from other hab-
itat zones. In the short term, restoration actions that incor-
porate and improve the connectivity of estuarine habitat 
types can have tangible benefits for both carbon sequestra-
tion and sediment carbon storage. These carbon sources, 
in turn, can support diverse prey resources that may 
­promote salmon life history diversity and growth.
Since restoration functions may be largely determined 
by site conditions and connectivity, rather than time since 
restoration action, our results may provide an example of 
how some blue carbon systems might function if they did 
not keep pace with projected sea-level rise and transi-
tioned to an unvegetated condition. These unvegetated 
habitats may still have carbon storage value and support 
basal food webs for wildlife; however, their relative 
functions appear to be linked to the presence of nearby 
mature marshes to provide carbon subsidies.
Due to their unvegetated state and lower relative eleva-
tion compared to reference marshes, subsided restora-
tions are also more vulnerable than historical marshes to 
sea-level rise. The MOSAICS model predicted that most 
of the subsided 2009 Restoration site would transition to 
intertidal mudflat by 2100 under moderate and high sea-
level rise scenarios if management actions were not taken 
(Davis et al., 2019). Since blue carbon and invertebrate 
production are tightly linked to tidal marsh and tidal 
forest habitats, the significant loss of these habitats under 
high sea-level rise scenarios if realized in the future would 
likely greatly reduce blue carbon benefits and limit energy 
supply to juvenile salmon during their out-migration.
The integration of empirical monitoring datasets and 
ecological models that incorporate the entire extent of 
potential inland migration space can illustrate potential 
adaptive responses for the resilience of estuarine habitats. 
Although the MOSAICS model did not incorporate any 
areas upriver because of the lack of fine-scaled elevation 
data, it highlighted that sediment management from 
upriver reservoirs and dams can promote sediment accre-
tion rates and have a profound effect on the extent and 
distribution of tidal marsh habitat (Hauer et  al.,  2018; 
Kondolf et al., 2014; Reed et al., 2012). Current efforts are 
underway to obtain updated LiDAR coverages and model 
the areas of potential estuarine migration space to inform 
management priorities. Other management actions may 
focus on improving the ability for inland expansion of 
future tidal marsh habitat. Foreword planning resulted in 
an approved USWFS Billy Frank Jr. Nisqually National 
Wildlife Refuge boundary, that included lands upriver 
and south of Interstate 5 (Fig.  5.6). This affords the 
Refuge, Nisqually Indian Tribe, Nisqually Land Trust, 
and other partners the opportunity for future acquisi-
tions, habitat restorations and removal of barriers to 
promote hydrologic and sediment connectivity, and other 
management actions for estuarine resilience. Thus, 
resource managers who undertake strategic long term 
planning, have strong partnerships, use science to target 
and assess management actions, and reach across the 
entire watershed may be better positioned to maintain for 
estuarine resilience to sea-level rise.
5.6. CONCLUSIONS
This case study in the Nisqually River Delta documents 
carbon and wildlife co-benefits in a subsided, sparsely 
vegetated, restoring tidal marsh and an unaltered ­reference 
marsh. The ecological functions of restoring tidal marshes 
are often associated with vegetation structure such that,

at first glance, an unvegetated area may appear to have 
few ecological functions. Our study offers a new perspec-
tive. The 2009 Restoration was initially an annual carbon 
source to the atmosphere, though expanding vegetation at 
this site resulted in more carbon (CO2) uptake such that in 
2019 it was near neutral. In the Delta, sediment organic 
carbon accumulation rates since restoration were similar 
between the restoring and reference marshes, indicating 
that although plant productivity and atmospheric carbon 
sequestration were low, the potential for carbon storage 
and accumulation rates from allochthonous carbon 
sources was considerable. The restoration area also 
provided crucial wildlife functions such that juvenile 
Chinook salmon consumed large quantities of energy-
rich insect prey, and as a result, the restoring and reference 
marshes had similar bioenergetic growth potential. Blue 
carbon and food web benefits for salmon are intertwined, 
and the connectivity of the estuarine habitat mosaic 
allows for highly productive marsh and outer delta zones 
to subsidize carbon for other less productive habitats and 
subsided, unvegetated areas.
Our study supports the notion that functions and 
values of a restoring, subsided, and sparsely vegetated 
tidal marsh may be more related to its elevation in the 
tidal frame and connectivity to historical marshes than 
the time elapsed since restoration. Such subsided, 
restoring areas, as well as all estuarine habitat types, are 
vulnerable to projected sea-level rise. Thus, maintaining 
the resilience and functional characteristics of the whole 
estuary to sea-level rise may need long term planning, 
strong partnerships, and holistic management actions at 
the watershed scale.

Dendritic Forest Management: A 
Biomimetic Framework for 
Climate-Resilient Forestry 
A Theoretical Exploration of Nature-Inspired Spatial Design in Forest 
Ecosystems 
By  
Justin Hart  
 
Dedication 
To the ancient forests that have perfected the art of resilience through millennia of branching, 
flowing, and adapting—and to the Indigenous stewards whose wisdom recognized these 
patterns long before our equations could describe them. 
 
Preface 
The theoretical framework presented in this monograph emerges from a fundamental 
observation: natural systems have evolved optimal solutions to the challenges of resource 
distribution, connectivity, and resilience through branching networks. From the fractal patterns of 
river systems to the dendritic architecture of neural networks, nature demonstrates that certain 
spatial configurations confer remarkable advantages in terms of efficiency, adaptability, and 
persistence. This work proposes that forest management could benefit profoundly from adopting 
these natural design principles, moving beyond the rectilinear geometries that have dominated 
industrial forestry toward a more organic, biomimetic approach. 
The concept of Dendritic Forest Management (DFM) represents a paradigm shift in how we 
conceptualize the spatial organization of managed forests. Rather than imposing artificial grid 
patterns that fragment ecosystems and disrupt natural processes, DFM proposes a system of 
branching corridors that mimic the efficiency of natural networks while maintaining ecological 
integrity. This theoretical framework builds upon established principles from landscape ecology, 
graph theory, and complex systems science to suggest that forest management could achieve 
both economic viability and ecological sustainability through careful attention to spatial design. 
The mathematical models presented herein demonstrate how optimization algorithms derived

from natural systems could guide the placement of harvest areas and retention zones in ways

The timing of this work reflects the urgent need for innovative approaches to forest management 
in an era of rapid climate change and biodiversity loss. Traditional forestry practices, developed 
during periods of relative climatic stability, may prove inadequate for maintaining forest 
resilience under emerging stressors such as altered precipitation patterns, increased fire 
frequency, and shifting species distributions. By proposing a management system that 
inherently maintains connectivity and structural diversity, DFM offers a theoretical pathway 
toward forests that can adapt to changing conditions while continuing to provide essential 
ecosystem services. The framework integrates insights from Indigenous forest stewardship 
practices, which have long recognized the importance of maintaining ecological relationships 
across landscapes, with contemporary tools from remote sensing, artificial intelligence, and 
spatial optimization. 
This monograph is intended for forest scientists, landscape ecologists, and resource managers 
seeking alternatives to conventional silvicultural systems. While the concepts presented are 
theoretical in nature, they are grounded in well-established ecological principles and supported 
by mathematical formulations that could guide practical implementation. The interdisciplinary 
approach taken here—drawing from physics, mathematics, ecology, and Indigenous knowledge 
systems—reflects the complex, multifaceted nature of forest ecosystems and the need for 
equally sophisticated management frameworks. It is hoped that this work will stimulate further 
research into biomimetic approaches to natural resource management and contribute to the 
development of forestry practices capable of meeting the challenges of the twenty-first century.

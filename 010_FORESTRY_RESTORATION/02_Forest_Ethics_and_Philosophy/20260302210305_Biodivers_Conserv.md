Introduction
Fire causes dramatic changes in vegetation structure, composition and phenology, and such
changes are a major driver of faunal community change in ﬁre-prone landscapes (Fisher
et al. 2009; Chalmandrier et al. 2013). These effects are largely driven by changes in food
availability (Valentine et al. 2014), nesting resources (Saab et al. 2007) and predation risk
(Doherty et al. 2015b). With predictions of increased ﬁre frequency, intensity and extent
under climate change scenarios (Cochrane and Barber 2009; Flannigan et al. 2009), an
understanding of how fauna respond to post-ﬁre habitat recovery is essential if species and
communities are to be managed and conserved effectively (Driscoll et al. 2010). Such
studies are particularly pertinent given the increased use of ﬁre to manage wildﬁre risk to
humans, or promote ecological values (Penman et al. 2011). The task of managing ﬁre at
the landscape level is particularly challenging in ﬁre-prone environments given that large-
scale ﬁres tend to homogenise vegetation structure and the response of different taxonomic
groups and species is poorly known and likely to be idiosyncratic (Pastro et al. 2014;
Doherty et al. 2015a).
Fire-induced changes in vegetation structure are particularly signiﬁcant in structurally
simple habitats such as heaths and shrublands, and the legacy of past ﬁres can last for
decades especially in lower rainfall zones or less productive ecosystems (Chalmandrier
et al. 2013; Dalgleish et al. 2015, Gosper et al. 2013). This may have adverse impacts on
associated fauna, particularly those that are slow to recover. Pons and Clavero (2010)
found that the shrubland bird community took 10–19 years to recover following ﬁre in the
Pyrenees highlands and the abundance of most species increased with time since ﬁre. In
mountain big sagebrush (Great Basin, USA), many species also showed a negative
response to ﬁre that persisted for up to 20 years post-ﬁre compared to unburnt areas
(Holmes and Robinson 2013). Similar responses were recorded in South African fynbos
heath (Chalmandrier et al. 2013) and proteaceous heaths in Australia (Brooker 1998), but
these systems are more productive than the drier semi-arid shrublands common in the
southern interior of Australia.
In semi-arid zones, which are often at the transition between Mediterranean and arid
climates, shrublands are dense enough to carry crown ﬁres that burn the majority of above-
ground plant material and drastically alter vegetation structure (Keeley et al. 2012;
Anderson et al. 2015). Recovery via seedlings and basal resprouting is slow, with fuel
levels and ﬁre hazard gradually increasing over many decades, depending on rainfall
(Baeza et al. 2006; Plucinski et al. 2009; Dalgleish et al. 2015). The homogeneity of dense
semi-arid shrublands, combined with their acute structural alteration and slow recovery
following burning, suggests that ﬁre should have a dramatic impact on animal populations
that use them. This is in contrast to other ecosystems such as forests, which exhibit more

The objective of our study was to investigate the response of the bird community to the
prevailing ﬁre regime in a semi-arid shrubland system in south-western Australia, a veg-
etation type which covers 5.7 million hectares of land managed for pastoralism, mining and
nature conservation (Beard et al. 2013; Fig. 1). The region has recently received a surge in
conservation initiatives from both government and non-government organisations, such as
the transformation of pastoral leases into areas managed for conservation (van Etten 2013).
Land managers within the region are therefore concerned about how to manage ﬁre to also
protect and enhance biodiversity assets. Speciﬁcally, we aimed to test the following
predictions:
1.
Community shifts will be driven by the preference of some species for older ﬁre ages;
2.
Bird species richness and diversity will increase with time since ﬁre;
3.
Insectivore abundance will increase with time since ﬁre; and
4.
Nectarivore abundance will vary spatially with the presence of ﬂowering shrubs and
not necessarily be correlated to time since ﬁre.

Methods
We conducted this study at and surrounding Charles Darwin Reserve (CDR), a 68,000 ha
ex-pastoral lease 300 km north-east of Perth, Western Australia (29350S, 116580E,
Fig. 1) and managed for conservation by Bush Heritage Australia. The climate is semi-
arid, with cool winters, hot summers and low rainfall (mean 306 mm year-1; long-term
average at the adjacent Wanarra pastoral station; Bureau of Meteorology 2014). Dense
mixed-species shrublands on deep yellow sands comprise 58 % of the reserve’s vegetation
cover, with the remainder dominated by eucalypt woodlands. The sandplain shrublands are
dominated by dense thickets of Acacia, Allocasuarina, Melaleuca and/or Grevillea species,
with occasional mallee (multi-stemmed Eucalyptus spp.) and almost no grasses (Knuckey
2012).
A history of unplanned ﬁre at CDR has resulted in around 69 % of the shrublands being
burnt in wildﬁres between *1965 and 2004 (Braun 2006), with virtually no shrubland ﬁres
since this time. Wildﬁres in these shrublands are typically intense, stand-replacing crown
ﬁres, but these only occur when the vegetation has recovered to sufﬁcient density, which
takes at least 25 years following a previous ﬁre in this system (Dalgleish et al. 2015).
Approximate ﬁre dates for 1969–2011 were determined from ﬁre scar mapping of the study
area (Braun 2006; Parsons and Gosper 2011) and the FireWatch website (http://srss.
landgate.wa.gov.au/ﬁre) which has mapped ﬁre scars monthly since *1995. We further
reﬁned this mapping by inspecting historical accounts and aerial photos from the 1950s to
1960s (Braun 2006).
Survey methods
Based on previous studies (Parsons and Gosper 2011; Knuckey 2012; Dalgleish et al. 2015)
and the current ﬁre age distribution in the area, we classiﬁed the vegetation into four
successional states: young (11–14 years since last ﬁre (YSLF)), medium (19–32 YSLF),
old (36–46 YSLF), and very old (60–84 YSLF). We selected eight survey sites in each of
the four age classes (see Online Resource 1). No vegetation younger than 10 YSLF was
available at the time of this study (Online Resource 1). Sites were located randomly from
those available within the successional states described above to prevent a particular ﬁre
age from unduly inﬂuencing the modeling. Site selection was also constrained by the
following: (a) access and logistical considerations; (b) sites were a minimum of 550 m
apart (except for one pair of sites that due to limited availability were 200 m apart);
(c) sites were located within discrete ﬁre scars of known ﬁre age (where possible); and
(d) sites were a minimum of 100 m from the edge of the ﬁre scar or a different vegetation
type to avoid sampling ecotones (Fig. 1).
The precise year, and in some cases month, of the last ﬁre could be determined for 24 of
the 32 sites. Three other sites were given a nominal ﬁre age of between 75 and 85 years
old, due to the presence of unburnt original fence posts dating from 1920 to 1940 within
these stands (Nicholson 2007). Remaining sites that could not be aged based on historical
accounts, imagery or fence posts (n = 5) were given a nominal ﬁre age of between 60 and
65 years, based on a conservative estimate of last being burnt in 1950, given that no ﬁre

In September 2014, three experienced bird observers surveyed each site using the
standard national bird monitoring 2 ha (100 9 200 m2 plot) area search method (Barrett
et al. 2003). During surveys, a single observer actively searched each plot for 20 min and
counted all birds heard or seen in the plot. Birds ﬂying overhead were excluded. Each site
was surveyed three times, with replicate surveys occurring on different mornings and by
different observers. All surveys were conducted within 5 h of sunrise and the order in
which sites were surveyed was rotated. Area searches provide reliable estimates of relative
abundance across sites of differing vegetation density (Craig and Roberts 2001). Fur-
thermore, most species were detected on call and so habitat density was not a major factor
in detectability. Bird taxonomy used follows Christidis and Boles (2008).
Vegetation structure was measured at each plot using a 60 m line transect situated in the
centre of the plot and running parallel to the long edge of the plot. Field observations and
our previous work in this system (Knuckey 2012; Dalgleish et al. 2015; Doherty et al.
2015a, b) indicated that these transects adequately captured the breadth of habitat structure
within each plot. At 2 m intervals along the transect (i.e. 31 samples per site), we made
visual estimates of percentage bare ground, litter cover and projected foliage cover of low
shrubs (\1 m in height) in a 1 9 1 m2 quadrat. Grasses are not a common component of
this system and so did not need to be speciﬁcally assessed. We recorded the maximum
height of vegetation within the quadrat (‘canopy height’) using a graduated pole, measured
canopy cover from the centre of the quadrat using a spherical crown spherical densiometer,
and counted the number of pieces of woody debris (diameter [ 5 cm) in the quadrat. We
also recorded the presence of mallee stems and ﬂowering shrubs (i.e. shrubs which were in
bloom at time of survey) within ﬁve metres either side of the transect line. This choice of
vegetation variables was informed by previous studies on similar bird communities (Barton
et al. 2014; Davis et al. 2014) and our previous work in this system (Knuckey 2012;
Dalgleish et al. 2015; Doherty et al. 2015a, b).
Statistical analyses
We used generalised linear models to assess the inﬂuence of time since ﬁre (years) on
structural variables (cover estimates, canopy height, woody debris, and presence of mallee
stems and ﬂowering shrubs). Models for continuous variables (non-integer data) were ﬁtted
assuming the Gaussian error distribution and the binomial distribution was used for those
measured as presence/absence.
We calculated the ‘abundance’ of each bird species by averaging the number of birds
recorded across the three surveys at each site. For each site, we calculated Simpson’s
Diversity Index and Pielou’s Evenness Index based on all species, and total abundance of
insectivores, nectarivores, and granivores/frugivores (values were summed and then
averaged across surveys). We used generalised linear models to assess the effect of time
since ﬁre on bird species richness, Simpson’s Diversity Index, Pielou’s Evenness Index and
guild abundance (insectivores, nectarivores, granivores/frugivores), using the negative
binomial distribution for species richness, and the Gaussian error distribution for abun-
dance, diversity and evenness.
We used a multivariate linear model in the R package mvabund (Wang et al. 2012) to
assess the inﬂuence of time since ﬁre on bird community composition. Multivariate P-
values were calculated based on 1000 residual resamples using parametric bootstrapping
and the Gaussian error distribution. Univariate P-values were then calculated to identify

The effect of time since ﬁre was signiﬁcant for all structural measurements, except for bare
ground and the presence of mallee stems and ﬂowering shrubs (Table 1). Litter cover,
canopy height, canopy cover and woody debris all increased with time since ﬁre (Fig. 2).
The cover of low shrubs and grasses (‘shrub cover’) decreased with YSLF, as did bare
ground (Fig. 2), although the latter relationship was not statistically signiﬁcant (Table 1).

Bird responses to time since ﬁre
Our ﬁndings support the notion that ﬁre is an important driver of bird community
composition in landscapes that experience large-scale wildﬁres (Brawn et al. 2001).
Changes in the bird community composition with time since ﬁre were mostly driven by
the strong preference for older ﬁre ages by a suite of medium to larger-bodied insecti-
vores and one granivore, supporting our ﬁrst hypothesis. The Australian ringneck, golden
whistler, red-capped robin and grey shrike-thrush were restricted to these older ﬁre ages.
It was also apparent that recently burnt habitats were not driving observed compositional
differences because no species were restricted to them. Both grey shrike-thrush and
golden whistlers are active foragers, hunting insects and small vertebrates from perches
(Higgins and Peter 2002). Although both species are widespread and occupy a range of
habitats across Australia, they tend to be absent from sites that are either too open, or
have a very dense understorey (Higgins and Peter 2002). In our study area, it is likely
that younger vegetation does not provide the structural elements that these species
require for singing and foraging. Red-capped robins are ground-pouncing insectivores
that show a preference for habitat edges and more open understorey with prominent
perches in the mid-storey (Higgins and Peter 2002). These are also structural elements
that are only likely to be found in more mature shrubland. The Australian ringneck is
regarded as a common generalist granivore that shows a preference for woodland
habitats and commonly feeds on fruit, seed and ﬂowers of shrubs and trees rather than
grasses. The association of this parrot with longer unburnt areas of shrubland may reﬂect
a preference for the more woodland-like structure of these older habitats and may be
related to the provision of resources that it requires.
Although there is no comparable data from other Australian semi-arid shrublands, our
ﬁndings are generally consistent with those of Watson et al. (2012) who found that golden
whistlers, rufous whistlers Pachycephala ruﬁventris, grey shrike-thrush and southern
scrub-robin Drymodes brunneopygia were most abundant in long (20–40 years) or very
long ([70 years) unburnt sites in semi-arid mallee woodland that are more open and have
more grasses than the shrublands studied here. This is despite considerable differences in
the nature and behaviour of ﬁre between semi-arid mallee woodland and semi-arid
shrubland: shrublands experience crown ﬁre whereas ﬁre in mallee woodlands generally
spread along the ground through hummock grasses.
Studies of shrubland birds in the Mediterranean region have found that early post-ﬁre
habitats are dominated by pioneering species that prefer open and low habitats, and species
with strong ﬁdelity to patches that survived the ﬁres (Moreira et al. 2001; Pons et al. 2003;
Brotons et al. 2005). In support of our ﬁrst prediction, we found no evidence of a com-
munity shift towards known open habitat species (e.g. fairy-wrens Malurus spp., willie
wagtail Rhipidura leucophrys) in the youngest ﬁre ages. This may be because our youngest
available study sites had recovered over 12–14 years post ﬁre, although the vegetation was
still very open at this age. However, other studies from woodlands in Australia (Taylor
et al. 2012; Watson et al. 2012) have found little support for an increase in open habitat
species in recently burnt areas, ﬁnding that total species richness and diversity and
prevalence of threatened species is often associated with areas of long unburnt vegetation.
Lindenmayer et al. (2014) reported that of 75 species recorded, only one, the ﬂame robin
Petroica phoenicea, favoured landscapes with more recently burnt vegetation. By contrast,
in our study the closely related (though ecologically different) red-capped robin was only
recorded in old and very old sites.

Our prediction that total bird species richness and diversity would be greater in older
ﬁre ages was supported by our data and is consistent with many other studies. As a guild,
insectivores showed the strongest increase with time since ﬁre, followed by granivores,
with the former trend supporting our third hypothesis. Brooker (1998) found that ﬁre in
Mediterranean-climate heathlands in Western Australia reduced the densities of birds for
up to eight years post ﬁre. This was particularly the case for insectivores such as the
splendid fairy-wren Malurus splendens, scarlet robin Petroica boodang, yellow-rumped
thornbill Acanthiza chrysorrhoa and western thornbill Acanthiza inornata (Brooker 1998).
Albanesi et al. (2014) recorded reductions in abundance of most species under high
severity and frequency ﬁre regimes in central Argentina, and Pons and Clavero (2010) also
found that areas with the longest post-ﬁre interval supported the highest abundance of
Pyrenees shrubland birds. Taken together, these results indicate that the recovery of
vegetation and ongoing structural complexity that develops post ﬁre supports a greater
abundance and diversity of birds.
The key drivers for such strong responses to ﬁre age are likely to be habitat selection for
food, shelter (including from predators) and nesting sites. Previous studies have found that
bird responses to ﬁre are diverse, with different species often responding to different
vegetation elements dependent upon their ecology and life history (Pons and Clavero 2010;
Santos et al. 2014). Fire reduces the availability of insect prey to birds, and tree or shrub-
foraging insectivores are known to be particularly impacted by ﬁre (Lopez de Casenave
et al. 1998; Milesi et al. 2002; Albanesi et al. 2014). Fire intensity and severity can also
have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on bird responses. Latif et al. (2016) found that aerial insec-
tivores and cavity nesters occupied more severely burned sites after ﬁre due to an increase
in resources for these groups. Conversely, they found a decrease in canopy-nesting
insectivores and pine seed consumers with increasing ﬁre intensity. In our study, it is likely
that insect prey abundance and diversity were higher in longer unburnt vegetation,
although further research is needed to conﬁrm this. The number of granivores and frugi-
vores recorded was generally low in this study (comprising only three species of par-
rot/cockatoo), thus limiting sound conclusions about their absence from younger sites. We
found partial support for our prediction that nectarivores would be positively associated
with the presence of ﬂowering shrubs at a site. Of the four nectarivorous honeyeaters
analysed, one species (white-fronted honeyeater) showed a positive response to ﬂowering
shrubs.
Conservation and management implications
Our study has important outcomes for conservation managers working in ﬁre-prone
landscapes. Old (36–46 YSLF) and very old (60–84 YSLF) sites, on average, contained 4.5
and 5.5 more bird species, respectively, than young (11–14 YSLF). Old and very old sites
also contained a distinct assemblage of bird species and four of the study species showed a
clear preference for these areas. Conversely, young and medium sites were not clearly
associated with any species in particular and they exhibited lower species richness. This
suggests that old and very old shrublands in our semi-arid study region may be critical
refuge or source habitats for bird species and therefore very important in a landscape
context. Vegetation may take 35 years or more to accumulate the structural attributes
characteristic of ‘long unburnt’ habitat, and old and very old vegetation appears to sustain a
distinct bird assemblage. We acknowledge that shrublands in drier regions do not tend to

experience such profound structural and birds responses with ﬁre age because their lower
shrub density and greater heterogeneity disfavours crown ﬁres.
Maintaining areas of long unburnt shrubland is likely to maximise bird species richness,
as well as providing important habitat for other taxa like mammals and reptiles (Doherty
et al. 2015a). This management approach aligns with that recommended for other Aus-
tralian ecosystems (e.g. Barton et al. 2014; Robinson et al. 2014), but often contrasts
directly with that recommended in other parts of the world where both younger and older
ﬁre ages are considered important for bird communities (e.g. Herrando et al. 2002; De
Caceres et al. 2013). This has important implications for the ﬁre management of Australian
ecosystems and suggests that broad generalisations from other locations may be detri-
mental to maintaining avian species richness and abundance. Maintaining areas of older
shrubland that are well connected to surrounding areas of vegetation is important for bird
conservation. As a consequence of our study, we recommend that future research: (1)
capitalises on future ﬁres in the study landscape to survey the youngest ﬁre ages; (2)
focuses on other Australian shrublands; and (3) seeks to determine the speciﬁc charac-
teristics of long unburnt vegetation that make it attractive to different bird species.

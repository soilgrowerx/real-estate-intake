22.1 INTRODUCTION 
It should be noted at the outset that there is a list of air toxics while no such list exists for water 
toxics. Rather, the practitioner has several options for specifying those chemicals that could be 
classifed as “water toxics.” Included in these eligible candidates are the Priority Water Pollutants 
(PWPs), chemicals that appear under the primary drinking water standards of the Safe Drinking 
Water Act (SDWA), and what has come to be defned by many as “unregulated contaminants.” This 
latter term is fully within the domain of Environmental Protection Agency’s (EPA) Contaminant 
Candidate List (CCL), a process that is employed to determine potential toxic chemicals, and a topic 
which is discussed in a later section titled “Unregulated Contaminants.” The importance of the role 
these chemicals play in the water management feld is discussed in the paragraphs to follow. 
The overwhelming population growth in recent decades and water crisis along with limited and 
uneven geographical distribution of freshwater resources is a growing challenge for the economic 
and human development. Wastewater reclamation and use could be an alternative for intact water 
sources and a promising solution to water scarcity and unequal distribution. However, wastewater is 
a double-edged sword since it serves as an accessible water source for food production and human 
usage but concurrently may carry uncharacterized content with unknown toxicological profle 
causing acute or long-term health risks. Pharmaceuticals, cosmeceuticals, nanomaterials and their 
chemical decomposition derivatives found in some wastewater are not well known. Their unknown 
toxicity, teratogenicity, and carcinogenicity profle, associated with lack of monitoring and control 
measures, impose a signifcant risk on the public health. 
The aforementioned world population growth coincides with higher demand for resources. 
Limited and uneven geographical distribution of freshwater resources will be challenging for this 
growing population as both a primary life requirement and for food production. Thus, the increas-
ing demand, especially from municipal regions, will impose a strict burden on the supply side for 
water resources. In such situation, wastewater recycling and reuse could be an alternative for intact 
water, and reuse could be an alternative for intact water sources and a potential promising solution 
to water scarcity and unequal distribution. 
As evident from the path that wastewater passes through homes, industries, and other usage 
points, it may contain many pathogenic microorganisms in addition to chemical and pharmaceutical 
residues. These chemical pollutants may include, but not limited to, salts, metals metalloids, resid-
ual drugs, organic compounds, endocrine disrupting compounds, and active residues of personal 
care products. Infectious outbreaks remain the most critical concern in low-income countries, while 
the chemical and pharmaceutical solution is the major health risk in developing and high-income 
countries. In particular, pharmaceuticals are synthetic or natural chemicals that can be found in 
prescription medicines, over-the-counter therapeutic drugs, and veterinary drugs. Pharmaceuticals 
contain active ingredients that have been designed to have pharmacological effects and confer sig-

22.2 TOXIC-RELATED TERMS 
Theodore et al. [1] provides the following defnition for toxic and toxic-related terms 
1. Toxic. A term used to describe a poisonous substance that has a harmful effect on an 
organism by ingestion, inhalation, or skin adsorption. 
2. Toxic air pollutant. A material contaminating the environment that causes death, disease, 
or birth defects in organisms that ingest or absorb them; the quantities and length of 
exposure necessary to cause these effects can vary widely. 
3. Toxic cloud. An airborne mass of gases, vapors, fumes, or aerosols containing toxic 
materials [2]. 
4. Toxic pollutant. A material contaminating the environment that causes death, disease, or 
birth defects in organisms that ingest or absorb it. 
5. Toxicant. A poisonous agent that kills or injures animal or plant life. 
6. Toxicity. The ability of a substance to cause damage to living tissue, impairment of the 
central nervous system (CNS), severe illness or death when ingested, inhaled, or absorbed 
by the skin. 
7. Toxicity assessment. A statistical evaluation of the toxicity of a chemical based on the 
available data from human and animal experimentation. 
8. Toxicology. The branch of medical science devoted to the study of poison, including their 
mode of action, effects, detection, or counter measures. 
9. Toxicosis. A diseased state brought about by the action of a poison. 
There are a number of ways in which chemicals can cause health effects. Some of the more common 
ways are listed in the following [3]: 
1. An irritant is an agent that causes health problems at the point of contact. 
2. An asphyxiant is an agent that inhibits or restricts the oxygen supply to tissues. 
3. CNS depressants act to interfere with brain or nervous system functions and generally sup-
press brain and/or motor activity. 
4. Many agents are known to show or are suspected of showing genetic activity; this is gener-
ally the result of prolonged chronic exposure but may also occur through a one-time acute 
exposure and include: 
• Carcinogenicity refers to a property of some chemicals that increases the probability 
that cancer will develop at a later time as a result of an exposure. 
• Mutagenicity refers to a property of some chemicals which measures the probability 
that mutations will develop at a later time as a result of an exposure. 
• Teratogenicity refers to birth defects.

22.4 DRINKING WATER STANDARDS AND REGULATIONS 
Drinking water standards are regulations that the EPA has established to control the concentration 
of contaminants in the U.S. drinking water supply for all PWS regardless of the size of the popula-
tion they serve. In most cases, the EPA delegates responsibility for implementing drinking water 
standards to states and tribes. Details are provided in Chapter 16. 
National Primary Drinking Water Regulations (NPDFWRs or primary standards) are the legally 
enforceable MCLs (maximum containment level) and TTs (tertiary treatment) that apply to PWS. 
The EPA sets primary drinking water standards through a three-step process. First, the EPA identi-
fes contaminants that may adversely affect public health and occur in drinking water with a fre-
quency and at levels that pose a threat to public health. The EPA identifes these contaminants for

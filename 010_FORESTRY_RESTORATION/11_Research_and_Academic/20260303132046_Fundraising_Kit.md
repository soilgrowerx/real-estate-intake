Fundraising Glossary
Pitch Deck: A presentation that provides an overview of the startup, including its 
market opportunity, product or service, business model, and growth projections.
Pre-Seed Funding: The earliest stage of funding, typically used for product 
development and market validation before seeking substantial investor funding.
Seed Funding: Funding received to help startups launch their product or service and 
gather initial traction.
Series A, B, and C Funding: Different rounds of funding that startups go through as 
they grow and scale, typically involving larger investments from venture capital firms.
Angel Investor: An individual who invests personal funds into a startup, usually in the 
early stages, in exchange for equity.
Venture Capital (VC): Institutional investors who provide funding to startups with high 
growth potential in exchange for equity.
Runway: The amount of time in which a startup's cash reserves will last, based on its 
burn rate (monthly expenses).
Valuation: The estimated worth of a startup, often determined by investors' 
perception of its potential.
Cap Table: A capitalization table that outlines the ownership stakes of founders, 
investors, and other shareholders in a startup.
Burn Rate: The rate at which a startup spends its cash reserves to cover expenses 
before becoming profitable.
Due Diligence: The thorough research and evaluation process that potential investors 
conduct to assess a startup's viability, including its financials, team, and market 
opportunity.
Term Sheet: A non-binding agreement outlining the basic terms and conditions of a 
potential investment, including valuation, investment amount, and investor rights.
Exit Strategy: A plan detailing how a startup will provide a return on investment to its 
investors, such as through an acquisition or initial public offering (IPO).
Syndicate: A group of angel investors or venture capitalists who pool their resources 
and expertise to invest in a startup collectively.
Run Rate: A projection of a startup's future financial performance based on its 
current revenue or expenses.
Pro Forma Financials: Financial projections that estimate a startup's future revenue, 
expenses, and profitability.
Convertible Note: A type of short-term debt that can be converted into equity at a 
later stage, typically during a future funding round.

Fundraising is a critical aspect of building and growing your startup. To navigate the 
fundraising process successfully, it is essential to understand different funding stages,

Researching Different Funding Stages and Strategies
Funding stages typically include seed funding, angel investment, venture capital, and 
later-stage funding rounds. Each stage comes with its own unique characteristics, 
expectations, and funding sources. It is important to research and understand the 
specific requirements and expectations associated with each stage. 
Develop a funding strategy that aligns with your business goals and funding needs. 
Consider which funding sources are most suitable for your startup, such as angel 
investors, venture capitalists, or crowdfunding platforms. Tailor your pitch and approach 
based on the target investors for each stage.
Seed funding is typically the initial round of funding for startups and is often 
sourced from the founders' personal savings or family and friends. It is used 
to cover early-stage expenses such as market research, product development, 
and initial go-to-market activities. Seed funding is crucial for validating the 
business concept and attracting more substantial investments in the future.
Angel investors are high-net-worth individuals who provide early-stage 
funding and valuable industry expertise. They often invest in startups in 
exchange for equity or convertible notes. Angel investors can offer mentorship, 
networking opportunities, and industry connections in addition to financial 
support.

Venture capital (VC) funding is typically sought after by startups that are 
ready to scale and have demonstrated significant growth potential. VC firms 
provide funding in exchange for equity and actively participate in strategic 
decision-making. Venture capitalists often invest larger amounts of capital 
compared to angel investors and can facilitate further funding rounds as the 
startup evolves.
Later-stage funding rounds, such as Series A, B, and C, involve larger 
investments and are aimed at scaling the business further. These rounds 
typically involve institutional investors, private equity firms, or strategic 
corporate partnerships. The focus shifts from validating the business model to 
scaling operations, expanding the team, and entering new markets.

business.

negotiation points. Consider how the terms impact your control, equity dilution, and 
future fundraising rounds.

Managing the Due Diligence Process
Due diligence is a process where potential investors carefully investigate and evaluate 
your startup's financial and legal aspects to assess its investment potential. It involves 
a thorough examination of various factors to ensure that the investment is sound and 
aligns with the investor's objectives and risk appetite.
During due diligence, investors review financial statements, including income 
statements, balance sheets, and cash flow statements, to gain insights into your 
startup's financial health. They assess revenue, expenses, profitability, and financial 
trends to understand your business's financial performance and projections.
Additionally, investors examine legal contracts, such as customer agreements, vendor 
contracts, and employee contracts, to identify any potential liabilities or legal risks. 
They also assess the ownership and protection of intellectual property assets, such as 
patents, trademarks, or copyrights, to ensure their validity and value.
To effectively manage the due diligence process, it's important to be well-prepared. This 
involves organizing and updating your financial records, legal documents, and other

## Purpose of this report
Arboracle’s job is to let a working arborist move from chaotic clipboards and generic CRM tools into an ecological-first, field-native system that handles estimates, jobs, invoices, and ecological intelligence without getting in their way. To do that credibly, the platform needs clean flows, zero dead-ends, and language that reflects the way crews talk and think rather than generic SaaS jargon.[2][3][4][5]
Below, each section is written as:
- What the arborist expects / pain point.
- Likely bugs / UX risks in the current implementation.
- Concrete fixes and copy guidelines that tell a story of care.
## Global platform issues
Field arborists typically juggle weather, traffic, client no‑shows, and safety risks; any glitch that forces extra taps or re-entry of information erodes trust quickly.[6][7]
Primary global risks and fixes:
- Session and state loss.
- Risk: Navigating from Dashboard → New Estimate → New Job → Invoices may lose form data if the user backs out or the network blips, which is catastrophic in a truck or bucket.[3]
- Fix: Implement auto-save drafts for all major forms (Estimate, Job, Client, Invoice) with clear “Last saved X seconds ago” indicators and offline-safe local storage, syncing when reconnected.[8]
- Mobile ergonomics and tap targets.
- Risk: Navigation items and call-to-action buttons may be too small or too close, especially when wearing gloves or with sun glare.[5]
- Fix: Enforce a minimum 44px touch target, prioritize a bottom nav for core flows (Dashboard, Jobs, Map, Bodhi, Invoices), and ensure high contrast for outdoor viewing.[8]
- Feedback and loading states.
- Risk: Silent failures (no spinner, no toast) lead crews to double-submit estimates or think data is lost.[9]
- Fix: Standardize spinners, progress bars, and success/error toasts with human-readable messages (e.g., “Job saved. Bodhi is now analyzing site risk.”) instead of ambiguous alerts.[8]
- Consistency in domain language.
- Risk: Mixing CRM terms (“leads,” “opportunities”) with arborist-specific terms (“site,” “scope of work,” “PHC”) causes cognitive friction.[7][2]
- Fix: Create a domain glossary and ensure all labels, tooltips, and help text use arborist-first language consistently across dashboard, forms, and analytics.[6]
## Dashboard-specific issues
The Dashboard currently surfaces key business metrics (clients, active jobs, overdue invoices) and ecological impact, but it needs clearer hierarchy and error-proof interactions for a field arborist checking it between stops.[10][1]
Likely issues and fixes:
- Overloaded hero area.
- Risk: “Welcome back,” org name, and multiple primary CTAs (“New Estimate,” “New Job”) may compete visually.[10]
- Fix: Make one primary CTA adaptive to role and context: for sales arborists, emphasize “New Estimate”; for crew leads, emphasize “Today’s Jobs”; expose secondary actions via a clear secondary button set.[6]
- Recent jobs table usability.
- Risk: Long job IDs and similar client names may make it hard to pick the right job on a phone.[10]
- Fix: Allow row expansion and a compact “Job card” layout on mobile showing: Client, address, status, scheduled date, value, and a one-tap “Open job” button.[5]
- Business alerts clarity.
- Risk: “Overdue invoices 2” and “Client follow-ups needed 1” may not indicate urgency or next action clearly.[10]
- Fix: Add urgency badges (“High”, “Medium”) and one-click workflows: tap “Overdue invoices” opens a ready-filtered invoice list and a bulk “Send reminder” action; tap “Client follow-ups” opens an auto-drafted call/email script.[4][8]
- Ecological impact panel trust.
- Risk: “Overall Health Score 8.7” might feel opaque or gamified if the arborist cannot see which metrics drive it.[11]
- Fix: Add a “View breakdown” micro-link that explains in one tap how the score is calculated (e.g., species diversity, canopy condition, soil interventions, PHC follow-through), reinforcing ecological seriousness.[11]
## Bodhi AI panel
Bodhi is positioned as a “personal ecological consultant,” which is exactly the narrative advantage over generic job-management tools. That means the panel must avoid feeling like a generic chatbot and instead feel like a tuned assistant that understands arborist workflows.[12]
Likely issues and fixes:
- Generic prompt list.
- Risk: Example prompts that feel like generic CRM queries (“over $2000”) may miss ecological nuance.[10]
- Fix: Add prompts tied to tree care and resiliency, such as:
- “Show me jobs where we recommended PHC but the client didn’t proceed.”
- “List sites with high-risk trees and overdue re-inspections.”
- “Which neighborhoods show declining species diversity in my portfolio?”[3][11]
- Context loss between screens.
- Risk: Clicking “Chat with Bodhi” from the Dashboard may not carry context about current client, job, or property, forcing repetition.[13]
- Fix: Pass current context (selected client, job, property, or filters) as system metadata into Bodhi so the assistant can answer “this job” or “this site” questions without additional user steps.[13]
- Trust and explainability.
- Risk: If Bodhi pulls numbers with no visible source (e.g., profit per client), crews may distrust recommendations.[13]
- Fix: Include inline “How this was calculated” toggles and, when Bodhi answers, show “Data sources: Jobs J‑2512-001, J‑2511-003, Invoices I‑…” with clickable links, reinforcing that insights are grounded in their own records.[8][13]
## CRM & sales / client lifecycle
Arborists often complain that existing tools make sales-heavy CRM the center of gravity instead of trees, sites, and safety. Arboracle needs to respect that the “client” object is deeply tied to properties, trees, and ecological commitments.[14][7]
Likely issues and fixes:
- Incomplete client profiles for field use.
- Risk: A client record that emphasizes email and marketing tags over property details, site maps, and tree history is misaligned with crew needs.[6]
- Fix: Ensure client views in the field default to “Today’s work at this property,” tree risk flags, access notes, gate codes, and past recommendations; move marketing fields deeper, away from the crew default.[5]
- Duplicate or fragmented contacts.
- Risk: Multiple client entries for “Tree Mann Solutions” across estimates, jobs, and invoices create confusion.[10]
- Fix: Add robust duplicate detection and a guided merge flow with clear rules (e.g., preserve latest contact info, merge properties and past work, keep invoice history intact).[8]
- Lead → estimate → job conversion path.
- Risk: If converting a lead to an estimate or job requires re-entering property details or tree data, crews will abandon the flow.[3]
- Fix: Provide one-click conversions that carry forward all relevant data and log the conversion path automatically (“Lead created 10/10, Estimate approved 10/12, Job scheduled 10/16”).[6]
## Job and operations flows
Crews in the field need reliable, simple flows for finding today’s work, understanding site risk, and recording completed work accurately.[5][6]
Likely issues and fixes:
- “New Job” form overload.
- Risk: Too many required fields or non-field-critical details may slow down entry during a site visit.[5]
- Fix: Split job creation into:
- Quick-create: client, property, basic scope, date, and crew.
- Advanced details: itemized tasks, equipment, PHC protocols, and ecological goals, which can be filled in later or by office staff.[8]
- Scheduling conflicts and clarity.
- Risk: No friendly conflict detection could allow overlapping jobs for the same crew or under-estimated durations.[6]
- Fix: Add conflict warnings and capacity views (per crew per day), and let Bodhi suggest realistic windows based on historical durations and travel times.[3][13]
- Work documentation and photos.
- Risk: If photo capture and notes are awkward, crews will fall back to camera roll and text messages, fragmenting data.[5]
- Fix: Provide in-job “Quick note,” “Before/After photos,” and “Voice note” buttons that are usable one-handed; auto-tag media with job ID, tree ID, and GPS coordinates.[3]
## Finance / invoices
Arborist-specific pain points include missed invoices, confusing line items, and lack of linkage between work performed and billing.[4][6]
Likely issues and fixes:
- Overdue invoices triage.
- Risk: The “Overdue Invoices $1,200 total overdue 2” alert may not help decide who to contact first.[10]
- Fix: On click, show a sortable list with days overdue, client type, and risk flags, and surface suggested actions such as “Send reminder email,” “Schedule call,” or “Add late fee per policy.”[4]
- Estimate-to-invoice integrity.
- Risk: Manual copying of line items from estimates to invoices is error-prone.[3]
- Fix: Implement “Generate invoice from estimate” with a clear diff view when items are added or removed, and maintain a linked history so crews can see what was originally promised.[8]
- Ecological value in invoices.
- Risk: Invoices that look like generic tree service bills miss the chance to reinforce ecological outcomes and long-term value.[11]
- Fix: Optionally include a short ecological summary (e.g., canopy preserved, soil interventions, risk reduced) drawn from the job’s ecological data, tailored to the client type.[11]
## Ecological impact / STIM alignment
The platform’s differentiation is the Soil Tree Intelligence Matrix and ecological focus, not just business tooling.[12][11]
Likely issues and fixes:
- Hidden or under-explained ecological metrics.
- Risk: “Total properties assessed,” “Total acreage managed,” and a single “Overall Health Score” may feel like vanity stats if the arborist cannot use them to drive decisions.[10]
- Fix: Enable drill-downs by:
- Property: show trends in health score, interventions, and risk over time.
- Client: show ecological commitments and follow-through.
- Region: highlight regenerative opportunities and gaps.[11]
- Lack of connection to work scopes.
- Risk: Ecological metrics not directly tied to job templates or scopes of work can feel bolted-on.[3]
- Fix: Ensure each scope type (pruning, PHC, removals, planting) maps to ecological outcomes (e.g., diversity, risk reduction, soil health), and render a simple “ecological ledger” for each property.[11]
## Training and onboarding
Field arborists often adopt software under time pressure; simple onboarding is crucial.[6][5]
Likely issues and fixes:
- Unclear “Your Journey” / path gamification.
- Risk: The Seedling Steward → Legacy Protector path may feel like generic gamification if not tied to real practice milestones.[10]
- Fix: Tie each step explicitly to meaningful achievements (e.g., “Forest Architect: completed X ecological inventories and implemented Y PHC plans”) and show practical benefits (templates, Bodhi skills unlocked).[11]
- In-app training gaps.
- Risk: A passive “Training” link may not be enough to change behavior.[10]
- Fix: Add inline guidance, short contextual checklists (“First day in Arboracle,” “First week with crews”), and embed micro-lesson links directly on pages where users get stuck.[8]
## Narrative and reassurance for the field arborist
Everything above should be reflected in microcopy and UI so a field arborist feels the platform was built around their pain points: lost paperwork, redundant data entry, fragmented ecological records, and generic CRM tools that do not understand trees.[14][7]
Key narrative elements for your team to weave into the UI text:
- Emphasize “built for arborists, not generic contractors,” especially around jobs, ecological dashboards, and Bodhi.[7]
- Reinforce that data captured once (in the field) flows everywhere: estimates, jobs, invoices, and ecological metrics.[4][3]
- Show that risk, safety, and long-term forest health are first-class citizens alongside revenue and invoices.[6][11]

# Lecture 9 - Water/Sugar Movement

## Executive Summary

Understanding the mechanism of water transport in trees is crucial for ecological understanding and land management. The xylem, composed of tracheids and vessels, facilitates water movement through adhesion and cohesion. Capillary action allows for quick water transport over short distances but is limited by gravity and the diameter of tracheids and vessels. Bulk flow, driven by transpiration-induced negative water potential, transports water across long distances without ATP. The phloem, with sieve cells and companion cells, enables sugar transport through active loading at sources and passive unloading at sinks. This knowledge has implications for optimizing plant growth and ecosystem health.

## Key Concepts & Taxonomies

* Xylem - dead at maturity; maximizes volume/reduces resistance
* Tracheids - long and thin xylem cells
* Vessels - short and fat xylem cells
* Adhesion - water adheres to polar surfaces
* Cohesion - water coheres to itself
* Capillary action - uses adhesion and cohesion for quick transport over short distances
* Bulk flow - driven by negative water potential; transports water across long distances without ATP
* Phloem - alive at maturity; expands with tree growth
* Sieve cells - minimal resistance, no nucleus or vacuole, few organelles
* Companion cells - support sieve cell function

## Actionable Takeaways & Core Claims

1. Water transport in plants is facilitated by xylem and driven by capillary action and bulk flow.
2. The phloem enables sugar transport through active loading and passive unloading.
3. Knowledge of water and sugar movement in plants has implications for optimizing plant growth and ecosystem health.

## Raw Reading Notes

### Plant Water/Nutrient Movement
- Xylem: dead at maturity, maximizes volume/reduces resistance
- Tracheids: long and thin xylem cells
- Vessels: short and fat xylem cells, more in taller trees
- Adhesion: water adheres to polar surfaces (e.g., glass)
- Cohesion: water coheres to itself
- Capillary action: uses adhesion and cohesion for quick transport over short distances, max height ~33 ft.
- Bulk flow: driven by negative water potential; transports water across long distances without ATP

### Water Movement in a Leaf
- Negative water potential caused by transpiration; more negative as you ascend the plant
- Water potential: sum of pressure and osmotic components

### Plant Sugar/Hormone Transport
- Phloem: alive at maturity, expands with tree growth, resistance is a problem
- Sieve cells: minimal resistance, no nucleus or vacuole, few organelles
- Companion cells: support sieve cell function
- Phloem loading (source): active, requires ATP; builds up positive pressure through osmosis
- Vertical transport: passive, no ATP required
- Phloem unloading (sink): active

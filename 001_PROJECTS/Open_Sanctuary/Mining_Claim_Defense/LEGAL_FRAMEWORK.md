# LEGAL FRAMEWORK: Non-Extractive Land Acquisition via Mineral Claims

## Executive Summary
This brief outlines the legal strategy for Open Sanctuary to hold federal unpatented mining claims for the primary purpose of biological protection and landscape restoration. It examines the viability of utilizing the 1872 General Mining Act in tandem with modern environmental standards (such as the BLM's 2024 Landscape Health Rule) to achieve non-extractive stewardship of public lands.

## 1. The BLM 2024 "Landscape Health" Rule and Conservation Leases
In mid-2024, the Bureau of Land Management (BLM) finalized the Conservation and Landscape Health Rule, which formally placed conservation on equal footing with extractive uses (mining, grazing, timber) under the Federal Land Policy and Management Act (FLPMA). 
*   **Conservation Leases (Restoration Leases):** The rule established a mechanism for entities to hold "Conservation Leases" for the purpose of restoring degraded landscapes or providing mitigation for development elsewhere. 
*   **Current Status:** The rule has faced significant political and legal headwinds (including recent legislative pushes to rescind it in late 2024/early 2025). However, its introduction establishes an administrative precedent that BLM has the authority to sanction restoration as a valid, primary use of federal land.

## 2. General Mining Act of 1872 vs. Modern Restoration Laws
To maintain an unpatented mining claim under the 1872 Act, a claimant must demonstrate a "discovery" of a valuable mineral and perform $100 of annual "assessment work" (labor or improvements) that tends to develop the claim and facilitate extraction.

*   **Can biological restoration qualify as assessment work?** Historically, courts require assessment work to directly aid in the extraction of minerals (e.g., road building, digging shafts, core drilling). Attempting to pass off biological restoration or soil stabilization as "assessment work" is legally precarious under traditional interpretations.
*   **The Mitigation/Reclamation Argument:** Under modern laws (FLPMA, NEPA), operators must reclaim land and prevent "unnecessary or undue degradation." If stabilization is a legally required component of a mining plan of operations, it *could* be argued as necessary development work. However, this typically requires an actual intent to mine.
*   **The Maintenance Fee Loophole:** In 1992, Congress established an annual maintenance fee (currently $165 per claim) that can be paid *in lieu* of performing the $100 of physical assessment work. **This is the critical pivot:** By paying the fee annually, a claim holder avoids the need to physically alter the land to maintain the claim, thus holding the mineral rights defensively without digging.

## 3. Strategy for Open Sanctuary: Defensive Claim Holding
Open Sanctuary can acquire and hold federal mining claims to prevent destructive extractive uses by competing interests, effectively creating a conservation buffer.

**Strategic Roadmap:**
1.  **Locate and Claim:** Stake unpatented mining claims on highly biodiverse or sensitive BLM/Forest Service land open to mineral entry. Ensure there is at least a theoretical "discovery" of a valuable mineral to make the claim valid.
2.  **Pay Annual Fees in Lieu of Work:** Pay the $165 annual maintenance fee to the BLM before September 1st each year. This completely bypasses the requirement to prove that physical "assessment work" (which requires destructive digging) was performed.
3.  **Utilize Surface Rights for Stewardship:** Under the Surface Resources Act of 1955, the federal government retains surface management rights on unpatented claims, but the claimant has the right to access and occupy the surface as necessary for "prospecting, mining or processing operations." Open Sanctuary can minimize surface disturbance.
4.  **Layer with Conservation Leases (if available):** If the BLM 2024 rule (or its state-level equivalents) survives, apply for a Conservation Lease covering the same footprint as the mining claim. This dual-layer approach provides both subsurface defensive control (blocking other miners) and surface stewardship rights (authorized restoration).
5.  **Deterrence:** By holding the mineral rights, Open Sanctuary forces any other entity wanting to mine that area to buy them out or prove the claim invalid, erecting a significant legal and financial barrier to destruction.

## Conclusion
While the 1872 Mining Act is inherently extractive, its administrative mechanics (specifically the payment of annual maintenance fees in lieu of physical labor) provide a backdoor for conservation entities to hold land defensively. Paired with emerging regulatory frameworks like the BLM Landscape Health rule, Open Sanctuary can legally occupy the space of a "miner" while acting purely as a restorative steward.
